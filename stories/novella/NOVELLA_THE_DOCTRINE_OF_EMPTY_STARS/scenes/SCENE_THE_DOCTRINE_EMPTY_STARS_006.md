The outer silence cells were not built for prisoners.

That made their new use worse, not better. A prison admitted its function. It
kept bodies by force and taught its doors the grammar of refusal. The outer
silence cells had been shaped for people who requested an hour without pleading
voices before release. Their walls softened sound. Their water slots opened from
both sides. Their thresholds remembered privacy, not judgment.

For one dimming, three Canticle survivors had breathed behind them under Synod
seal.

Sael stood in the lower corridor with the deep chapel record in her hand and
felt the station try to keep the difference small.

Held under irregularity.

Cause: edited surface.

The slate had cooled after taking the marks, but it had not become light. It lay
flat against her palm with the modest weight of an honest thing that did not
pretend to be finished. Veyr had read it above the lower stair when Sael
returned from the deep chapel. He had not praised her. He had not corrected her.
He had kept the record through one dimming, then returned it to her after the
wall light made its small adjustment across his face.

"A held burden gathers claims," he had said.

"Yes, Elder."

"Record only what can be borne without making the dead useful to fear."

No order to release. No order to conceal. No absolution.

Sael had bowed because obedience still had a shape inside her, and because
nothing in Veyr's instruction was false. That had become the difficulty with
him. He rarely gave her a sentence she could reject cleanly.

Tovan waited beside the first outer silence door. He had washed the pressure mark
on his palm and bound it in gray cloth. The bandage had already begun to lose
the shape of his hand. In the Narthex, even injury was discouraged from
becoming emblem.

He looked from the slate to Sael's face. "Lower record?"

"Held."

His shoulders shifted once. Relief or worry. With Tovan, the two often shared
the same body.

"Veyr knows?"

"Yes."

"And still sends you here."

"The claim must be recorded."

Tovan did not ask whose claim. The Canticle captain's presence had made a claim
the moment he entered through the maintenance seam and recognized the thrum in
the wall. The edited surface had made another. The station itself had a claim,
too: not to be loaded with indefinite witness until its lower rooms forgot how
to return.

Truths did not become lighter because they disliked sharing a line.

Sael pressed the slate against her ribs. "Is he coherent?"

"Enough to resent water."

"That is not an anchor state."

"No." Tovan glanced at the door. "But it is a state."

The cell's pressure line held steady. On the other side, someone breathed in the
controlled rhythm of a pilot keeping nausea from becoming panic. The outer
silence walls took the sound and shortened it before it could travel far. Their
kindness made eavesdropping feel like theft.

Sael set two fingers to the door mark.

"Statement," she said.

The door considered the word. It had been taught silence, not interrogation.
Then the seal recognized the lower slate and opened by the width of her hand.

Ives Mar sat on the floor with his back to the white wall, knees drawn up, hands
open where Sael could see them. His helmet lay beside him. Someone had cleaned
the blood from beneath his nose, but not completely. A dry line remained in the
crease above his mouth, dark as an unfinished mark. His Canticle jacket had been
removed. Without armor he looked less like an invader and more like a man who
had spent too much of himself on a door that was not there anymore.

The first officer slept against the far wall, or had been placed in the shape of
sleep. Their hands twitched at intervals against the floor, counting controls.
The smaller survivor lay on the low cot with a damp cloth across their brow.
Their mouth moved soundlessly. Sael did not try to read the word.

Ives watched her watch them.

"No names?" he asked.

"Not unless offered cleanly."

"Your elder's kindness has rules."

"All kindness that survives use has rules."

He looked away first. That gave her no pleasure.

Sael knelt outside the open line of the door, not crossing the threshold. It was
not fear. It was procedure, and therefore also fear given useful bones. The cell
was permitted to hold bodies under care. It was not permitted to become a room
where opposing doctrines took shape around a wounded man.

She set the slate across her knees.

"State your claim," she said.

Ives laughed once through his nose. It cost him. The sound became a cough he
held behind his teeth.

"That is what you call it?"

"Yes."

"Efficient."

"Necessary."

He rubbed his thumb against the base of each finger on his right hand. Anchor
sequence, abbreviated. Thumb to first finger. Thumb to second. The third touch
hesitated.

Sael waited.

"Ives Mar," he said, as if his own name were an object he had just found in a
coat pocket. "Canticle recovery. Assignment closed. Claim active."

"A closed assignment cannot claim active standing."

"That is true on forms."

"Forms are where claims survive the room."

"Forms are also where they go to die quietly."

Sael did not write that. She let the silence cell keep it instead.

Ives leaned his head back against the wall. The posture should have looked like
rest. It looked like rationing.

"What did you find in the chapel?" he asked.

"State your claim."

"You found something."

"State it."

His mouth tightened. Exhaustion made anger slow across his face, more sediment
than flame. Good. Flame would have been easier to dismiss.

"One station," he said.

Sael opened the first blank line.

"Name."

"Tern Gate."

The slate took it. Not as proof. As pressure.

"One station," Ives said again. "Outer ring already thinning. Families moved to
evacuation tiers before the second corridor window. Canticle command had two
routes and not enough memory weight for both."

"That is more than one station."

"That is the arithmetic that made it one station."

Sael looked at him.

He did not look triumphant. He did not look ashamed enough to be cleansed by it.
He looked like a man reading numbers from the inside of a sealed room.

"Go on," she said.

"One late corridor." He closed his eyes briefly. "The first corridor went to a
denser system. More registered lives. More civic memory to stabilize. Better
chance of clean return. Tern Gate got the second opening."

"Canticle triage."

"Yes."

The admission came without defense. That made it heavier.

"How late?" Sael asked.

"Late enough that the station had begun losing edge function. Late enough that
boarding lines were being held by voice instead of gates. Late enough that the
corridor mouth was already thin when the last ship tried to enter."

She wrote: evacuation corridor opened after station coherence loss.

The slate accepted the line without comment.

"One missing ship," Ives said.

The smaller survivor on the cot made a sound and then swallowed it. Ives turned
toward them at once, hand lifting but not touching. The motion was precise, worn
by repetition.

"Anchor," he said softly.

The survivor's lips moved.

"Anchor," he repeated.

No answer.

He lowered his hand. His face did not change. Sael saw the cost of that too.

"The ship," she said.

"Auxiliary Vesper. Civilian load, two Fleet pilots, one recovery archivist, six
crew. Sent for the last tier after the main corridor had already taken strain.
It entered transmission range. Then it did not enter the return manifest."

"Destroyed?"

"Missing."

"Those are often the same mercy."

His eyes came back to her. "No."

The word had no volume. It struck anyway.

Sael held his gaze because looking down would have let him keep the force of it
unchallenged. "No?"

"Destroyed leaves wreckage. Wreckage remembers impact, heat, hull break, prayer,
if there was time for prayer. Missing is an account with a hole in it. Holes
keep charging interest."

Canticle language, she thought. Ledger language wearing a different uniform.

"And the recovery order?" she asked.

Ives's fingers went again to their sequence. Thumb. First. Second. Third. The
fourth finger did not answer at once.

"Quietly closed," he said.

"By whom?"

"Command."

"That is not a person."

"No."

"Then it is not a complete answer."

He gave her a tired look. "You asked for arithmetic."

"Arithmetic has authors."

"So does doctrine."

The silence cell took the sentence and blunted it before it could become an
argument. Sael was grateful. She disliked being grateful.

Ives looked at the sleeping officer, then at the cot. "We recovered three
fragments before closure. Warning chime clipped before the rise. Passenger count
by number. Hand on bulkhead near the recorder. No image. No full voice. Enough
pattern to search. Not enough proof to move command against its own triage."

Sael's hand stopped over the slate.

Not because he had said anything new. He had given her those fragments in the
service passage. But then he had been a breach, a weapon risk, a voice entering
the rite. Now he was only a wounded claimant in a room built to keep last wishes
from being crowded. The facts had less noise around them.

That made them worse.

"Why close it?" she asked.

"Officially?" His mouth moved around something that was not quite a smile.
"Search density exceeded survivable return threshold. Continued recovery risked
additional anchor loss without probable retrieval."

It sounded like a clean sentence. It sounded like water over sealed stone.

"And unofficially?"

"Unofficially, Tern Gate was already counted. Vesper was a bad remainder. A
missing ship makes families dangerous for years. A destroyed ship lets them
build a wall."

Sael wrote nothing.

Ives watched her not write.

"Do you hear me making Canticle holy?" he asked.

"No."

"Good. Don't."

The instruction unsettled her more than a plea would have.

He rested his head against the wall again. "My command triaged Tern Gate late.
My command closed the recovery when proof got expensive. My Fleet would use that
memory if we got it out. Hearings. Charges, route revisions, public grief, all
the machinery. Veyr is not wrong about use."

He opened his eyes. "If we return with it, the first record will not begin as
mourning unless someone forces the line. It will begin as chain of custody.
Recovered material. Possible route evidence. Command will call that discipline."

The honesty did not cleanse him. It only showed Sael where the dirt would enter
the form.

Sael's thumb tightened on the slate edge. Veyr's name in Ives's mouth should
have felt like trespass. It felt instead like a tool set on a table between
them.

"Then why come?" she asked.

"Because use is not the only harm."

The smaller survivor whispered something. This time the word had shape.

"Vesper."

Ives closed his eyes.

Sael did not write it. It had not been offered to her. It had escaped a wound.

After a moment Ives opened his eyes again. "Someone had that memory. Someone
edited it. Someone brought it here under a voluntary phrase. Maybe Synod hands.
Maybe not at first. Maybe my people made the first clean absence and yours are
only finishing it with better manners. I don't know."

That last sentence mattered. Sael felt it enter the cell and find a place to
stand.

I don't know.

It was the first thing he had said that did not try to pull her toward his
shape of the world.

"You want the reliquary," she said.

"Yes."

"You want it preserved as evidence."

"Yes."

"You want me to violate custody so you can resume a recovery your command
closed."

"No."

She looked at him sharply.

He let the look land. "I want you to audit it honestly. If honest audit says the
memory was offered freely after something ugly, I will still hate it. That will
not make me right. If honest audit says it was coerced, then release completes
the coercion. That will not make Canticle clean."

Sael's pulse had begun to beat in the hand holding the slate. She placed that
hand flat on her knee until it steadied.

"You do not know what you ask."

"I know some of it."

"No."

He accepted the correction without flinching. Perhaps he was too tired to defend
his pride. Perhaps he had spent it elsewhere.

Sael looked at the white wall behind him, at the water slot, at the door line
that had opened for statement and would close for silence. The cell was doing
its best with a function it had not been built to carry. So was she.

"Surface resonance has already failed," she said.

Ives went still.

She should not have given him that. Not in those words. The slate seemed to
warm in warning against her knee.

"Failed how?" he asked.

"Do not make my sentence useful before it is complete."

He shut his mouth.

That obedience did not make him trustworthy. It made him disciplined. Sael was
learning the difference mattered more than comfort.

She went on because she had begun and because stopping there would be a more
elegant dishonesty than speaking. "A surface check can show an irregularity. It
cannot tell whether the edit hides refusal, route data, panic, or a petition
damaged before arrival. It cannot tell whether release would be theft or mercy
with an ugly history."

"What can?"

She did not answer at once.

The forbidden thing did not look forbidden when named in her mind. That was part
of its danger. It had no blade shape, no hidden appetite, no theatrical darkness.
It was only a deeper procedure used where surface and custody could no longer
bear the weight of ambiguity.

Full resonance.

Not witness in the ordinary sense. Witness kept the auditor near the edge of a
memory, letting pressure and pattern report class, burden, consent, risk. Full
resonance lowered the wall between the memory's surviving structure and the
auditor's anchor sequence. The auditor did not merely observe. The auditor lent
coherence. The memory used the self as a temporary room.

Afterward, some part of the witness remained.

Not a restored memory. Not a clean copy. Nothing so useful or so merciful. An
imprint. A pressure under an anchor. A borrowed weight that could make one
sentence in the self answer with another person's gravity.

That was why acolytes were forbidden.

That was why elders did not use it unless the alternative had become a cruelty
with paperwork.

Ives watched her face and did not ask again.

"Full resonance," she said.

The first officer against the far wall stirred. The smaller survivor went quiet.
Even the cell seemed to dislike the words. Sound softened around them until
Sael's own breathing came back too close.

Ives said, "Can Veyr do it?"

She heard the care in the question and almost hated him for it. He had not asked
can you. He had not yet placed the cost in her hands.

"Yes," she said.

"Will he?"

Veyr's voice returned to her, gentle and exact: You will not make the dead
useful to your doubt.

The instruction was still right.

That was the problem. A forbidden audit could become appetite. Sael could enter
the memory because doubt wanted proof, because Ives wanted evidence, because
the first crack in faith often mistook itself for honesty when it was only
hunger turned around.

But obedience could become appetite too. The appetite for being clean. The
appetite for letting an elder decide where the weight belonged. The appetite for
keeping her own anchors uncontaminated while a child's hand kept striking the
same metal inside a polished lie.

"I do not know," she said.

Ives nodded once.

The nod carried no victory. If anything, it cost him hope.

"Then ask the memory," he said.

"That is what you want."

"Yes."

"That does not make it honest."

"No."

"You may be using me."

"Yes."

Again the answer came without defense.

Sael looked at him then, really looked. His face held no clean courage for her
to admire. His eyes were red from corridor sickness and inadequate sleep. His
hands still lay open because a closed fist inside a cell would become an
argument. He had broken into her station, frightened her rite, brought weapons
beside penitents, and carried a claim his own command had set down when the
numbers became inconvenient.

He was not a hero.

That did not make his arithmetic false.

"If I perform full resonance," she said, "the memory will touch my anchors."

"I know."

"You know as a pilot knows cost in another body."

He flinched then. Slightly. Enough.

"Yes," he said.

"If it carries fear, I will carry pressure from fear."

"Yes."

"If it carries a name, the name may answer where my own doctrine should."

His gaze dropped to her hands.

"Yes."

"If the surface has been prepared to steer an auditor, it may try to use me as
well."

That possibility had not occurred to him cleanly. She saw it enter. She saw him
do the arithmetic and fail to make the sum smaller.

"Then don't," he said.

The words were quiet.

Sael did not understand them at first.

Ives swallowed. "Don't do it because I ask. Don't do it because I came through a
wall and bled near your chapel and said a ship name with enough damage in my
voice. If that is what this is, don't."

For the first time since the breach, he sounded angry at her.

No. Not at her.

At the machinery that could turn even refusal into leverage.

"What should I do instead?" she asked.

"Audit your own record."

The cell held that.

He leaned forward by a fraction. It made his outline less certain. He corrected
himself with an anchor touch against his sternum.

"You marked something," he said. "You don't have to tell me what. You marked it
because the memory did not match its label. Audit that. Not Canticle. Not Synod.
Not me. Your mark."

Sael looked down at the slate.

Held under irregularity.

Cause: edited surface.

Her own mark. Her own hand. Not Veyr's argument. Not Ives's claim. Not the
Canticle Fleet's hunger for proof or the Synod's hunger for lightness. A line
she had opened because the room was kind and the kindness could have completed
the edit.

She closed the slate.

"I will perform full resonance."

Ives did not move.

It was Tovan, outside the cell, who drew a short breath.

Sael had forgotten he stood close enough to hear if the cell chose not to soften
a word. Or perhaps the station had let him hear because the decision had become
too heavy for one witness.

Ives said, "You are not authorized."

"No."

"Veyr will know."

"Yes."

"Your anchors may not come back clean."

"They are already not clean."

That was not rebellion. She knew it as she said it. Rebellion would have been
larger, warmer, easier to mistake for courage. This was smaller and colder. A
ledger correction. A refusal to let a false category do work because it had been
given a gentle room.

Ives bowed his head.

Not gratitude. She would have refused gratitude.

It looked like a man making space for a cost he could not pay.

"Sael," Tovan said from the corridor.

She stood before she answered. Her knees complained after the long kneel. The
complaint steadied her. Bodies were honest in ways doctrine tried to teach and
institutions often forgot.

At the cell door, Tovan blocked her without touching her.

"You cannot take him with you," he said.

"I know."

"You cannot take his account as proof."

"I know."

"You cannot make Veyr's delay into permission."

That one struck.

Sael held the slate against her ribs. "I know."

Tovan looked at the closed door line, then at her. His bandaged palm flexed once.

"Then what is this?"

She wanted a better answer than the one she had. A doctrinal answer. A line with
the old architecture under it. Instead she had only the weight of the mark she
had made.

"A record trying not to lie," she said.

Tovan's face changed by almost nothing.

"That can become pride."

"Yes."

"It can become harm."

"Yes."

"It can also become obedience by a longer road," he said.

Sael looked at him.

Tovan lowered his bound hand. "I do not know which. That is why I am afraid."

He stepped aside.

The corridor beyond him seemed longer than before. The Narthex had not changed
its architecture. It had changed her relation to distance. Above lay Veyr and
the release chapel and the folio where Lio's name bent a page. Behind lay Ives
Mar and two Canticle survivors, their failed anchor checks breathing inside a
room designed for consent. Below waited the deep chapel, clean and kind and no
longer innocent to her.

Sael went down.

The lower stair accepted her without echo.

She carried no cradle now. That made her hands feel improperly free. They kept
wanting to close around handles, to take pressure from an object and call the
pressure external. The slate did not give them enough work. It lay against her
left palm, exact and cool.

At the first landing, she stopped.

Not from doubt. Doubt had followed her from the first ledger line. This was
calculation.

Full resonance required three permissions in ordinary practice: custodial hold,
elder authorization, and auditor anchor registration. She had the first. She did
not have the second. The third was the cost. It could not be borrowed. It could
not be assigned to a room or a doctrine or a superior whose voice made pain
orderly.

Auditor anchor registration.

The phrase had once seemed dry to her. Now it looked like a door with her name
on it.

She resumed descending.

At the bottom, the deep chapel waited open.

The room looked as it had before. White floor. Soft black walls. Basin at the
entrance. Low release bed at the center. Seven chalk lines scored around it,
their curves still bent inward by less than a hair where the reliquary's weight
had taught them attention.

The casing rested on the bed under hold.

No stain. No visible wrongness. The chamber was still almost kind.

Sael washed her hands. The basin accepted sweat, slate dust, the faint metal
trace from the outer cell door. It kept none of them. When the water drained,
her fingers looked too clean for what they intended.

She placed the lower slate in its wall slot.

Held under irregularity.

Cause: edited surface.

A third blank opened because she touched it with intent.

Audit class.

Sael did not press the first mark. Surface review. It would be lawful, careful,
inadequate.

She did not press the second. Elder referral. It would be obedient, careful,
inadequate for the thing that had already happened in her own record.

She pressed the third.

Full resonance.

The chapel floor lowered by a fraction. Not warning. Accounting.

A new line opened beneath the audit mark.

Authorization.

Sael set her thumb over the blank and did not press.

She could stop here. The slate would close after three breaths and keep only a
failed petition. Veyr would see the attempt. He would know the shape of her
disobedience without its completion. He might punish her. He might agree. He
might perform the audit himself and spare her anchors the imprint.

Spare.

The word entered like appetite.

Sael lifted her thumb.

She did not falsify Veyr's seal. She did not enter authorization where none had
been given. That would have made the record lie before the memory had a chance
to answer.

Instead she touched the line below, the one no unauthorized hand was meant to
open.

Auditor assumes cost.

The slate woke beneath her fingers.

This was the forbidden path. Not hidden. Not dramatic. Only narrow, old, and
left in the procedure because sometimes every authorized answer arrived too late
or too clean. An auditor could assume cost without elder seal. The record would
not call it lawful. It would call it attributable.

Attributable was a hard word. It meant no doctrine would carry the weight for
her.

Sael pressed her thumb to the cost line.

The slate opened her anchor registration.

Name.

She spoke because writing would not be enough. "Sael Orro."

The chapel took the sound and set it near the reliquary.

Station.

"Narthex of Low Mercy."

The black walls seemed to draw nearer by a breath, not crowding her, only
recognizing themselves inside the sentence.

Office.

"Keeper of release ledgers."

The lower slate warmed.

Doctrine.

Sael closed her eyes.

The old line waited. It had not abandoned her. That was the grief of it. She
still believed it, though belief no longer made it a floor.

"A burden set down is not betrayal."

The reliquary answered.

No vision opened. No corridor unfolded. The full resonance had not begun. The
casing remained sealed on the release bed, and Sael's hands were not in the
contact grooves.

But the witness memory had heard the shape of her anchors.

Pressure moved under the fourth line.

Not enough to break it. Not enough to replace it. A small palm against metal,
wide-fingered and cold through condensation, settled beneath the word burden as
if it had found the first place in her that could bear weight.

Sael opened her eyes.

The slate had recorded the registration.

Auditor: Sael Orro.

Authorization: assumed.

Cost: anchor imprint accepted.

The letters held steady. They did not ask whether she was brave. They did not
ask whether she was faithful. They only made her accountable.

Inside the casing, the hand struck once.

This time Sael felt it answer from within her own sequence.

The audit had not started.

The cost had.
