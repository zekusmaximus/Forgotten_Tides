The chapel did not remember the seven releases.

That was its work and its tenderness. By the time the last penitent left, the
benches had folded themselves into the walls with no stored warmth, the chalk
ring had been brushed thin, and the small reliquaries lay open on their cloths,
emptied of the burdens they had been asked to set down. No light lingered where
grief had been. No floor seam bowed around the shape of a sob.

The Narthex had done kindness and let the evidence of kindness go.

Only the sealed folio remained heavy.

It waited on the ledger stand beside the black evacuation casing, outside the
release ring and inside every consequence of the morning. Dust had gathered
along the casing's lower edge in a dark skirt. The folio clasp rested closed
over Sael's handwriting, and beneath the clasp one name kept the paper from
lying flat.

Behind the west wall, three Canticle survivors breathed inside the seal recess.

Sael could hear none of it. The recess door held by function and by the careful
lie she had given it: maintenance until service ends. Still, the knowledge of
breath had mass. It pulled at the side of her face. It made the chapel seem
slightly unbalanced, as if a hidden room had leaned close to listen.

Veyr Anik stood in the cleared chalk ring until the last acolyte finished
folding the cloth.

He had completed the rite without raising his voice, without looking toward the
west passage, without allowing the penitents to learn fear at the threshold of
release. That had been mercy. Or discipline. Sael could no longer keep those
words in separate hands.

Tovan waited near the side door. His palm, borrowed earlier by the outer
threshold, had gone red around the old pressure mark. Ilet stood at the emptied
reliquaries with a basin of gray water. No one spoke. The chapel had been asked
to forget too much to bear casual sound.

Veyr looked at Sael at last.

"Now," he said.

No accusation entered the word. It did not need to. Sael bowed her head once and
went to the west passage.

The door to the service passage opened with a resistance it had not had before
the breach. The Narthex had learned a new caution there. Metal remembered having
been folded. Frost remembered the shape of a tear. The maintenance seam behind
the pressure panel remained bent at one corner, sealed over by Tovan's temporary
work and a strip of black binding wax.

Sael set both palms on the recess door.

For one breath she let herself want it empty.

That was a smaller cowardice than wanting the breach undone, and therefore
easier to recognize. She wanted to open the recess and find tools, blank wall,
no living witnesses to the thing she had held through the rite. She wanted Ives
Mar to have been a pressure hallucination given shape by the Horizon. She wanted
the preserved memory to be what the form had said: voluntary, burdened, ready.

She opened the door.

Ives sat with his back against the recess wall, hands visible on his knees. The
discipline of that posture had survived pain, hunger, and whatever the Black
Horizon had taken from his anchors. His face looked worse without the helmet
glass between them. Not older. Less held. Attention slid from his left shoulder
and corrected itself when it found the Canticle insignia burned there.

The first officer crouched beside him, one arm around the smaller survivor. The
smaller one had stopped whispering. That did not comfort Sael. Silence was not
always stability. Sometimes it was only a place where a name had failed to
arrive.

Ives looked past Sael toward the chapel.

"The memory," he said.

Veyr's voice came from behind her. "Water first."

Ives's eyes moved to him.

For the first time since the breach, Sael saw the Canticle captain with no task
ready in his hands. He had measured doors, weapons, bodies, floor angles, the
distance to evidence. Veyr gave him no useful shape for those measurements. The
elder stood in a plain robe with both hands folded into his sleeves, old and
unarmed and impossible to dismiss as harmless.

"You preside here," Ives said.

"I serve here," Veyr answered. "Presiding is a heavier verb than this chapel is
permitted to keep."

The first officer made a rough sound. It might have been disbelief. It might
have been an anchor slipping.

Veyr did not look at him sharply. "Tovan. Ilet. Water, salt, and a low cot in the
outer silence cells. Their weapons remain in the passage. Their names are not to
be requested until they can offer them without losing more."

Sael felt the instruction enter the corridor and change it. Not kindness alone.
Custody with clean hands. Mercy shaped like a lock.

Ives tried to rise.

His body refused him by two inches. He stopped, not because pain won, but
because he was trained enough to know when losing balance would become an
argument someone else could use against him.

"That casing is evidence from a failed evacuation," he said. "If you move it,
you damage chain of custody."

Veyr considered him with a patience that made Sael's shoulders tighten.

"There," he said softly. "Already useful."

Ives stared at him.

"A child may be trapped in that memory," he said.

"A child may be trapped by what you intend to make the memory do." Veyr turned
slightly to Tovan. "Captain Mar is not to be harmed. He is not to be obeyed."

Tovan inclined his head.

The first officer said, "We are not your penitents."

"No," Veyr said. "You are wounded people in a sanctuary you entered by force.
That gives us obligations before it gives us answers."

The words should have been a relief. They were, partly. Sael had feared the
moment when Synod hands closed on Canticle bodies. She had feared, with a shame
she could not yet examine, that Veyr would make violence simple by naming it
defense.

Instead he made it complicated and therefore heavier.

Ilet helped the smaller survivor from the recess. The survivor flinched at the
chapel spill-light and pressed one hand over the center of their suit as if
holding a private door shut. Tovan guided the first officer next. The officer
looked once toward the weapons on the floor and then away when Ives said nothing.

Ives came last.

He paused beside Sael, close enough that she smelled the stale metal of his
breath filter and the sour trace of corridor sickness on his skin.

"Do not let him bury it," he said.

Sael kept her eyes on the bent panel. "You will walk."

"Sael."

Her name in his mouth had no right to weigh anything. It did anyway, because
names were hooks and because she had already given him one.

Veyr said, "Captain."

Ives looked at him.

"You may keep your breath for the work of remaining yourself."

For a moment Sael thought Ives would answer. His jaw moved. Then the first
officer stumbled, and Ives used what strength he had left to turn toward his
people. Tovan took them into the passage, Ilet following with water and the
basin.

The recess stood empty.

Not clean. Empty.

Sael closed the door. It sealed more easily now that it was not being asked to
hide living weight, and the ease accused her more sharply than resistance would
have.

Veyr waited until the footsteps faded toward the outer silence cells. Then he
looked at the bent maintenance seam, the two weapons on the floor, the pale
marks on Sael's hands where the recess had borrowed pressure from her.

"State the breach," he said.

The form of command steadied her. A ledger could hold a terrible thing if the
lines were clean.

"During active release, a Canticle recovery team entered through the west
maintenance seam," Sael said. "Three survivors. Two armed. All under-anchored.
No weapon discharged. No penitent made contact with fear from the breach. No
reliquary surface scarred."

"You disarmed them?"

"Captain Mar ordered the weapons down. I moved them from the chapel door."

"You opened the seal recess."

"Yes."

"You placed them inside."

"Yes."

"You told the recess to hold."

The word came back with its full weight. Tovan had warned her. Holds was a
dangerous verb in a station built for release.

"Yes," Sael said.

"Did you report when Tovan found you?"

"No."

"Did Tovan instruct you to report?"

"Yes."

"Why did you refuse?"

She could have answered with procedure. Active release. Open reliquaries. Nine
penitents in final response. Any of those lines would have been true and
acceptable.

Veyr had not asked for acceptable.

"Because if I called you, fear would enter before you did," she said. "Because
there were weapons in a passage beside the chalk ring. Because the captain's
people were close enough to panic that a raised voice might have become an
order. Because the rite was doing real mercy and I did not want their breach to
teach the chapel a new function."

Veyr was silent.

The service passage dimmed by one correction. The Narthex, relieved of the
concealed bodies, seemed to lift by less than a breath. The evacuation casing in
the chapel answered by settling harder onto the ledger stand.

"And because he recognized the memory," Veyr said.

Sael's throat tightened.

"Yes."

"Before or after you chose concealment?"

"Before I closed the recess. After I chose not to shout."

Veyr nodded, as if the sequence mattered. It did. Sequence was the difference
between mercy and excuse, between accident and doctrine.

"You preserved the rite," he said.

The sentence struck her in the place that still wanted praise.

"Yes," she said.

"You also preserved his chance to speak again."

That place closed.

"Yes."

"Both can be true. Truths do not become lighter because they dislike sharing a
line."

Sael lowered her gaze. The two Canticle sidearms lay near the torn panel, dense
and ugly and real. Their presence made the passage feel overfurnished.

"Will I be removed from ledger duty?" she asked.

"Do you want removal to make this simpler?"

The question was gentle. It was not kind.

Sael thought of the folio clasp, of Lio bending a page where no name belonged.
She thought of Ives saying evidence as if the word could wash his hands. She
thought of Veyr completing the release service while three armed intruders
breathed behind a wall.

"Yes," she said.

Veyr's face softened with something too exact to be pity. "Then no."

He stepped past her into the chapel.

Sael followed because there was nowhere else honesty led.

The chapel looked almost bare now. Ilet had taken the emptied reliquaries away.
The chalk ring was a faint crescent at the floor where the brush had missed one
curve. The benches were gone. The preserved evacuation casing sat beside the
sealed folio with the stubborn patience of an unended question.

Veyr stopped before it.

"Captain Mar will call this witness," he said.

Sael did not answer.

"The Canticle Fleet will call it evidence. Their archivists will call it
recoverable truth. Their commanders, if they are ashamed, will call it necessary
review. If they are angry, they will call it a weapon and try to make anger look
like justice."

The casing thrummed once, low enough that Sael felt it in the ledger stand
before she heard it.

"They are not wrong to hate what may have happened," Veyr said. "Do not protect
yourself from them by making them monsters. Monsters are easy to release."

Sael looked at him then.

His eyes remained on the casing. There was no contempt in his face. That made
the teaching harder to refuse.

"They have their mercy," he continued. "They preserve. They recover. They say:
let no death become unrecorded. They say: let no failure hide in the dark. Those
are not wicked sentences. Many decent people have given their lives to them."

"Then why does it sound like accusation when you say it?"

"Because decent sentences can still become cages."

The folio clasp clicked softly as the casing's weight shifted the stand by a
fraction.

Sael placed one hand on the ledger edge. Not on the folio. Near it. She did not
trust herself to touch the name without becoming less precise.

Veyr said, "Imagine they take this memory."

"I have."

"No. You have imagined rescue. Imagine process."

She closed her eyes because she knew he was right to ask it and hated him for
being right. Process was where institutions lived. Not in the clean moment of
finding a body or hearing a name. In custody stamps. In hearings. In orders
reopened because shame needed a corridor back to usefulness.

"Imagine the first record," Veyr said.

Sael did.

The first record was never only first. It taught the second what it could ask.
If the casing entered Canticle custody as evidence, mourning would have to
petition evidence for room. If it entered as route data, every later mercy would
arrive after use had already named itself necessary.

She imagined the casing aboard a Canticle ship. Stabilized, copied, audited,
presented. The child-name Lio entered into reports that would not know how to
hold a child. The hand on the bulkhead replayed for officers who needed the
sound to mean command failure, Synod crime, route negligence, jurisdiction. The
dead made to answer questions the living had written too late.

She imagined Ives standing in that room, exhausted and correct, letting the
memory be used because proof was the only mercy he had left.

Veyr said, "They will ask the dead to remain legible until the living finish
their arguments."

Sael opened her eyes.

"If the memory was taken," she said, "release without witness would hide the
taking."

"Yes."

The answer came too quickly for doctrine to protect her.

Veyr touched the edge of the reliquary cradle, not the casing itself.

"Coerced release is theft," he said. "So is coerced witness. That is why this
work is difficult, and why people who enjoy certainty should not be trusted with
mercy."

Sael wanted to believe him.

The want rose in her with a force that frightened her more than doubt had. If
Veyr was right, then Ives Mar was not a rescuer but another kind of custodian,
another institution reaching through a wound to claim the dead for use. If Veyr
was right, then Sael's first horror could return to its proper size: an anomaly,
a petition failure, a grief that needed finer handling before it could be set
down. If Veyr was right, then Lio's name did not have to become a summons. It
could remain what the Synod had always taught a name might become at the end:
a hook gently loosened from flesh.

She wanted that.

It would let the Narthex be merciful again without being innocent.

The trouble was the hand.

Not even the image of it. First resonance had given her almost no vision. Only
the flat sound against metal, small and urgent, kept beneath discipline by
someone who had been trying not to frighten a child. The memory had not said
no. It had not said yes. It had struck the bulkhead once and made a name heavy
enough to bend a ledger page.

"What does the child suffer if we remember?" Veyr asked.

Sael looked at the black glass.

"Use," she said.

"What does the child suffer if we release?"

The word waiting in her mouth was peace.

It should have been peace.

"Silence," she said.

Veyr drew a slow breath. "Good."

The praise did not warm her this time. It entered like a weight set carefully
on a scale.

"Then we do not leave the memory here," he said.

Sael looked up.

The turn had come without a raised hand, without a bell. That was how decisions
moved in the Narthex when they had already become heavier than argument.

"Where will it go?" she asked.

"The deep chapel."

The service lights made their small correction. Sael felt the chapel floor
answer the words, not with fear, but with recognition.

The deep chapel lay below the lower ledger cells, near the station's lowest
coherence point. Novices did not serve there. Most acolytes entered only after
their second year of fasting rites, and then only to clean the approach steps
without speaking. It was not an execution chamber. Sael had been taught that
carefully. It was a place where memory was brought when ordinary rooms could no
longer hold it without learning hunger or grief.

Near the deep chapel, the Narthex remembered almost nothing except descent,
release, and return.

Not all who entered with a burden returned with one.

"For adjudication?" Sael asked.

"For mercy."

"Those are not the same instruction."

"No," Veyr said. "That is why you will listen while carrying it."

Her hand tightened on the ledger stand.

"I will carry it?"

"You will escort the reliquary under my seal before second dimming. Tovan will
guard the Canticle survivors. Ilet will tend the outer threshold. I will prepare
the lower record."

Sael heard the absence inside the list. No other elder. No public transfer. No
rite announcement for the acolytes to steady themselves around. The witness
memory would leave the release chapel by narrow passages and go down, taking
its question with it.

"Elder," she said, "I concealed a breach from you during service."

"Yes."

"I spoke a holding phrase to a recess."

"Yes."

"The Canticle captain addressed me by name."

"Yes."

"Then I am compromised."

Veyr turned from the casing to face her fully.

"You are burdened," he said. "There is a difference. A compromised acolyte hides
the point where duty begins to hurt. A burdened one can still name it."

Sael had no answer.

"You recorded Lio," he said.

The name left his mouth cleanly. No flinch, no ritual avoidance. It struck the
folio and made the clasp shiver.

Sael hated that he could say it with tenderness.

"You heard Captain Mar," Veyr continued. "You chose containment at cost to your
standing. You can no longer pretend the memory is light enough for another hand
to move without consequence."

"Is this discipline?"

"Yes."

"Is it trust?"

"Yes."

"Those are not the same instruction either."

"No," Veyr said. "But they can share a corridor when the walls are thin."

The evacuation casing settled again. Dust crawled toward its lower seam.

Sael looked at the folio. She remembered the first day, Veyr asking what she
had chosen not to record. She had thought then that omission could be mercy. She
thought so still. That was the worst of it. None of the true things had stopped
being true just because another truth had entered with more weight.

"If Captain Mar is right," she said.

Veyr waited.

"If the offering was never offered."

"Then release must be approached without the comfort of calling it clean."

"And if you are right?"

"Then witness must be approached without the vanity of calling it love."

The chapel held both sentences. It did not lighten them.

Veyr lifted the black transit cradle from beneath the ledger stand. Sael had not
seen him unlock it. The cradle was a narrow frame of dull metal, made for
burdens too unstable to be carried by hand alone. Its straps had no names
stitched into them. Its handles were wrapped in plain fiber that would not keep
the shape of fingers after use.

He set it beside the casing.

"Open transfer," he said.

Sael checked her anchors before touching the reliquary.

Name. Station. Office. Doctrine.

Sael Orro.

Narthex of Low Mercy.

Keeper of release ledgers.

A burden set down is not betrayal.

The fourth line caught. Not enough to fail. Enough to make honesty necessary.

She added no correction.

The casing was colder than before. It took pressure from her palms and returned
it changed, as if the memory inside had learned the shape of her hands and did
not yet know whether to trust them. The black glass lifted from the table by the
smallest fraction, then resisted. Dust fell upward against its lower seam and
stayed there.

Veyr did not help.

That, too, was instruction.

Sael moved the casing into the transit cradle. The moment it crossed from the
ledger stand into the frame, the folio page relaxed. She did not see the line
where Lio was written, but she felt the paper lift by a hair's width, relieved
of one kind of gravity and abandoned to another.

The cradle locks closed.

The sound was quiet and final.

Veyr pressed his thumb to the seal node. The node darkened around his print and
then forgot the print, keeping only authorization.

"You will not open the casing," he said.

"No."

"You will not answer Captain Mar if he calls to you."

Sael's face warmed. "No."

"You will not make the dead useful to your doubt."

That instruction found the deepest place.

She bowed her head. "No."

Veyr rested one hand briefly on the cradle, not blessing it, only testing its
weight.

"Mercy is not the refusal to know," he said. "It is the refusal to let knowledge
become appetite."

Sael took the handles.

The cradle pulled at once. Not downward only. Toward the folio. Toward the west
passage. Toward the lower stair. Memory did not want one direction. It wanted
relations, and every relation became a line of force. Sael let the pull enter
her elbows and set her feet until her own body became the first part of the
escort path.

Behind her, the release chapel became lighter by the exact weight she now
carried.

At the threshold, Veyr said her name.

She turned.

He stood beside the ledger stand, old, patient, and more dangerous in compassion
than another elder might have been in anger.

"Do not despise Captain Mar," he said.

The instruction startled her.

"I do not."

"Good. Despising him would make him simple, and he has not earned that mercy."

Sael thought of Ives on the floor of the service passage, forcing his officer's
hand away from a weapon. Ives hearing the thrum and saying no as if recognition
were an injury. Ives using evidence because perhaps evidence was the only tool
his institution had left him.

"He believes he is preserving the dead," Veyr said. "So did we, once, in other
words. Everyone begins with a kindness they can bear to defend."

Sael waited for the warning.

It came softly.

"The danger is what kindness asks to be excused."

The cradle handles pressed into her palms.

"Yes, Elder."

She stepped into the corridor.

The Narthex had changed since the service. Not visibly enough for an untrained
visitor to name. The wall seams held a little too sharply where the station had
been made to remember itself. The west passage carried the shape of the breach
like a bruise under cloth. Farther below, the lower stair waited in a gray light
that did not reach its own end.

Sael walked toward it.

With each step, the reliquary asked her arms to become more certain. With each
step, the question inside the casing remained unspent.

Behind one outer silence door, a Canticle captain was being kept alive under a
Synod roof. Behind the folio clasp, a child's name lay flatter now and no less
real. Ahead, the deep chapel remembered descent, release, and return.

Sael carried the witness memory toward it because Veyr had named mercy, and
because for one terrible breath she had almost believed the name was enough.
