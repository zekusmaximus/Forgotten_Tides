Anchor weather arrived before the route did.

At first it was only pressure in the lower walls, a slow thickening that made
the black panels sweat and then forget the sweat. The route room did not grow
dark. It had never been bright enough for darkness to have drama. Its table
held a field of thin marks under sealed glass, each mark opening and closing
around the idea of a path before the Black Horizon could subtract it.

Sael stood beside the witness memory because the memory had learned her shape.

That was Veyr's ruling.

For three dimmings she had slept in a chair outside the deep chapel, woken under
watch, run her anchors into a slate that no longer returned office as present
tense, and eaten broth without salt because the Narthex discouraged the body
from making appetite into a record. She had not returned to the upper ledger
cells. She had not spoken with Captain Mar. She had not opened the casing.

The casing was here now.

It rested in its transit cradle at the route table's near edge, black glass
sealed, dull straps closed around it. Dust gathered along its lower seam and
then crawled away in wrong directions, uncertain which relation mattered most:
the table, the lower stair, Sael's hands, or the corridor marks opening and
dying beneath the glass.

Across the room, Ives Mar tested an exit line with two Canticle survivors and
no weapons.

Veyr had permitted it. That fact did not make the room less dangerous. If the
Canticle team could leave without the witness, he had said, then their departure
would not become another burden the Synod had to release. If they could not,
the rite would proceed under full knowledge of what preservation had failed to
save.

He had said it gently.

He had not come down to watch.

Tovan stood by the door with both hands visible. Ilet waited beside the water
basin, her face stripped of all expression except attention. Sael had been
placed at the witness cradle, not as keeper and not as acolyte. The lower slate
named her only contact custodian until rite.

Contact custodian was a clean phrase for a broken office.

Ives's portable anchor board lay open against the route table. It was Canticle
work: compact, scarred, careful where the Synod preferred spare. Four contact
plates. A narrow coherence gauge. A memory-drive key too depleted to glow except
when a hand touched it with enough self to count as fuel.

"Name," Ives said.

The first officer answered from the left plate. "Present."

"Say it."

The officer closed their eyes. "Aven Tor."

Sael had not been given the name before. It arrived thin, not offered to her,
offered to the board. She let it pass without making a record. Names spoken
under anchor audit belonged first to survival.

"Ship," Ives said.

"Canticle recovery auxiliary Pell."

The board accepted the line with a faint pressure tick.

"Task."

"Prepare exit corridor. Preserve crew. Return claim if survivable."

The last word drew a slight glance from Ives. He did not correct it.

"Return."

Aven Tor swallowed. "Near-edge beacon. Then fleet jurisdiction."

The board held.

At the second plate, the smaller survivor placed both hands flat and whispered
answers Sael could barely hear. They had offered a name on the second day under
custody, not to Sael, not to Veyr, but to Ilet while taking water. Rin. The rest
of the name remained unstable and therefore unasked.

"Name," Ives said.

"Rin."

"Ship."

"Pell."

"Task."

Rin breathed through the count twice before answering. "Keep the board from
lying."

Ives paused.

Then: "Return."

Rin looked at the route marks. "I don't know."

The board dimmed.

Aven Tor shifted as if to speak.

"Do not fill it for them," Sael said.

The room turned toward her. Even the table seemed to lift its marks by a hair.

Ives watched her across the board. His face had been thinned by three days of
custody and anchor rationing. He looked less like a man resting between dangers
than a line drawn too many times over the same failing edge.

"She is right," he said.

Aven Tor's mouth tightened, but they held silence.

Sael should not have felt steadied by his discipline. She did anyway. The cost
of disliking him cleanly had become too high.

Ives set his own hands on the final plate.

The board woke harder.

Not brighter. Canticle devices did not confuse light with coherence. The air
above the plate thickened, and the route marks under the table glass drew
toward his palms as if his bones had become a modest gravity.

"Name," Aven said.

"Ives Mar."

The board accepted him.

"Ship."

"Pell, attached recovery."

"First ship."

The line had not been part of the others' checks. Sael heard it as an older
anchor, a personal knot kept beneath rank and task because duty could fail and
still leave a first vessel's name to call a pilot back.

Ives opened his mouth.

Nothing came.

The room did not become quiet. It became attentive.

The Black Horizon pressed through that attention at once.

Rain struck the route table.

There was no ceiling leak. There was no rain in the lower route room, no weather
but the slow subtraction beyond the station walls. Yet three drops fell across
the sealed glass and rolled uphill toward Ives's hands. One drop contained the
smell of engine oil. Another carried a burst of laughter quickly swallowed by
alarm. The third flattened over the place where a name should have entered the
board and remained there, trembling.

"First ship," Aven said, softer.

Ives stared at the board.

His thumb moved to the base of each finger. First. Second. Third. Fourth. The
motion should have steadied him. Instead his outline loosened by less than the
width of sight. The chair behind him slid back without being touched, as if it
no longer trusted the amount of weight he claimed.

"I know the rail," he said.

No one answered.

"Left side of the first bridge. Brass under black paint. The captain kept a
crack in the upper viewport because he said seals should learn humility." Ives
breathed once, hard. "I know the smell after a long correction burn. I know the
instructor who slapped my glove away from the drive key. I know..."

The rain on the glass shivered.

He stopped.

Sael felt Lio move beneath her name. Not as speech. As recognition of a person
finding an absence where address should have been.

Aven's lips shaped a word.

Ives saw.

"Do not," he said.

Aven's face changed. "Captain."

"If you say it, it becomes yours telling me a fact." Ives kept his hands on the
plate. His voice remained level because he had made the break sit under
discipline. "It will not be my anchor."

The route table registered loss.

One mark below the glass opened, stretched toward the near edge, and thinned
until it became only a pale scratch on black.

Ilet crossed her arms against her ribs. Tovan did not move. Sael could feel both
of them resisting the same training: to step toward an injured person, to offer
water, to make care into action. Here, movement would be another memory for the
weather to take.

The Black Horizon had found the checks.

It came through them one by one.

Behind Rin, the wall remembered a narrow kitchen with steam on a low window.
The image did not appear whole. It made the room smell briefly of boiled grain
and a burned cloth. Rin made a small sound and pressed both hands harder to the
plate.

Aven's sleeve grew a band of white frost around the wrist. In the frost, tiny
letters formed and broke before Sael could read them. Aven did not look down.
That was courage or terror. Perhaps both had the same posture at that depth.

Under Ives's palms, the rain became a flat deck vibration. Sael felt it through
the soles of her shoes. A vessel she had never known tried to teach the route
room how it had once held him. It had everything except its name.

The lower walls flexed.

Not physically. Worse. They remembered being other walls. A cell wall. A bridge
wall. Tern Gate bulkhead metal cold beneath a child's spread palm. For one
breath Sael stood in all of them and in none of them, and the Narthex's clean
black panels had to fight to remain panels rather than proof.

"Stop the check," Tovan said.

"If we stop now, the board keeps the break," Ives said.

"Then break less."

The Canticle captain looked at him. Exhaustion left no room in his face for
offense. "That is the work."

Rin whispered, "There are too many homes in here."

The route marks responded to homes. They brightened, scattered, pulled in four
directions. One sought the near-edge beacon. One curled back toward the torn
maintenance seam. One bent toward Tern Gate so hard the table glass creaked.
One opened toward nothing Sael could name and died before the room could learn
it.

The witness casing settled deeper into its cradle.

The whole table leaned toward it.

Sael placed one hand on the cradle before thought could become argument.

Cold entered her palm. With it came the child's hand on bulkhead metal, the
adult's arm holding a small body close, the donor phrase laid over refusal, and
beneath all of it the route pressure Veyr had admitted without flinching.

Fear points home.

She still hated the sentence for being true.

"Sael," Ives said.

He should not have used her name. He knew enough by now to know that. The name
still steadied the room because it was the only one being called from outside a
failed anchor.

She did not look at him.

"Run your sequence," she said.

"I am running it."

"No. You are searching the lost line."

His jaw worked once.

"Let it be absent," she said.

Aven stared at her as if she had proposed killing him by grammar.

Sael kept her hand on the cradle. "A false recovery will not hold. A borrowed
name will not hold. Let the absence be where it is."

"That is Synod counsel," Aven said.

"Yes."

"Your counsel ends in release."

"Sometimes."

The word entered the room and did not find an easy place to land.

Sael felt Veyr in it. His patience, his grief, his dangerous refusal to make
harm simpler than it was. She was not outside his teaching. She could not become
outside it by wanting Ives to live. The Narthex had trained her to let go of
what could not be held cleanly. That training was the only reason she could see
the difference between an absence and a lie.

"Anchor to what remains," she said.

Ives closed his eyes.

Rain slid under his fingers, impossible and cold.

"Name," he said.

Aven answered the prompt for him. "Ives Mar."

"Ives Mar," Ives repeated.

The board held.

"Ship."

"Pell," he said. "Current assignment. Recovery auxiliary Pell."

"First ship," Aven said.

Ives's mouth tightened.

"Lost," he said.

The board jolted.

The route room lowered by a fraction. Not down. Into weight. Sael felt the
station recognize a true absence and stop spending force against it.

"Task," Aven said.

"Prepare exit corridor. Preserve crew." He opened his eyes and looked at the
witness casing. "Preserve witness if survivable."

The casing answered.

Not with a thrum. With weight.

Every loose thing on the route table moved toward it: rain, dust, one torn fiber
from Ives's glove, the pale scratch of the failed route mark. The witness memory
did not open. It did not speak. It simply became the heaviest relation in the
room.

Lio settled under Sael's name.

For one breath, Sael was the most stable person there.

The realization did not arrive as pride. It arrived as embarrassment, almost.
The Canticle crew had entered with anchors like tools, names worn smooth by
drills, ship histories, return beacons, tasks braided into duty until each line
could answer another. The Black Horizon had found all those lines and bled
through them.

Sael had been trained to become lighter.

Her anchors were few and spare. Name. Station. Office, now past. Doctrine,
wounded but not gone. The Synod had taught her not to clutch at every image that
passed through the mind begging to become self. It had taught her to let memory
end without contempt. That teaching had been used to excuse theft. It still knew
how to stand in weather.

She stood.

The route marks steadied around her hand on the cradle.

Ives saw it. So did Tovan. So did Ilet, whose face finally changed, not with
fear but with sorrow that had found its object.

"It answers to you," Ives said.

"No," Sael said.

The correction came at once, hard enough to keep the room from making a false
map around her.

"It answers through me. That is not the same mercy."

The witness casing pressed colder into her palm.

The route table drew one line from the lower edge of the map field toward the
near-edge beacon. It was thin. It wavered. Halfway across, it bent toward Tern
Gate and almost tore itself apart. Sael felt the child's hand in that bend, felt
fear pulling toward home because fear had not been allowed to become anything
else.

Ives leaned forward. "Can we ride that?"

"No," Aven said before Sael could answer. "Not enough coherence."

Rin whispered, "Burn the casing."

No one moved.

The words were small. Frightened. Practical.

They entered the room like a dropped tool.

Burn the casing.

A memory drive could do it. Even depleted, the Canticle key could take a dense
witness memory and spend it into corridor force. Fear had weight. The route
would thicken. The mark would open. The Pell, if the skiff still remembered
itself beyond the maintenance seam, might carry them out.

The witness would not.

The donor phrase would become irrelevant. The refusal beneath it would become
irrelevant. Lio's name would flare into use and then lose the chance to remain
anything at all. The Synod would call that release if it happened in a rite.
Canticle would call it emergency fuel if it happened under a board.

Both words would leave the child gone.

Sael took her hand from the casing.

The route mark thinned at once.

Ives said, "No."

It cost him. Sael heard the cost because his lost ship name had left a hollow
where another sacrifice might have been easier to justify. He needed the route.
He needed his people alive. He needed the witness out. He still said no before
the room could make Rin's fear useful.

Rin began to cry soundlessly.

Ilet went to them this time. The movement stirred the table marks, but not
enough to break them. She placed one cup of water beside Rin's plate and did not
touch their hands.

"No burn," Ives said. "Mark it."

Aven looked at him. "Captain, if we cannot thicken the line -"

"No burn."

"Then we lose it."

"Maybe."

Sael looked at him then.

Maybe was not enough. Not for the route. Not for the memory. Not for the room
where Veyr's mercy and Ives's preservation had both brought knives clean enough
to pass for instruments.

The route did not need the witness spent.

It needed the witness held.

The knowledge entered Sael without vision. It came from procedure, from the
weight under her palm, from the way the route mark had steadied when Ives named
his loss honestly and bent when fear tried to point back to Tern Gate. Burn the
memory, and the path would thicken once. Preserve it, and the path might keep
relation long enough to be followed.

Might.

All clean mercies had already arrived too late.

Sael stepped to the lower slate beside the route table.

Tovan's hand lifted by an inch. He did not stop her.

The slate recognized her as contact custodian. Its first fields were already
set by Veyr's disposition.

Acolyte disposition: held until rite.

Office: withdrawn.

Post-rite burden: release.

The last line waited like a door built before anyone knew what would come
through it.

Sael opened the route field.

Mode.

The slate offered three permitted marks.

Personal anchor burn.

Stored memory burn.

Release resonance.

Each one was a corridor with the walls already named.

Personal anchor burn would take from Ives or his crew until one of them became
too light to return. Stored memory burn would consume the witness cleanly and
call the opening survival. Release resonance would do what Veyr intended in the
rite: spend the memory's final coherence toward ending, not testimony.

There was no mark for preserve.

Of course there was no mark.

The Narthex did not prepare burdens to remain.

Sael heard Veyr's voice as clearly as if he stood beside the table. Witness can
become a second captivity.

True.

She heard Ives in the outer silence cell. Use is not the only harm.

True.

She heard Lio under her own name, not accusation, not forgiveness.

Weight.

Sael touched the blank line beneath the permitted marks.

The slate resisted. Not violently. It simply had no habit for what her hand was
asking it to hold.

"Sael," Tovan said.

His voice held warning and grief in the same small space.

"If you add a mark here, the lower record will carry it to Veyr."

"Yes."

"Before the rite."

"Yes."

"Then it is no longer only thought."

She looked at him.

"It never was."

Tovan closed his eyes once and stepped back.

Sael pressed the blank again. This time she did not ask the slate for a
category. She gave it one.

"Witness preservation," she said.

The slate warmed.

It did not accept.

She kept her finger on the blank until the heat crossed into pain. The station
had been built for release. It had also been built for exact record. Those
obediences met under her hand and could not immediately decide which one had
the stronger claim.

"Transit resonance," she said.

The route table answered with a thin pulse.

The slate opened a new line.

Burn status.

Sael's breath caught. The record had made room, not out of mercy, not out of
agreement, but because an exact thing had been placed before it and exactness
was one of the old kindnesses still alive in the Narthex.

"Unburned," she said.

The word struck harder than she expected.

The witness casing lifted in its straps by the width of a fingernail and then
settled. The route mark under the glass drew a narrow line toward the near-edge
beacon, corrected toward Tern Gate, corrected back, and held between them.

Not stable.

Viable.

The difference had a cost-shaped edge.

The slate opened another field.

Custodian.

Sael knew before it asked.

The memory had learned her shape. The route had steadied through her hand. A
preserved witness could not be left as an object on a table and expected not to
become fuel the moment fear made fuel practical.

Someone had to carry the prohibition.

Someone had to be the room where the memory remained unspent.

Sael had been trained all her life to become light enough to serve release.
Now that training left her standing while heavier people slipped. It gave her
the steadiness to carry a burden she had been raised to set down. It did not
make carrying holy. It only made refusal possible.

She pressed the custodian field.

"Sael Orro."

The slate took her name.

Lio answered beneath it.

The route room changed.

No alarm sounded. No elder appeared in the doorway. The walls did not crack to
announce that obedience had broken. The room simply grew heavier by one preserved
thing, and the Narthex, built to remain light, had to learn the weight.

Ives withdrew his hands from the board.

The route mark stayed.

His face had gone very pale. "That makes you part of the route."

"No," Sael said.

Then, because the old habit of clean language would not let her keep comfort
where accuracy belonged, she corrected herself.

"Yes. But not fuel."

The distinction was small.

The distinction was everything.

Aven looked at the slate, then at Sael. "If you fail, the corridor takes the
memory anyway."

"Yes."

"If you hold it too tightly, the route may shear around you."

"Yes."

"If Veyr releases it before we open -"

"He will try," Sael said.

She had not meant to say it that way.

Not Veyr will harm it. Not Veyr will steal it. Try. As if he remained what he
was: sincere, merciful, dangerous, and wrong at the point where mercy asked a
stolen fear to disappear.

The lower slate cooled around her mark.

Mode: witness preservation, transit resonance.

Burn status: unburned.

Custodian: Sael Orro.

The words looked impossible only because they had become real.

Sael ran her anchors.

Name.

Sael Orro.

Lio.

The child's name no longer merely settled beneath hers. It weighed there,
recognized by the route field, tied to the unburned line her hand had made.

Station.

Narthex of Low Mercy.

The station answered, slower than before.

Office.

Past.

The answer hurt less than it had in the deep chapel. That frightened her. Loss
could become familiar. Familiarity could become permission.

Doctrine.

The old sentence rose.

A burden set down is not betrayal.

Sael held it.

Then she placed beside it the older line Veyr had taught her and could not now
use to release her from its meaning.

A burden cannot be set down by the hand that stole it.

The two sentences did not cancel each other. They made a room.

Inside that room, Lio remained.

Ives said nothing. That was the first kindness he could offer.

At the route table, the thin mark held its course, unsteady and viable, pointing
not home and not away from home, but through the damage between them.

Sael took her hand from the slate.

The mark stayed.

For the first time since the full resonance, the witness memory did not ask to
be released.

It asked to be kept.
