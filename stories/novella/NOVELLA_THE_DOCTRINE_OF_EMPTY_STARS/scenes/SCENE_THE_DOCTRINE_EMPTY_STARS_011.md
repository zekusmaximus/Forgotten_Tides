The corridor opened badly.

That was the mercy of it. A clean opening would have lied.

The route mark on Ives Mar's board widened by less than a finger. It did not
become a door. It became a refusal in the air above the cracked chalk: a narrow
place where the lower nave no longer agreed to be only station, and the failed
Tern Gate evacuation no longer agreed to remain past.

Sael felt the line before she saw it.

It pulled through her hand on the witness casing, through Lio beside her name,
through the slate that still named the memory preserved, unburned, and under her
custody. The pull did not ask for release. It asked for sequence.

Child to guardian.

Guardian to exit.

Exit to station.

Station to home.

The line had been there since the full resonance. It had been hidden under false
offering, under donor phrases, under the clean black patience of the Narthex.
Now Lio's name gave it enough weight to cut a path through failure, and every
wall near the lower nave had to decide what it would stop being in order for the
path to hold.

Ives bent over the board. His shoulders had begun to shake with fatigue, but his
hands stayed disciplined: one on the contact plate, one near the depleted
memory-drive key and not on it.

"Narrow," Aven Tor said.

"Yes," Ives said.

"Too narrow."

"Also yes."

No one mistook the answers for calm.

Rin's lips moved around their own name. The board took the name each time and
gave back less. The Black Horizon was close enough now that even repetition
spent itself. Beyond the high panels, the service stair showed in broken pieces
where the wall had thinned under Lio's added weight. The acolytes held the
station sequence in a low murmur.

Wall.

Floor.

Threshold.

Chapel.

Nave.

Each word made a small repair. Each repair cost the room around it.

Veyr stood opposite Sael with the release bed between them. He had not resumed
the rite. He had not forgiven the interruption. His hands were folded in his
sleeves as if mercy could still be kept warm there.

"Captain Mar," he said, "can you make passage?"

Ives looked at the line. "For three people and a casing, maybe."

Aven did not turn. "Four."

"Four if no one asks the corridor to be kind."

Kind had become a technical term. Sael hated that she understood it.

The corridor flickered. The thin opening folded toward the casing, not hungry,
not cruel. Simply accurate. The witness memory was the heaviest relation in the
room. Any route trying to remain itself near the Black Horizon would seek the
thing with the most memory and use it for shape.

Use was not the only harm.

Sael kept her palm on the casing strap. The cold had already passed through
pain into numbness. That frightened her more than pain would have. Pain still
argued that a boundary existed.

"If we move now," Ives said, "we follow the stair. The route can only stay open
where the station still knows threshold from wall."

Tovan's recitation stuttered for one syllable. He recovered.

Veyr heard the implication. "Behind them?"

Ives's eyes stayed on the board. "Behind us, the station spends what it stops
needing."

No one spoke for a moment.

Sael looked at the lower nave.

It had once seemed beautiful to her because it had asked so little of the world.
No ornament except what procedure required. No names on the walls. No history
pressed into stone to make grief civic and permanent. The room had been built
to hold a burden only until the burden could be set down.

Now the room had to pay because the burden had not been cleanly offered.

The chalk arcs nearest the release bed lifted at their edges. One line broke
from itself and slid toward the corridor mark as a pale curve without ritual
meaning. The release bed sank by a fraction, not into the floor but into
importance. The rest of the nave became lighter around it.

"Move the casing," Ives said.

Veyr's gaze came to Sael.

Not permission. Not command.

A question he would not soften by asking aloud.

Sael lifted the transit strap.

The casing did not want to move. It had never wanted or refused in any human
sense; that would have been too easy. It simply had weight, and weight had
relation, and relation had direction. When Sael pulled, the casing drew the
release bed with it until the cracked chalk screamed faintly under the load.

Tovan stepped forward. "Custodian."

He did not say her office. He did not say acolyte.

Sael almost thanked him. She did not have room for gratitude. She gave him the
rear strap instead. Together they raised the casing enough for Aven to slide
the transit cradle beneath it.

The corridor mark steadied.

Ives exhaled once. "Board first. Aven with me. Rin, drive key only if I tell
you. Sael stays on the casing."

"I will not be placed behind my own custody," Sael said.

He looked at her. His face tightened, then accepted the correction because
there was no time to spend on rank.

"Casing first," he said. "Board beside. I hold the opening. You hold the
witness."

The phrase entered the lower nave and did damage.

You hold the witness.

It sounded like Canticle evidence. It sounded like Synod custody. It sounded
like a sentence no doctrine could make harmless.

Veyr's eyes lowered to the casing. "Witness can become a second captivity."

"Yes," Sael said.

The answer surprised him. It surprised her less. The old truth had not stopped
being true because he had brought it to a stolen room.

She tightened her grip on the strap. "That is why it must remain sealed."

"And carried."

"Until the ship clears the Horizon."

Veyr closed his eyes briefly. When he opened them, sorrow had been folded back
into exactness. "An interval can become a shrine if the living fear its end."

"Then I will have to answer for the interval."

"You will."

No threat. No absolution.

The corridor line flared.

It found the service stair.

Not the visible stair behind the thinning panel, but the function of stair: a
permission for one level to become another without pretending distance had
vanished. The high wall peeled open along a seam it had not possessed a moment
before. The panel did not slide away. It forgot enough of its wallhood to let
the stair be reached.

Cold air came through. It smelled of dust, metal, and a room that had just lost
its reason to remain hidden.

"Now," Ives said.

They moved.

The lower nave did not let them go cleanly. Sael took three steps with the
casing cradle dragging between her and Tovan, and the floor under her feet
lightened each time. Not collapse. Collapse would have been simpler. The floor
remembered support only where weight requested it. Behind the cradle, pale
patches opened in the black material and showed a cable run below, then a dark
void where the cable run forgot that it had ever served a wall.

Acolytes held their ring and recited station function.

Wall.

Floor.

Threshold.

Chapel.

Nave.

On the last word, the release nave behind Sael lost the shape of a rite.

She felt it go.

The change was not visible at first. The cracked chalk remained. The release
bed remained. The acolytes remained with their hands open and their faces
strained. But the room stopped knowing why they stood in a ring. It became a
large lower chamber with a heavy object leaving it and a group of people
spending breath to keep surfaces from thinning too fast.

Nave went out of it.

Sael nearly stumbled.

Tovan took more of the casing's weight without comment. That, too, was mercy.
The kind that did not ask to be named.

The corridor pressed them onto the service stair.

The stair was too narrow for the cradle. It widened only where the route touched
it, one step at a time, and closed behind them with the delicacy of a wound
refusing infection. Sael could not see the top. She could see only the next
step, the casing, Ives's board line cutting thin beside her shoulder, and Rin's
hands closed around a key they had been ordered not to use.

The witness memory remained sealed.

That was the first rule she held.

Not open. Not burn. Not speak through her.

Sealed.

Lio sat beside her name, not in her mouth, not behind her eyes. A relation, not
a passenger. The distinction had to be made again with every step because the
corridor wanted simpler arrangements. It wanted the heavy thing to lead or the
heavy thing to burn. It wanted a clean use.

Sael gave it neither.

At the first landing, the stair forgot the level below.

There had been a lower nave. There had been a release bed. There had been chalk
and Veyr across from her and the moment Lio became weight in the room. Sael
remembered all of it, and because she remembered it, the absence behind her had
shape. But the station no longer spent itself on making the lower nave reachable
from the stair.

The opening behind them closed into a wall with no seam.

Ilet's voice, distant now, held the station sequence without breaking.

Wall.

Floor.

Threshold.

Sael wanted to look back.

"Do not," Ives said.

His voice was flat with effort. He had not recovered the name of his first ship.
He had not reached for it again. The route board had accepted lost as a true
answer and now used that honesty as one of its few stable edges.

"I know," Sael said.

"I said it because I did not."

That small confession passed between them without comfort. He was tired enough
to become honest where a stronger man might have performed command.

They climbed.

At the second landing, the deep chapel door appeared in the wall where no door
belonged. Sael recognized its clean black threshold, the slight bevel at the
edge, the quiet that had once seemed kind. The corridor had drawn it here
because her body knew the route from chapel to casing to full resonance, and
the Black Horizon had learned to press on known paths.

The door stood open.

Inside, no room waited.

Only the idea of a place where burdens were made ready to end.

The casing pulled toward it.

Tovan swore once under his breath. It was so unlike him that Sael almost lost
her grip.

Ives shoved the board against the stair rail. "Sael."

"I have it."

She did not.

The casing tilted toward the false chapel with enough force to drag the cradle
sideways. The corridor narrowed. Aven braced one shoulder into the wall. Rin
made a thin sound and lifted the memory-drive key.

"No," Ives said.

Rin froze.

The false chapel widened by an inch.

Sael felt the answer the Synod had prepared for emergencies like this. Empty
yourself. Become so light that the burden cannot catch in you. Give the corridor
your raw presence and let everything else pass through absence.

Zero-anchoring.

The word came with all its forbidden precision. It would work, perhaps. It
would give the line a clean absence to hold around. It would burn what it found
of her and call that cost. She had been trained toward lightness all her life.
Her mind knew the posture. Her body almost took it.

Name.

Sael Orro.

If she zero-anchored, even briefly, she might make herself empty enough for the
corridor to pass the casing through without relation.

Lio.

And then the witness would be unclaimed at the exact moment every force in the
room wanted to claim it.

No.

Sael set both hands on the casing and stopped trying to be light.

The weight hit her.

Not the full memory. The casing did not open. No scene entered her. No child's
voice rose to make a wound useful. The witness remained sealed, and because it
remained sealed it arrived inside her as pressure with walls: an intact room she
was not allowed to enter and not allowed to abandon.

Her knees struck the stair.

Tovan took the rear strap before the cradle could slide.

Ives said something that might have been her name.

Sael could not answer. She was busy making grammar where the corridor wanted
fuel.

Name: Sael Orro.

Witness: Lio.

Status: sealed.

Burn status: unburned.

Custody: until the ship clears the Black Horizon.

Not forever.

Not evidence without mourning.

Not release by stolen hand.

Until clear.

The sealed room inside her held.

It hurt in a way pain did not know how to imitate. It was not memory burn. It
was not possession. It was continuity under load. The witness pressed against
her anchor sequence and found a place beside her name, not beneath it now, not
inside it, never replacing it. Beside. A line shared by two weights, one living
and one sealed, both refusing to become fuel.

The false chapel door thinned.

The casing settled back into the cradle.

Ives's board answered with a hard pulse. The corridor opened another handspan
along the stair, still narrow, still injured, but no longer bending toward the
chapel.

Veyr's voice came from below.

"Sael."

She looked down despite Ives's warning.

The wall where the lower nave had been was no longer closed. It had become
transparent to function, if not to sight. Through it she saw Veyr on the
landing below, one hand against the wall, acolytes behind him in scattered
positions where the ring had broken into station work. He must have followed as
far as the room allowed. His face was pale. Not with fear. With recognition.

"You did not zero-anchor," he said.

The words were not accusation. They were audit.

"No."

"You took custody inward."

"Sealed custody."

"Until clear."

"Yes."

He held her gaze through the failing wall. "Then the cost is not yet fully
known."

"No."

Veyr nodded once. "Good. Do not trust a mercy that accounts for itself too
quickly."

The sentence entered her like a kindness and a blade together.

Then the wall remembered itself and cut him from sight.

They climbed.

After that, the station forgot faster.

The archive access behind the third landing lost archive first and access
second. Rows of narrow shelves appeared through the wall, then became stacked
matter, then a smooth dark pressure with no invitation to record. The silence
cells opened along the corridor for three steps, their doors lined like closed
eyes. As Sael passed, the cells forgot silence. Sound struck their interiors
and came back wrong, full of bootfalls and breath and the scrape of the cradle.
Then they forgot cells.

Only doors remained.

Then not doors.

Each forgetting steadied the corridor ahead by a fraction.

Sael understood and hated understanding. The Narthex was spending rooms that no
longer mattered to immediate survival so the route could keep one narrow
sentence open: out, with witness unburned.

This was not sacrifice freely offered by wood or stone. Rooms did not consent.
Stations remembered what people taught them to remember. The Narthex had been
taught lightness so thoroughly that now, under strain, it could only save the
center by letting its limbs forget their names.

Veyr had helped teach it that.

So had she.

Office.

Past.

The answer struck harder here, among rooms losing function. Her office was over
not because Veyr had withdrawn a title, not only because the lower slate said
so, but because the self who could have returned to the ledger cells and called
release the first mercy no longer existed in a form the Narthex could house.

The knowledge did not make her anti-Synod.

It made her homeless inside the doctrine that had raised her.

At the top of the service stair, the west maintenance seam waited.

It had been the Canticle breach once. Now it was a ragged threshold with the
Pell's recovery lock pressed against it from outside, the small ship holding
itself to the station by clamps that groaned each time the Narthex forgot what
outside meant.

The Pell was not large enough for dignity.

Its hatch stood open. Aven went first with the route board, backing in because
Ives would not release contact until the last possible moment. Rin followed,
still holding the unused key. Ives remained at the seam with one hand on the
board and one against the hatch frame.

Beyond him, the ship's interior flickered with corridor weather. Sael saw a
row of lockers become a childhood hall and recover itself. She saw the deck
rise toward the witness casing and flatten when Aven spoke the ship's name.

"Pell," Aven said. "Recovery auxiliary. Current assignment."

The ship held.

Not proudly. Proud things wasted force.

Tovan helped Sael guide the casing over the seam. The threshold tried to decide
whether the cradle belonged to station or ship and briefly became neither. The
front runners dropped through absence. Sael's arms took the weight. The sealed
room inside her slammed against her ribs.

Lio remained beside her name.

She did not pray. Prayer would have asked someone else to hold the line.

"Now," Ives said.

The threshold remembered enough floor to receive the cradle.

They pushed the casing into the Pell.

The ship darkened around it. Not failure. Adjustment. Loose tools in the lock
rolled toward the cradle. A torn strip of insulation lifted and laid itself
against the casing strap as if even damage wanted relation.

Ives looked past Sael to Tovan. "Come."

Tovan's hands stayed on the rear strap.

For the first time since Sael had known him, he looked young.

"I have stationside function," he said.

"You have a door," Ives said.

"No." Tovan's gaze went to Sael. "I have walls that still answer my voice."

Sael wanted to argue. She could not find a clean sentence. Tovan had chosen the
same exactness that had damned and steadied them all.

Ilet appeared behind him on the maintenance side, breathless, one sleeve torn
and one palm blackened from contact with a thinning panel. She did not try to
enter the ship. She touched Tovan's shoulder, then the seam.

"Elder is coming," she said.

Veyr reached the west seam as the corridor narrowed behind him.

He had shed his outer robe somewhere below. Without it he looked less like an
elder and more like an old man who had carried doctrine until it shaped his
bones. Acolytes moved in the passage behind him, reciting wall, floor,
threshold in broken overlaps. Some were guiding others toward upper compartments
that still held pressure. Some were sealing doors that no longer knew what seal
meant. No one was screaming. That restraint made the scene worse.

Ives shifted at the hatch. "Elder Anik."

Veyr looked at him. "Captain Mar."

"There is margin for one more."

The corridor shivered at the lie or the hope.

Veyr smiled faintly. It was not warmth. It was grief recognizing courtesy in a
language it distrusted.

"No," he said.

Sael had known before he spoke. Knowing did not keep the word from hurting.

Ives did not press like a hero. He only checked the board, the seam, the casing,
the narrowing corridor. "You can still leave."

"I can still remain."

"That is not the same thing."

"No. It is the truer one."

The station passage behind Veyr thinned. Through one wall Sael saw the outline
of the lower ledger stairs, then the outline lost stairs and became a slant of
dark. Acolytes continued their recitation inside it, voices making structure
where architecture no longer could.

Sael stepped to the hatch edge. The sealed witness inside her made every motion
feel delayed.

"You marked me for release," she said.

"Yes."

"You would still, if I stayed."

Veyr's face did not change. "Yes."

Ives made a small movement behind her. She ignored it.

"Then why hold the corridor for me?"

Veyr looked at the casing inside the Pell. "Because release cannot correct a
lie by completing it faster."

The answer was so nearly what she needed that she distrusted it.

He saw that too.

"Do not make me clean in your memory," he said.

Sael's throat tightened.

"I will not."

"Do not make Captain Mar clean either."

Ives said, quietly, "No danger of that."

Veyr's gaze moved to him. "Your command will want a weapon."

"Yes."

"Your dead will want justice."

"Some of the living will call revenge justice before the report dries."

Veyr inclined his head. "Then you understand one burden."

"Not enough of yours."

"No."

There it was again: no innocence, no easy exchange. Only two compromised forms
of care meeting at a threshold neither had built cleanly.

Sael gripped the hatch frame. "Come anyway."

The words left her before doctrine could inspect them.

Veyr looked at her then with such tenderness that she almost stepped back.

"Child," he said, and the old address hurt because it had once been safe. "If I
leave, the Narthex becomes only what you carry away from it. That would also be
false."

"It is thinning."

"Yes."

"It may not hold."

"Then it should not be left with no one who knows what it tried to be."

"What it did."

"Both."

The correction passed through her and found no contradiction. The Narthex had
been a mercy house. The Narthex had stolen witness. The Narthex had sheltered
release. The Narthex had turned fear into route. A record that held only one
truth would become another false rite.

Veyr placed one hand on the seam.

The metal remembered station under his palm.

"I will hold what remains long enough for your ship to clear," he said.

"Why?"

"Because you are carrying the witness unburned." His eyes went to the casing,
then back to Sael. "And because I would still release it if you failed. That,
too, is reason for me to stay where my hand can no longer reach it."

Dangerous.

Merciful.

Coherent.

Wrong at the exact point where stolen fear asked to disappear.

Sael could not forgive him. She could not reduce him. Both refusals had weight.

The corridor began to close.

Ives's board snapped once, a sharp sound like bone under cloth. Aven called his
name from inside the ship.

"Captain."

Ives did not look away from Veyr. "Your acolytes?"

"Those who can move are moving upward. Those who can hold are holding."

"And you?"

Veyr turned slightly. Behind him, Ilet and Tovan had taken places on either
side of the seam, hands against opposite walls. Neither looked at Sael now. They
were reciting threshold with the slow precision of people keeping one word
alive by refusing every other fear.

"I remain with the Narthex," Veyr said.

Not for punishment.

Not for rescue.

For witness of another kind.

Sael wanted the old doctrine to tell her what to do with that. It offered only
fragments.

A burden set down is not betrayal.

A burden cannot be set down by the hand that stole it.

No star is owed a wound.

No wound is owed a shrine.

No shrine is owed a name.

There the doctrine failed.

Here the doctrine did not fail enough to let her hate it.

The cost arrived fully then. Not the sealed witness. Not the cold in her hands.
Not the loss of office. A simpler, larger break.

Sael could never return to the self that believed release was always mercy.

That self had been sincere. That self had been useful. That self had recorded
less than she noticed because she trusted that less could be kindness. It had
not been evil. It had not been enough.

That self did not burn. It remained as memory, intact and uninhabitable.

Ives touched Sael's shoulder once, two fingers only, to move her through the
hatch before the seam forgot permission.

She went.

The Pell's lock closed behind her by halves. First pressure. Then door. Then
seal. Each function had to be named by Aven before it held.

"Hatch," Aven said.

The inner plate slid.

"Seal."

The outer pressure steadied.

"Ship."

"Pell," Ives said.

The ship answered with a low structural groan.

Through the narrowing view, Sael saw Veyr on the station side. Tovan stood to
his left. Ilet to his right. Behind them the west passage lost depth. Rooms
unstacked themselves from memory: silence cells, service stair, lower nave,
deep chapel, ledger access. They did not vanish. Vanishing would have been too
simple. They became matter without enough function to call them back.

Veyr lifted his hand.

Not farewell.

An acknowledgment entered into no ledger.

Then the seam forgot it had ever been a door.

The Pell lurched.

Sael fell against the witness casing. The sealed room inside her held. Lio's
name stayed beside her own, distinct and heavy, while the ship turned toward
the thread Ives had opened through the failed route.

The corridor received them without kindness.

It was narrow. It was costed. It was not clean.

Behind them, the Narthex of Low Mercy paid in rooms.

Ahead, the Black Horizon waited to see whether the living could carry one
unburned witness farther than mercy had allowed.
