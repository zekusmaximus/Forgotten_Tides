Dust leaned toward the reliquary before Sael touched it.

It came in thin gray threads from the table seams, from the sleeve cuffs of the
acolytes who had carried the casing in, from the dry joints of the ledger stand.
The particles crossed the air slowly and gathered on the black glass as if the
memory inside had lowered a hand.

Sael watched until the motion steadied. Only then did she set the stylus to the
first empty line.

Witness class: evacuation.

Offering status: voluntary.

Retained burden: high.

The last phrase resisted the stylus. Its nib dragged, then sank a little deeper
than the paper required. Sael let the pressure travel up her wrist and into her
forearm. High was not a number. The Narthex did not keep numbers where a human
being might be tempted to admire precision more than mercy. Still, high had
weight. It pulled dust across a room. It made the ledger stand creak in the
quiet.

The release chapel remembered only its function.

There were no names carved above the doors, no painted cycles of founders, no
wall of rescued dead. The floor was a single dull plane, cleaned until no foot
pattern could settle into it. The benches folded into the wall after each rite.
Even the light was corrected every third pull so it would not learn to favor one
corner.

That plainness was not neglect. It was devotion shaped into engineering. In the
Black Horizon, a place that remembered too much became an argument against
itself. Rooms pulled inward around old griefs. Corridors lingered on thresholds
they had once loved. The Narthex of Low Mercy survived because it carried almost
nothing that was not needed.

Sael had always found the restraint beautiful.

Across the table, Tovan and Ilet settled the reliquary cradle into its chalk
ring. Neither spoke. Their faces were drawn from the long fast before a release
rite, but their hands were careful. Tovan brushed dust from the casing with a
fiber cloth, slow as bathing a fevered brow. Ilet checked the seal nodes twice
and then a third time, not because she distrusted the first two checks, but
because no one in the Synod treated grief as simple cargo.

The casing answered each touch with a low thrum.

Sael wrote:

Custody: received from outer penitent line.

Donor phrase: "I set down what no longer carries me."

She paused over the quotation marks. The phrase was standard, but the hand that
had recorded it was not. A formal clerk would have written the line in clean
Synod script. This one sloped hard to the right. Pressure had broken the ink at
the downstroke of carries.

Fear, then. Or haste.

She did not write that.

"You saw the break," Veyr Anik said.

Sael had not heard him enter. She kept her stylus resting on the page and lowered
her head.

"Elder."

Veyr stood just inside the chapel threshold, plain-robed, both hands folded into
his sleeves. Age had thinned him rather than bent him. It made his face seem
less like a face than a patient arrangement of bone and attention. He looked at
the reliquary first, then at the ledger, then at Sael.

"What did you choose not to record?"

The question held no trap. He had trained her long enough for that. A trap used
fear to make a mind clumsy. Veyr preferred the opposite cruelty, if cruelty was
the word for it: he made a mind precise.

"The donor phrase was copied under strain," Sael said.

"And?"

"The strain may have belonged to the clerk."

"And?"

"Or to the donor."

Veyr nodded once. "So why leave it out?"

Sael let her gaze rest on the black glass. Dust thickened along the reliquary's
lower edge. The memory inside was old enough to have cooled, but not old enough
to have lightened.

"Because strain is not refusal," she said. "Because fear can accompany chosen
release. Because if we record every tremor as evidence, we make grief stand trial
before it is allowed to end."

Tovan's cloth paused over the casing. Ilet looked down at her seal board. No one
smiled. In the Narthex, agreement rarely needed a face.

Veyr came to stand beside Sael. He smelled faintly of boiled water and ash soap.
"Good."

The word should have warmed her. It did, a little. Praise from Veyr was never
generous, but it was exact.

"The galaxy teaches that witness is always holy," he said. "It says remember the
ship, remember the colony, remember the face. It builds vaults until the dead
become masonry. And then it calls the weight love."

Sael knew this teaching. She had copied it as a novice until her fingers ached.
She had believed it before she understood it, and understood it before the Black
Horizon made it physical.

"We do not sneer at love," Veyr continued. "We only ask what love becomes when it
refuses to set down the body."

The reliquary throbbed once, soft and deep. The chalk ring held.

Sael looked at the ledger line where voluntary waited beneath her hand. The word
was a door. A proper door, if the offering behind it was true. Through it, a
memory could pass from witness into release. Not destruction. Destruction left
wreckage and wreckage remembered. Release let a burden spend itself toward the
ordinary entropy of the universe.

Less pain. Less pull. Less debt laid on the living.

This was mercy as Sael had been taught to practice it. Not a blade. Not a theft.
A loosening.

"Run the first resonance," Veyr said.

Sael inclined her head.

Tovan and Ilet withdrew to the side marks. Veyr remained beside the ledger. His
presence did not crowd her. That, too, was one of his arts.

Sael slid two fingers into the reliquary cradle's contact grooves. The black
glass was cold through her gloves. She checked her own permitted anchors before
opening attention to the casing: name, station, office, doctrine. Sael Orro.
Narthex of Low Mercy. Keeper of release ledgers. A burden set down is not
betrayal.

The reliquary answered.

Not with vision at first. With pressure.

A corridor wall, or something like one, pressed against her left shoulder. Metal
that had forgotten warmth. A warning chime clipped short before it could become
music. Breath gathered too close in too many mouths. Someone's palm slid on a
bulkhead slick with condensation.

Sael kept the contact shallow. First resonance was not full witness. It was only
enough to confirm class and burden, enough to make sure the casing contained what
its label claimed. She did not enter the memory. She let its outer mass touch the
trained surface of her mind.

Evacuation, yes.

Crowd density, high.

Orientation: outbound.

She wrote with her free hand, the stylus moving in the small script used for
records meant to vanish cleanly after the rite.

Behind the pressure came sound.

Not screams. She had braced for screams. The outer memory carried discipline:
boots kept inside painted lines, palms against shoulders, a voice counting
children by number rather than name. Someone near the recorder repeated the donor
phrase under their breath. I set down what no longer carries me. I set down what
no longer carries me.

The words were frayed, but they were there.

Voluntary, Sael thought.

Her fingers eased.

Then a hand struck the bulkhead from inside the memory.

Small. Flat-palmed. Not loud enough to be panic. Loud enough to be recognized by
the person who loved it.

A voice bent around the sound.

"Lio."

The name entered the chapel with no force at all.

The stylus stopped.

Sael kept her hand in the groove. That was procedure. A broken contact could
scar the memory surface and make later release uneven. She breathed once through
her mouth, letting her ribs lift only as far as the fasting wrap allowed.

The voice came again, lower this time, close to the recorder's body.

"Hold Lio's hand."

The contact groove cooled further beneath her fingertips.

Sael waited for the memory to shed the detail. Voluntary release memories often
carried loose fragments at the edge: a cup, a door tone, the smell of a sleeve.
Entropy took what doctrine did not need. Names were different. Names were hooks.
To leave a name inside an offering was to leave a hand still gripping the rail.

The ledger did not have a column for names.

That was by design. A release ledger protected the penitent from being re-bound
by the vanity of record. Sael had written thousands of lines without a single
proper name after the header. Parent, pilot, witness, donor, clerk. Functions
were sufficient. Functions were kind. Names gathered mass.

Voluntary, the form said.

Lio, the memory said.

Veyr's sleeve shifted beside her. "What is it?"

Sael looked at the page. She had already recorded class, burden, custody, donor
phrase. The ledger was almost clean enough to do its work. If she wrote residual
familial artifact, no rule would break. If she wrote name-sound present, the rite
supervisor would ask whether the donor had requested retention. If no request
was found, the memory would be delayed.

Delayed meant held.

Held meant weight.

Already the dust had climbed the casing in a dark skirt. Already the table
tilted by a fraction her body could feel before her eyes could prove it. The
Narthex could carry such a memory for one rite. Perhaps two. Not indefinitely.
Places had limits. Mercy had load tolerances.

"A residual tether," Sael said.

The words tasted like good procedure. Too good.

Veyr's gaze stayed on her, not on the reliquary. "Voluntary offerings often ache
where they were held longest."

"Yes."

"Do you hear refusal?"

The question was soft. Softer than accusation. Softer than comfort.

Sael listened.

The memory gave her the corridor wall again. Breath. Counting. The small palm on
the bulkhead. She did not hear no. She did not hear yes either. She heard a name
kept under the tongue of someone who had supposedly chosen to set down what no
longer carried them.

Maybe that was all grief was at the end: a mouth refusing to empty.

Maybe the mercy was to help it empty.

Her training gave her the next line. She could feel the sentence waiting in the
muscles of her hand.

Residual tether accepted for release.

The rite would proceed. The memory would thin. Lio would become less than sound,
then less than absence. No one would have to carry the child through the Black
Horizon. No station wall would bow around a grief that could no longer help the
living.

Sael had copied Veyr's teaching once in novice ink: Witness can become a second
captivity.

She believed that still.

The trouble was the hand.

Not the image of it. First resonance had shown her almost nothing. But the sound
of that palm on the bulkhead had weight. It had entered through the glove, up the
finger bones, and settled behind her own knuckles. She flexed them once. The
motion did not free it.

"Sael," Veyr said.

Not stern. Only present.

She removed her fingers from the contact grooves according to procedure: first
the left, then the right, then both palms flat on the table until her own anchors
settled. Name. Station. Office. Doctrine.

Sael Orro.

Narthex of Low Mercy.

Keeper of release ledgers.

A burden set down is not betrayal.

The fourth anchor answered, but not as cleanly as before.

She turned the ledger slightly so Veyr could see her hand. Hiding would have
been heavier. The Synod had taught her that too.

"I cannot certify it clean," she said.

Tovan looked up. Ilet's seal board clicked once against the table.

Veyr was silent long enough for the chapel lights to make their minor correction.
The shift moved across his face and left no expression behind.

"Because of the tether?"

"Because it is a name."

"Many who choose release keep a name until the last threshold."

"Then the name is recorded in the retention petition."

"If the petition survived."

He was right. Records failed. Clerks shook. Stations near the Black Horizon lost
small facts before anyone noticed the floor had changed pitch. A missing petition
was not proof of coercion. Sael knew each of these truths as well as she knew the
ledger's grain beneath her wrist.

She also knew the ledger did not become honest by choosing the lighter truth
because it was easier to carry.

"What did you hear?" Veyr asked.

Sael should have said a child's name. That would have been sufficient. It would
have preserved the shape of concern without giving the concern a body.

The dust on the reliquary rose another finger-width.

She wrote before she could make herself wise.

Lio.

Four letters. One line not meant to hold them.

The page bowed under the ink.

It was slight. Tovan might not have seen it. Ilet might have mistaken it for the
table's old warp. But Sael felt the ledger take the name. The chapel did too. A
thin creak ran through the stand, and somewhere above them a hinge remembered it
had once been part of a door.

No one moved.

Veyr looked at the word for a long time.

Sael set the stylus down parallel to the ledger's spine. Her hand felt larger
than it had before, as if the name had altered its measure.

"You have made the record heavier," Veyr said.

There was sorrow in it. Not anger. Sorrow was worse.

"Yes," Sael said.

"For an uncertainty."

She looked at the reliquary casing. Beneath the dust, black glass held the
evacuation memory in its patient pressure. A preserved terror. A chosen burden,
if the form was true. A stolen one, if it was not.

The name waited on the page. It did not explain itself. It only weighed.

"For a child," Sael said.

The Narthex kept its silence around them, disciplined and merciful and no longer
quite light.
