The first heavy thing was a bolt.

It dropped from the overhead after the Pell cleared the Black Horizon. For the
length of the corridor it had hung beside the torn access panel as if waiting
for a rule to return. Then the ship crossed some border no bell could mark, and
down became down again.

The bolt struck the deck.

Sael flinched against the witness casing.

No one cheered.

Cheering would have required certainty. The Pell had none to spend. Its lock
still smelled of burned insulation and old corridor frost. The inner hatch had
closed, sealed, and been named ship only after Aven Tor repeated the word four
times with one hand braced against the frame. A strip of wall plating had peeled
back near Rin's shoulder and stayed peeled, not forgetting now, only damaged.
That difference mattered. Damage could be repaired. Forgetting had to be
answered before repair knew what it was meant to restore.

The route board under Ives Mar's hands went dark by degrees.

Not all at once. First the Tern Gate line thinned. Then the near-edge thread
lost its hard white pressure. Then the central plate, which had been warm enough
to heat the air above it, cooled and left both of Ives's palms lying on ordinary
metal.

He did not lift them.

"Name," Aven said.

The word came out hoarse.

Ives closed his eyes. "Ives Mar."

"Ship."

"Pell."

"First ship."

The pause remained.

It was no longer a search. It had become a marked absence with edges worn clean
by use.

"Lost," Ives said.

The board did not answer. It did not need to. They were outside the Horizon now,
and ordinary machinery had less mercy than a corridor. It accepted nothing. It
only cooled.

"Return," Rin whispered.

Ives opened his eyes.

Sael could see the effort it took him to look at the ship and not past it.

"Outside," he said.

The word did not sound like salvation. It sounded like a report no one had the
strength to file.

Outside.

The Black Horizon did not release them kindly. It simply stopped being the
nearest law.

Weight came back in pieces. The bolt on the deck. A scatter of metal shavings
sliding toward the witness casing and then settling when the ship's local
gravity corrected itself. Sael's own blood remembering the bottoms of her feet.
The casing strap cutting into her palm with plain material force instead of
corridor demand.

Inside her anchor sequence, the sealed room remained.

Name: Sael Orro.

Witness: Lio.

Status: sealed.

Burn status: unburned.

Custody: until the ship clears the Black Horizon.

The last line had changed without permission. Until had become a completed
thing. Sael felt it the way a ledger page felt after the ink dried: not lighter,
only less able to pretend it was still provisional.

She waited for the witness to leave her.

It did not.

That was the first cruelty of being outside.

No scene opened. No child's voice rose. No hidden contents entered her mind.
The witness memory remained sealed in the black casing against her ribs, cold
through the torn sleeve of her robe. But the inward custody that had carried it
through the corridor did not vanish cleanly at the border. Its pressure changed.
The sealed room stopped leaning on her anchor sequence for route function. The
walls of it drew back from her name.

Lio remained.

Not beneath Sael. Not inside her. Not as a voice. A name beside a name, and now
only the name, because the witness itself was still where it had to be: sealed,
unopened, unburned.

Sael breathed once and found that breathing hurt in ordinary ways.

That, too, was information.

"Clear?" Aven asked.

Ives looked toward the forward bay, where the cracked navigation plate showed a
starfield that held its places. Stars. Small hard points, each one far enough
away to remain itself. Sael had not expected to be moved by their discipline.
The Narthex had lived under deliberate darkness and the Black Horizon's
pressure. Its few lamps had been permissions. These stars did not ask the ship
to believe in them. They were simply there, remembering themselves with a
density no station could imitate.

"Clear of immediate Horizon shear," Ives said.

Aven let out a breath. "Not clear clear."

"No."

Rin gave a sound that might have been agreement if it had not been mostly pain.
Their right sleeve was dark at the cuff. Not much blood. Enough to be another
fact no one could set aside.

Ives finally lifted his hands from the board. His fingers had tremor in them
now that command no longer required them to stay still. He saw Sael notice and
closed them around the board edge until the tremor became less visible.

Not gone.

Disciplined.

The distinction had carried them farther than heroism would have.

"Casing status," he said.

Sael looked down.

The black casing lay half across her lap and half against the lock cradle. Its
seal bands had held. The transit strap was abraded white at the edges. One
corner bore a fresh dent from the threshold crossing. No light showed through
the seam. No heat came from it. No voice. No plea. No useful wound.

"Sealed," Sael said. Her own voice sounded too formal in the small ship. "Burn
status unburned. Physical custody aboard the Pell."

Ives nodded once.

"Anchor custody?" Aven asked.

Sael felt the question move through the ship. It was not Aven's cruelty. It was
Canticle arithmetic. If a person had carried a witness through a costed
corridor, someone would need to know whether she was still carrying it.

Veyr would have asked with gentler words and the same blade underneath.

"Emergency term completed," Sael said. "No contents opened. No fusion. No
possession."

Rin looked at her then. Their face was pale under the lock light.

"And the name?"

Sael touched the casing strap with two fingers.

"Still known."

No one corrected her. No one asked whether known meant safe.

The Pell shuddered.

Every loose thing answered. A broken fastener rolled under the route board. The
peeled wall plating clicked against its brace. Somewhere forward, a pump tried
to resume its work and failed three times before catching. Each failure had a
sound. Each sound belonged to matter, not to the Black Horizon. Sael listened to
them with an attention that felt almost indecent. She had spent her life among
rooms trained to become less. Now she sat inside a damaged ship that kept
insisting on itself.

Hull.

Deck.

Air.

Ship.

Pell.

The words came silently at first. Then Aven said them aloud from the hatch
console, as if hearing the same need.

"Hull holding. Deck holding. Air thin but holding. Ship: Pell."

The ship did not grow noble under the words.

It remained a cramped recovery auxiliary with a wounded lock, a depleted route
board, and four living people arranged around a memory no one could afford to
misname.

Ives pushed himself upright. The movement cost him. Sael saw it in the delayed
set of his shoulders, the care with which he did not lean on the casing for
support.

"Aven, isolate the external recorder. Rin, get pressure on that wrist."

"It is not that bad," Rin said.

"Good. Then it will be easy to keep it from becoming worse."

Rin obeyed. Not quickly. Quickly had been spent in the corridor.

Aven moved to the forward console. The route board remained between Ives and
Sael, dark now, its last line gone from visible instrumentation and still
present in the room because they remembered where it had been.

Sael looked toward the small side port.

There was no view of the Narthex.

Of course there was not. The station had been hidden in the Black Horizon's
memory shadow before the corridor and would be hidden worse after it. No line
ran from the Pell back to the west maintenance seam. No wall recitation reached
the ship. No hand lifted in a threshold she could see.

Veyr, Tovan, Ilet, and the remaining acolytes were stationside.

The statement had edges. Nothing beyond it did.

Sael did not write dead over them in her mind. She did not write saved. She did
not write released.

Stationside.

That was the only honest word.

Ives followed her gaze and said nothing for long enough that she knew he was
choosing against comfort.

"Can you signal them?" she asked.

"No." He looked toward Aven. "Not with this damage. Not through the remaining
Horizon interference. Maybe not at all."

Maybe not at all was cleaner than false hope. It still hurt.

Sael nodded.

The old doctrine offered a sentence.

A burden set down is not betrayal.

She did not know whether boarding the Pell had been setting down or carrying
away. The difference would have to be audited later, if later remained a place
she could reach.

Aven said, "Recorder isolated. Long-range transmission is in delay until the
array resets or we patch it manually."

Ives's eyes closed for one beat.

Relief passed over his face and left no cleanliness behind.

Sael understood the shape of it before he spoke.

The report.

What Canticle would call proof had cleared the Black Horizon with them. The
sealed witness, the physical casing, the route record, the dark board, the crew
testimony, Sael herself: Canticle command would want all of it. The Synod had
preserved unwilling fear to stabilize a route. Canticle had closed a failed
recovery and let its dead be misfiled into silence. There would be inquiries.
There would be denials. There would be people who had loved the lost and would
take any instrument handed to them if the instrument promised shape for grief.

Witness could become a second captivity.

Veyr's sentence found the new room and sat down inside it.

Sael hated him for being right.

She hated more that hating him changed nothing useful.

"What happens now?" she asked.

Ives did not pretend not to know what she meant.

"First record," he said. "Damage report. Recovery claim. Witness chain of
custody. Then command."

"And then?"

His mouth tightened.

"Boards. Tribunal if anyone has the spine to convene one. Intelligence review
whether or not they call it that. Synod response if this reaches them through
proper channels, which it will not stay in for long."

"You will give them the casing."

"I will log the casing."

"That is not an answer."

"No." He looked at the witness. "It is the answer I can make true for the first
hours."

The first hours.

So small a mercy.

So real a danger.

Sael shifted the casing fully onto the cradle. Her arms trembled once the weight
left them. She folded her hands to hide it, then stopped hiding. There was no
office left whose dignity required concealment.

"Captain Mar."

He looked at her.

The rank sat on him badly in the aftermath. Not falsely. Badly. Like a coat
that had kept him alive and would never forgive him for it.

"Do not turn Lio into a weapon before the name has been mourned."

The ship noises made the sentence feel smaller than it was: pump, plate,
cooling board, Rin tearing a seal strip with their teeth. No nave. No rite. No
elder on the other side of a cracked chalk ring. Only a damaged recovery ship
and a request so precise it could not protect itself from being refused.

Ives looked down at the casing.

Sael watched him make himself tired enough to answer honestly.

"I cannot promise that."

Rin went still.

Aven did not turn from the console, but one hand stopped above the recorder
controls.

Ives continued. "Not fully. Not past the first record. Not against every person
who will see proof of what happened and call use by cleaner names."

Sael felt the answer enter her without surprise. It weighed because it was
true.

"Canticle will want justice," she said.

"Yes."

"Revenge."

"Some of them."

"Route knowledge."

He did not blink.

"Some of them."

"A charge against the Synod."

"Yes."

"A way to make their own closed order look less like abandonment."

That one struck. She saw it land in his jaw before his discipline covered it.

"Yes," he said.

No defense. No swagger. No purified institution placed between himself and the
sentence.

Sael almost wished he had argued. Argument would have let her stay inside a
cleaner anger.

"Then what can you promise?" she asked.

Ives braced one hand on the route board. "I can keep the transmission delayed
until the casualty annex is attached."

"A list."

"Names, if we have them. Unknowns named as unknowns, not numbers hidden inside
a tactical summary."

The Synod had trained Sael to distrust large records because they could make
grief civic and permanent. The Narthex had taught her that names could become
hooks.

The last five days had taught her that absence could become a theft.

"What else?"

"I can mark the witness sealed and unburned under recovery custody. No route
testing. No drive interface. No reprisal analysis until a memorial audit is
entered." His voice roughened. "That will not stop everyone. It will make them
break the first record if they want to use it sooner."

Sael understood the difference.

It was not protection. It was friction.

Friction had saved the witness from becoming fuel.

"And your report?" she asked.

"I can write that the first surviving use of the witness after exit was
mourning, not extraction."

The words were Canticle words. Report words. Useful words.

Still, something in them held.

"Can you make that true?" Sael asked.

Ives looked at the blank emergency ledger clipped beneath the console.

"Not alone. Not securely."

The old office in Sael answered before she did. It rose with all its habits:
intake line, custody mark, donor phrase, burden status, release permission,
lower-record exception. A room in her she could no longer live in still knew
how records began.

That, too, was a survival she had not asked for.

"Give me the ledger," she said.

Aven turned then.

Ives did not. He reached beneath the console and unclipped the emergency book.
It was smaller than Synod release ledgers, bound in gray composite, its pages
treated for pressure, water, and vacuum. A ship's thing. Practical. Made for
damage entries, deaths if deaths came too quickly for full forms, last messages
if communications failed.

He held it out.

Sael did not take it at once.

"If I write in this, it becomes Canticle record."

"Yes."

"If I do not, your first record begins without Lio's name."

"Yes."

There were traps that did not require malice. Institutions built them out of
sequence. First this form, then that custody, then transmission, then review,
then use. No one step had to hate the dead in order to spend them.

Sael took the ledger.

It was heavier than it should have been.

Not metaphysically. Not yet. It was heavy because it was blank and because
blank things invited the living to call order innocence.

"Pen," she said.

Rin held one out with their uninjured hand. Their fingers were shaking. Sael
took it carefully, so no one had to pretend not to see.

She opened to the first unused page.

The paper lay flat.

That steadiness almost broke her.

Synod ledgers had been designed to make names light. Their columns narrowed
toward release. Their inks thinned under accepted absence. This book had no
doctrine built into its lines. It did not know what mercy should look like. It
would learn from the first thing written in it.

Sael set the pen to the page.

For a moment, she could not move.

If she wrote Lio, the name entered Canticle custody. If she did not write Lio,
the witness remained a sealed object with a child hidden inside institutional
language. The two harms faced each other across a space too small for purity.

Veyr would have said witness can become cruelty.

Ives had said I cannot promise that.

Both men were right inside limits that could still kill.

Sael wrote the first line.

Lio.

The page bowed.

Not enough to alarm the ship. Not enough to draw tools across the lock. A small
physical answer only, paper taking the weight of a name that had been forced to
serve as route, rite, proof, and burden before anyone had given it a place to be
grieved.

Metal shavings near the casing shifted toward the ledger and stopped halfway.

Sael kept the pen down until the ink settled.

Under the name, she wrote:

Child witness, Tern Gate evacuation.

Status: sealed.

Burn status: unburned.

First post-Horizon record: mourning before use.

She paused.

The old office wanted a custodian line.

Her hand knew the shape of it. Custodian: Sael Orro. It would be true in part.
It would also place the name under her again, as if the corridor had not ended,
as if custody could be solved by being brave enough to keep holding.

She changed the line.

Physical casing aboard Pell under sealed recovery custody.

Then, because the record would become false if it gave the stationside dead or
living a shape they had not yet earned, she added:

Narthex stationside status unresolved.

Veyr Anik, Tovan, Ilet, and remaining acolytes not confirmed released, dead, or
saved.

The page bowed further.

Sael did not know whether it bowed for Lio or for the refusal to close the other
names. Perhaps there was no useful distinction. Records became cruel when they
made grief choose one category too early.

Ives read without touching the page.

"That line will cause trouble," he said.

"Which one?"

He looked at her.

For the first time since the corridor opened badly, something almost like humor
moved in his face and failed before it could become light.

"All of them."

"Good," Sael said.

The word surprised her. It had no triumph in it. Only function. A clean record
would have moved too easily.

Ives took a breath that caught at the end.

"Add my authorization."

Sael looked up.

He had not made himself cleaner. The future appetite of Canticle command had
not vanished. This could still become evidence used as blade, route claim,
speech, indictment, banner. It could still keep Lio working long after mercy
should have asked whether work had ended.

But a first record was a kind of threshold.

Thresholds mattered.

"Write it yourself," she said.

He accepted the correction.

Sael turned the ledger toward him.

His hand shook harder with the pen than it had on the route board. That was
ordinary too. The body often waited for survival before announcing its cost.

He wrote:

Authorized: Captain Ives Mar, Pell recovery auxiliary.

First-ship anchor: lost.

He stopped there.

Sael felt the second line more than read it.

He had not needed to include it. A Canticle report would not require a damaged
personal anchor in the authorization. He had written it because the record that
protected Lio from use could not begin with a man pretending to be whole.

The ink dried.

The page held its curve.

Not accusation. Not forgiveness. Weight.

Ives returned the pen to Rin.

"Aven," he said, "lock the first record under delayed transmission. No automatic
burst."

Aven hesitated. "Command will ask why."

"They will ask many things."

"Captain."

"Do it."

Aven did.

No salute. No speech. Their obedience was tired and exact, and therefore more
costly than fervor.

The Pell moved under them.

Not into a corridor. Not into triumph. Into damaged space with ordinary engines
and a failing pump and a sealed witness that had survived every person who
wanted to make mercy, proof, route, or revenge out of it.

Sael closed the ledger.

The cover did not lie flat now.

She held it in both hands and felt the first heavy thing the new record had
made.

There should have been a rite for this. Not a Synod release rite, not Canticle
recovery procedure, not a tribunal opening, not a speech over the dead. A rite
for proof that arrived too late to save anyone it named and too early to be
trusted with the living. A way to say: this is grief before it is evidence.

No such rite existed.

So Sael sat on the deck of the damaged Pell, beside a sealed black casing, under
stars that remembered themselves without asking her permission, and kept the
ledger closed with her palms.

She did not release Lio.

She did not claim Lio.

She did not resolve the Narthex, or Veyr, or Tovan, or Ilet, or the rooms paid
behind her in walls and thresholds.

She remembered.

The galaxy had something it would call proof now.

Sael had once believed proof would make a lie lighter. It did not. Proof was a
mass before it was justice, and sometimes never became justice at all. It
entered the room and asked every person nearby what they would spend to hold it
honestly.

The Pell carried that mass forward: one sealed witness memory, one former Synod
acolyte, one Canticle captain with a lost first ship, and a ledger whose first
page would not lie flat.

Outside, the stars held their old weight.

Inside, no one spoke for a while.

That was the mourning they had.
