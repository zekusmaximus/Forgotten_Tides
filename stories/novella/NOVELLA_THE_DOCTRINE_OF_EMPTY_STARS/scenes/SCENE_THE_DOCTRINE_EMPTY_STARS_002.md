The outer threshold failed without drama.

No bell sounded. No shutter dropped. The Narthex did not believe in alarms that
taught fear how to gather. It gave one small correction through the floor, a
tilt so slight Sael first mistook it for the heaviness in her own hand.

She was halfway between the release chapel and the lower ledger cells, carrying
the sealed folio against her ribs. The folio held the morning's work: custody
marks, burden class, donor phrase, one name written where no name belonged. Lio
did not press through the cover. Ink was not memory, not fully. Still, the folio
had made her sleeve hang wrong.

Another correction passed underfoot.

This time the floor did not tilt. It considered tilting. Sael felt the thought
of it travel through the soles of her slippers and stop before becoming angle.
The passage lights dimmed to their maintenance gray, then brightened by less than
a breath.

From the far end of the corridor, Tovan called, "Outer mark."

He did not shout. In the Narthex, volume was a kind of architecture. Too much of
it taught rooms to expect crisis.

Sael slid the folio into the wall lock beside the ledger cells. The lock accepted
it reluctantly. For one bad moment the slot remained open around the leather, a
mouth uncertain whether it was meant to swallow or speak. Then the inner catch
remembered itself and closed.

She pressed two fingers to the plate until the witness seal cooled under her
touch.

Lio, the ledger held.

Not hers to carry. Already carried.

She went toward the outer mark.

The corridor grew plainer as she descended. The inner Narthex still allowed small
human concessions: a basin at hand height, a worn rail where fasting acolytes
steadied themselves, the faint oil trace of hinges that had served bodies for
years. Near the exterior threshold even those kindnesses thinned. Walls met floor
without molding. Doors were pressure-lines only, their frames kept shallow so the
station would not become fond of division.

The Black Horizon leaned there.

Sael had no better word for it. Pressure did not come from one direction. It was
not wind or vacuum or gravity in any clean sense. It was the universe's urge to
spend less memory on the fact of walls. It arrived as quiet subtraction. Edges
softened. Directions became negotiable. A passage that had always led outward
began to wonder whether out was a burden worth maintaining.

Tovan and Ilet were already at the threshold.

Tovan had one palm flat against the left reveal, his brow nearly touching the
wall. He was broad through the shoulders from years of cradle work, but the
Horizon made him look drawn on thin paper. Ilet knelt at the floor-line with her
seal board across her knees, lips moving around numbers she was not allowed to
write down.

Between them, the door stood open and closed.

That was the only way Sael could understand it at first. The threshold's black
inner surface reflected the corridor behind her. It also showed the outer service
lock beyond, a narrow tube ribbed with frost. Between those two images lay a
third: not darkness, but the absence of an agreed place for darkness to be.

"How long?" Sael asked.

"Three pulls since the first drift," Ilet said.

Tovan kept his hand on the reveal. "It still takes pressure. It does not take
meaning."

Sael stepped close enough to feel the threshold against her teeth. The door knew
mass. If an acolyte crossed, sensors would wake. If the outer lock breached, the
bulkhead would resist. Matter still had a grip on matter.

But function had thinned.

A door was not only a slab and a hinge. It was an agreement: this side and that
side, admitted and refused, shelter and exposure. The threshold had lost the
agreement. It showed the service lock because light reached it. It showed the
corridor because polish held it. It showed the third thing because the Black
Horizon had found a place where purpose had been kept too light.

Sael had copied the maintenance doctrine as a novice:

A threshold may remember function. It may not remember ceremony.

No founding passage. No first arrival. No names of those who had entered for
release. A door in the Narthex was permitted to know opening, closing, pressure,
seal. Nothing more. The rule had seemed severe until the first time Sael watched
a memorial arch in a ruined station fold around its own importance and crush the
hall it had meant to dignify.

"Can it be let go?" Ilet asked.

She did not mean abandon the station. She meant permit the threshold to release
its function and seal the outer narthex behind a blank wall. It would save weight.
It would cost access, evacuation, repair, the modest dignity of choosing where
one could stand.

Tovan answered before Sael could. "There are three penitents in outer silence."

Ilet closed her eyes once.

The outer silence cells held those preparing for release rites who had asked to
be kept away from human voice. Voluntary burdens, the records said. People who
wanted the last hours before erasure to contain no pleas from the living. Sael
had served water through those walls. She had never asked their names.

"Then we cannot set it down," Ilet said.

No one praised the mercy of that. It was only work.

Sael drew her focus into the trained sequence. Name. Station. Office. Doctrine.

Sael Orro.

Narthex of Low Mercy.

Keeper of release ledgers.

A burden set down is not betrayal.

The fourth line answered with a hitch. Not a refusal. A small snag, as if some
unfiled thing had caught in the words. She kept her face still.

Tovan noticed anyway. "You came from the chapel."

"Yes."

"Heavy casing?"

"Heavy enough."

He did not ask more. Tovan's gifts were practical and sometimes holy. He could
leave a wound covered when uncovering it would not help the repair.

Ilet rose. Her seal board gave a thin crack as the floor released its grip on the
edge. "We recite permitted structure."

Sael looked at the threshold. "It may not hold."

"It does not need to hold forever," Ilet said.

That, too, was doctrine. Forever was one of the great cruelties. Enough was the
kinder measure, if one had the courage to stop at it.

They took their marks.

Tovan remained at the left reveal, weight through his arm, body offered as a
measure of inside. Ilet stood at the right, seal board against her sternum. Sael
knelt at the floor-line and set both hands on the cold strip where the threshold
should have known bottom from passage.

The strip was losing temperature. Not cooling. Forgetting heat as a relevant
property.

Sael began, because ledger-keepers were trained to bind only what had been
authorized.

"This is the outer threshold of the Narthex of Low Mercy."

The words entered the metal. Not strongly. They made a faint density beneath her
palms, a remembered grain.

Tovan followed. "It opens for service."

Ilet: "It closes for shelter."

Sael: "It distinguishes the station from the Horizon."

The third image between open and closed tightened. A line appeared through it,
vertical and trembling.

Tovan: "It does not keep names."

Ilet: "It does not keep grief."

Sael's turn came. Her mouth shaped the required phrase.

"It remembers function only."

Lio moved behind her knuckles.

Not sound. Not vision. Only the old pressure of a small palm striking a bulkhead
from inside someone else's terror. Sael held the phrase steady until it left her
clean.

The threshold answered. The black inner surface drew inward by the width of a
fingernail. Frost in the service lock sharpened into ribs. Behind Sael, the
corridor reflection lost a little of its depth.

"Again," Tovan said.

His voice had roughened. The reveal was taking weight through his bones.

They recited again.

This is the outer threshold of the Narthex of Low Mercy.

It opens for service.

It closes for shelter.

It distinguishes the station from the Horizon.

It does not keep names.

It does not keep grief.

It remembers function only.

The door thickened. Sael felt the station gather itself around the words, not
proudly, not hungrily, but with the weary obedience of a body asked to rise from
fasting. The wall seams darkened. The pressure-line at the floor grew definite
enough to cast a shadow.

Then the Horizon pushed back.

The third image widened.

For one instant Sael saw through it.

Not far. The Narthex's memory shadow lay close around the station, a region of
deliberate unimportance where search patterns slid away and attention found
nothing to hold. Beyond that shadow, the Black Horizon opened without scenery.
No stars that the eye could trust. No fixed dark. Only distance relieved of its
duty to mean.

Something flashed there.

Three pulses. A pause. Three pulses.

The rhythm was too disciplined for drift and too spare for prayer.

Ilet's recitation stopped on an inhale.

Tovan did not look away from the reveal. "Say the line."

The pulses came again.

Sael knew the shape from old caution tablets, from Synod lessons that never
raised their voice when teaching danger. Canticle search discipline favored
minimal witness: brief light, tight interval, no mournful call into the dark.
They believed a beacon should ask a question without making the universe carry a
speech.

Three pulses. A pause. Three pulses.

Outside the station's memory shadow.

Not at the door. Not yet.

Ilet swallowed. "If we strengthen the threshold, the shadow thins."

"If we do not," Tovan said, "the threshold opens without opening."

Sael kept her hands on the floor-line. The cold had passed through her gloves.
Her fingertips felt far away, as if they belonged to the strip and the strip had
not decided whether fingers were useful.

The correct choice existed. That did not make it clean.

To leave the Narthex light was to keep it hidden. Lightness had protected them
for years in a region where pursuit had to remember what it pursued. The Canticle
Fleet could not easily find a station that refused most of its own story.

To make the threshold remember was to give the station an edge. Edges caught
attention. Attention drew search. Search carried engines, orders, weapons, proof.

But behind the threshold were three penitents in outer silence, and all the
acolytes who had fed them water without names, and the release chapel with its
plain benches, and the ledger cell where Lio had become four letters of weight.
The Narthex was not innocent. It was also not empty.

Sael thought of Veyr's teaching: mercy is not the worship of less; it is the
refusal to make suffering permanent.

A threshold that forgot itself would make suffering brief. Brief was not always
merciful.

She said the first line before either of the others could ask who had chosen.

"This is the outer threshold of the Narthex of Low Mercy."

Tovan closed his eyes in relief or pain. "It opens for service."

Ilet joined a beat late. "It closes for shelter."

The station took the words.

Not merely the threshold this time. The corridor behind Sael stiffened. The
shallow door-frames along the passage deepened by a visible fraction, remembering
that they had been frames before doctrine had thinned them. Somewhere overhead, a
rail settled into its brackets. The Narthex became more itself.

Sael felt it as weight in her knees.

"It distinguishes the station from the Horizon," she said.

The third image narrowed to a wound.

Tovan: "It does not keep names."

Ilet: "It does not keep grief."

Sael's mouth dried. The final phrase waited. If she spoke it, the threshold
would draw the station's permitted memory into one hard enough line to close.
Permitted memory. Function. Shelter. Service. No names.

Lio.

She did not say the name. She did not release it either.

"It remembers function only."

The door closed.

It did so incompletely. The surface sealed from top to bottom, but the center
remained faintly translucent, a bruise of elsewhere held inside black glass. The
floor-line warmed by a degree. Sael's fingers returned to her as separate things.

Tovan lowered his hand from the reveal. The skin of his palm had gone pale where
the metal had borrowed pressure from him.

"Partial," Ilet said.

"Enough for the outer cells?"

Ilet checked her board. "Enough for bodies. Not for memory bleed."

Enough, Sael thought, had become a narrower word.

The threshold gave one soft knock from inside itself. Then another. Not a hand.
Not Lio. Only the service lock equalizing around a door that had remembered too
quickly and not well.

Sael stood carefully. Her knees objected to the added station-weight. Around
them, the corridor held its new definition with visible effort. Edges were
cleaner. The air felt less forgiving.

Through the bruise at the center of the door, the beacon flashed again.

Three pulses. Pause. Three.

Closer, perhaps. Or only easier to see now that the Narthex had given itself an
outline.

Ilet bowed her head. "Canticle."

No one corrected her. No one needed to.

Tovan rubbed his borrowed palm with his thumb. "Report to the elder?"

"Yes," Sael said.

Her voice sounded calm because the station had taught calm into the walls and she
had learned from walls as much as from people.

She looked once more at the door. The repair would fail. Not now, not in the next
pull, but soon. The Horizon had found the weakness. The threshold could be
reminded; it could not be made young. Their recitation had not solved decay. It
had purchased shelter with attention.

Outside, the beacon flashed a fourth time.

This time, after the second set of pulses, another light answered it from farther
out. Brief. Precise. Waiting.

The Narthex held still around Sael, heavier than it had been that morning.

In the ledger cell behind her, a child's name waited in the dark with all the
other things the station was not supposed to keep. Ahead, beyond the repaired
door, someone had begun asking the Black Horizon where a hidden mercy might be
found.
