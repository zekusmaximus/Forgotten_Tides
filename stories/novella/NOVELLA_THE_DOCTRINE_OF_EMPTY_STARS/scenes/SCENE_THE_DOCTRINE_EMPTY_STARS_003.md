The release service began with the benches remembering how to hold bodies.

They unfolded from the chapel walls one at a time, plain-backed, narrow, without
creak or ceremony. Each had been taught the same mercy as the rest of the
Narthex: serve, then set down service. By the time the penitents entered, no
bench would keep the warmth of a hip or the tremor of a hand longer than the rite
required.

Sael watched from the ledger stand with the sealed folio beneath her left palm.

It had been two days since she wrote Lio into a line that did not want a name.
The folio should have slept in the lower cells until Veyr decided whether the
retention petition had failed or never existed. Instead it lay beside the active
rite ledger, black clasp closed, witness seal intact. Not in the release ring.
Not outside the chapel. A held burden, awaiting judgment, allowed near the rite
because hidden weight distorted rooms more dangerously than visible weight.

That was Veyr's ruling.

Sael had written it in the provisional column: presence permitted; release
withheld.

The words were correct. They did not lighten anything.

Around her, the service gathered itself without hurry. Tovan stood at the left
aisle, fasting wrap drawn tight across his broad chest. Ilet moved between the
benches with a cup of plain water, offering it to each penitent before they took
their places. Some accepted. Some touched the rim and declined. None gave names.
The rite did not require them.

There were nine penitents today, plus three in outer silence whose water had been
passed through the cell walls before first dimming. Voluntary burdens, the
records said. People tired of being made useful by grief. People whose families
had petitioned, pleaded, argued, or finally gone still. People who had come to
the Synod because the wider galaxy preserved everything it could and called the
end of carrying a failure of love.

Sael believed some of them had chosen freely.

She needed that belief to remain true.

At the front of the chapel, Veyr Anik stood within the chalk release ring. The
ring held seven small reliquaries today, none heavier than a closed fist. A last
meal with no mouth left to taste it. A father's voice copied too many times by a
daughter who could no longer sleep. The pattern of an old habitation dome under
rain, preserved after the dome collapsed and the survivors began mistaking every
storm for warning.

The Synod did not laugh at such burdens. It did not hurry them. Each reliquary
had been cleaned, weighed by hand, and set on a plain cloth. Each donor phrase
had been copied without ornament.

Veyr lifted both hands from his sleeves.

"What is offered may refuse spectacle," he said.

The chapel took the sentence into its walls. Not strongly. A rite did not ask a
room to become proud of itself.

The penitents answered, voices low and uneven. "What is carried may be set down."

Sael wrote the response mark in the rite ledger. Her stylus moved cleanly until
the folio under her other hand shifted by a fraction.

Not moved. Settled.

Lio, ink said beneath leather and seal.

She kept her palm still. Name. Station. Office. Doctrine.

Sael Orro.

Narthex of Low Mercy.

Keeper of release ledgers.

A burden set down is not betrayal.

The fourth anchor answered with its familiar hitch. She did not correct it. A
false smoothness was still false.

Veyr began the opening inventory. His voice was soft enough that the penitents
had to lean toward it, and by leaning made themselves part of the rite.

"Memory of shelter after shelter has ended."

Ilet touched the first reliquary.

"Memory of a voice after consent to silence."

Tovan touched the second.

"Memory of fear retained beyond danger."

At the third phrase, the repaired outer threshold knocked somewhere below the
chapel.

No one turned. The Narthex had been making small sounds since the repair. A rail
remembering its bracket. A door frame trying to keep an edge the Horizon wanted
to soften. The station had become more itself, and like any fasting body asked
for strength, it complained in private places.

Veyr did not pause.

"Memory of duty after duty has released the living."

Sael marked the line. Her hand remained steady because steadiness was a practice,
not a feeling.

Another knock came.

This one had direction.

It traveled through the floor from the side service passage behind the ledger
stand, up through Sael's knees and into the folio clasp. The clasp cooled beneath
her palm. The active reliquaries in the chalk ring trembled, each one no more
than the width of breath.

Veyr's eyes moved to Sael.

Not alarm. Question.

Sael listened with the part of her training that knew doors by pressure and
records by weight. The sound was not the outer threshold. Too close. Too narrow.
It came from the maintenance seam behind the west wall, a service gap kept thin
enough that most days the station almost forgot it existed. Waste heat, seal
access, old repair lines. No names. No history. A seam, not a room.

After the threshold repair, the Narthex had been asked to remember its edges.
Edges had consequences.

Veyr lowered one hand, barely. Continue, the gesture said, unless harm enters.

The next reliquary waited in the ring.

Sael inclined her head and stepped back from the ledger stand. Tovan saw and
moved half a pace toward her, but she touched two fingers to the air: hold the
aisle. He stopped. Acolytes in a rite did not cross each other's work without
need.

The penitents kept their eyes down. That was kindness, or discipline, or fear
trained into the shape of kindness. Sael could not tell. She had learned not to
need the difference during service.

She slipped through the narrow side door behind the ledger stand.

The west service passage had been designed to discourage attention. No lamp
bracket, only a gray wash from the chapel's spill-light. No rail. No carved
threshold. Its floor met the wall in a shallow curve so dust would not gather
into a line and teach the eye to follow. The maintenance seam lay five paces in,
behind a pressure panel marked only by function.

Heat exchange. Seal access. Do not retain.

The panel breathed inward.

Sael stopped.

Metal did not breathe unless something had convinced it breath was relevant. The
panel drew back again, then returned, slow and irregular. Around its edge, frost
formed and erased itself. A hairline opened along the seam.

From inside came a human voice, flattened by a helmet speaker.

"Anchor one."

Another voice answered, nearer to breaking. "Name."

"Anchor one."

"Name."

"Say it."

Silence.

The panel bent outward.

Sael had one instant to decide whether to call for Tovan.

If she called, the rite would hear. Penitents in release contact would turn
toward the sound. Veyr would stop the inventory. Tovan would come with his hands
empty because no one carried weapons inside the chapel. Whoever stood behind the
panel might not know that. Fear would acquire architecture. The room would learn
a new function.

The panel gave way without a crash.

It folded at one corner and sagged open. A gloved hand slid through, fingers
spread against the passage floor. The glove was gray under black dust, its seal
charms stuttering with low blue ticks. A shoulder followed. Then a helmeted head
bowed so hard against the floor that Sael heard the visor strike.

Not an invader.

A body, barely keeping its own outline.

The first Canticle officer pulled himself through the seam and rolled onto his
side. His suit had been scored by cold and attention. Patches of its surface
looked less damaged than unconvinced, as if the Black Horizon had argued with
the material about whether armor needed to be continuous. His breathing came in
measured counts that failed every fourth number.

Behind him, another figure shoved a smaller suited body through the opening. The
smaller one landed badly, made no sound, and clutched both hands to the front of
their helmet as if holding in a thought.

The third came last.

He did not come smoothly. He braced one forearm on the torn panel, checked the
passage before committing his weight, and only then stepped through. That
discipline, more than the insignia half-burned on his shoulder, told Sael who led
them.

Canticle Fleet.

The man stood with the careful stillness of someone who knew his body might
misreport the floor. He was not large. His suit made him angular rather than
imposing, all worn seals and field repairs. The face behind the visor was pale
with corridor sickness. Blood had dried beneath one nostril in a straight dark
line. His eyes found Sael, then the door behind her, then the ledger stylus at
her belt, arranging facts before fear could arrange them for him.

He lifted one hand slowly.

Empty.

Sael did not step back. The passage was too narrow to give ground without
offering direction toward the chapel.

"You are inside a release rite," she said.

Her voice sounded like the walls: quiet, functional, not yet afraid.

The Canticle officer swallowed. The motion shifted the loose collar seal at his
throat.

"Ives Mar," he said. "Canticle recovery. We need the memory."

The first officer on the floor laughed once, a dry sound without amusement. "He
means hello."

Ives did not look at him. "Anchor check."

The first officer pressed two fingers to his own sternum. "Name. Ship. Task.
Return."

"Name," Ives said.

The officer closed his eyes. His mouth worked around emptiness.

Ives's jaw tightened, but his voice stayed level. "Skip. Ship."

"Canticle..." The officer breathed hard. "Canticle vessel. Recovery assignment."

"Task."

"Preserve evidence."

"Return."

No answer came.

Sael felt the service continuing through the wall. Veyr's voice, low and clear.
The penitents' response. The chapel had not stopped. Not yet.

The smaller Canticle body pressed both palms harder to the helmet. "There are
too many edges here."

"There are exactly as many as necessary," Sael said.

Ives looked at her again. His gaze sharpened around the sentence. Exhausted, yes.
Under-anchored, yes. Not clumsy.

"Your threshold repair made you visible," he said.

Sael's hand closed around the stylus at her belt before she chose the motion.
Not a weapon. A habit of record. "You followed the beacon."

"We followed what answered it."

Another knock passed through the station, deeper than the first. The seam behind
them flexed. Cold pushed around the torn panel and brought a taste of empty metal
into the passage.

The first officer rolled to one knee and reached for the sidearm clipped beneath
his chest plate.

Sael stepped between him and the chapel door.

It was not brave. There was nowhere else for her to stand.

"No weapons in the rite passage," she said.

The officer's hand kept moving.

Ives caught his wrist. Not hard, because hard would have become a struggle. He
closed his glove around the joint and held until the other man looked at him.

"We did not come here to shoot penitents," Ives said.

"We came here to get out."

"We came here to finish the recovery."

"Same thing, if we fail."

Ives did not deny it. That restraint touched Sael more sharply than denial would
have. A lying man might have offered noble purpose. A frightened competent one
kept the ugly arithmetic visible.

Through the wall, Veyr said, "What is offered may refuse witness."

The penitents answered, "What is witnessed may refuse use."

Ives went still.

Not because of the doctrine. His head turned toward the chapel with the sudden
small precision of a person hearing his own name in a crowd.

Sael saw the change and hated it before she understood it.

The sealed folio lay on the ledger stand beyond the side door. The preserved
evacuation memory rested beside it in its black casing, outside the chalk ring
but inside the rite's pressure. It had been quiet through the opening inventory.
Held burdens sometimes quieted in the presence of release, the way a fevered
body quieted when water entered the room.

Now it answered the breach.

A low thrum crossed the chapel wall.

The sound came through stone and seal, shallow enough that a penitent might think
it part of the rite, deep enough that Sael's knuckles remembered the child's palm
against the bulkhead.

Ives put one hand to the passage wall.

"No," he said.

The word had no triumph in it. Only recognition, and underneath that a fatigue
so old it might have been part of his uniform.

Sael said, "You will lower your voice."

He did. That, too, told her something.

"That casing," he said.

"You have no standing here."

"No. I have orders telling me I don't." He drew a breath through his teeth. "Had
orders. Past tense, if command still knows what tense means."

Sael should have opened the chapel door and called Veyr. She should have let the
elder's calm enter the passage before these broken people made their fear into
action. She should have trusted the rite to hold.

Instead she listened.

Ives kept his palm to the wall. The reliquary thrum moved through him visibly.
Not a seizure. A trained body receiving bad evidence.

"We had three fragments from a failed evacuation," he said. "A clipped warning
chime. Passenger count by number, not name. A hand striking bulkhead metal near
the recorder. Then the recovery order closed."

Sael's mouth dried.

The passage seemed to narrow by a fraction, but it was only her attention drawing
hard lines around the facts.

"Many evacuations carry such things," she said.

"Yes."

He did not make the answer easy for either of them.

"This one has the same outer pattern," he said. "Same pressure cadence. Same
fear kept disciplined too long." His eyes flicked to the side door. "And a name
someone tried not to let become a name."

Lio moved beneath Sael's knuckles though the folio was no longer under her hand.

The first officer stared at Ives. "You found it?"

"Maybe." Ives took his palm from the wall. His glove left a fogged print that
began to fade at once. "Maybe we found a thing someone wants released before it
can answer."

"You accuse us inside a release service," Sael said.

The sentence came out colder than she expected. Good. Cold could be useful.

Ives looked back at her. "I am asking where you keep evidence from dead people
who did not get to finish leaving."

"You are asking with a breach behind you and a weapon at your man's chest."

The officer on his knee flinched as if the sidearm had become visible only after
she named it.

Ives released his wrist slowly. "Unclip it."

"Captain."

"Unclip it and set it on the floor."

For a moment no one moved. The rite continued beyond the door. Seven reliquaries
in chalk. Nine penitents seated on benches that would not remember them. Veyr's
voice making mercy sound almost possible.

The officer set the sidearm down.

It touched the floor with a small, dense click.

The passage disliked the sound. Sael felt it try to keep the shape of the metal,
the threat of it, the new fact of armed intention inside a wall that had never
been asked to carry such a thing. She crouched and slid the weapon toward the
torn maintenance panel, away from the chapel door.

"All of them," she said.

Ives unfastened his own sidearm without argument. It took him two tries. His
right hand shook on the second latch, and he stopped, pressed his thumb to the
base of each finger in sequence, and tried again.

Anchor checks, Sael thought. Not enough of them answering.

The smaller Canticle survivor had no weapon. They had begun whispering a word
that might have been a name or might have been the memory of one. Sael could not
tell. She did not ask. A name requested in such a state became a hook.

When both weapons lay by the seam, Ives said, "Now tell me who presides."

"Elder Veyr Anik."

Recognition did not cross his face. Only calculation. "Will he stop the rite?"

"If harm enters."

"Has harm not entered?"

Sael looked at the folded panel, the frost, the two weapons, the man on his knee
who could not complete his own anchor, the smaller survivor holding their helmet
as if it contained a skull made of water.

Then she looked at Ives.

"Not yet," she said.

It was the most dangerous truth available.

The side door opened behind her.

Sael turned before fear could move her too quickly. Tovan stood in the gap, one
hand on the frame, the other empty at his side. He saw Sael first. Then Ives.
Then the collapsed panel. Then the weapons on the floor.

His face did not change. Tovan's mercy had always been practical; his alarm was
the same.

"Sael," he said.

Behind him, Veyr's voice continued. If the elder had sent Tovan, he had done so
without interrupting the service. If Tovan had come on his own, he had chosen
silence over protocol for at least three breaths.

"Maintenance breach," Sael said.

Tovan's gaze moved to Ives's insignia.

"Canticle breach," he said.

The first officer on the floor reached toward the seam where the weapons lay.

Ives said, "Leave them."

Tovan heard the command. Sael saw him hear the discipline inside it.

From the chapel came a shift in the penitents' voices. They had reached the
release response. In another few breaths, Veyr would open the first reliquary to
the ring. During that contact, interruption could scar the memory surface. A
startled penitent could clutch a burden back into themselves. An acolyte crossing
the chalk carelessly could draw all seven offerings into one another, grief
braided by panic.

Sael knew this. Tovan knew it. Ives did not, but he understood enough of her face
to wait.

Tovan said, "We must report."

"Yes," Sael said.

The correct answer. The answer that did not decide when.

He looked at her then, fully. "Now."

Sael listened to the chapel.

Veyr: "Memory may thin without contempt."

Penitents: "Memory may end without betrayal."

The words passed through the wall and entered the service passage with terrible
gentleness.

If Tovan called, the rite would break. If Ives resisted, Tovan would stop him
with his body. If the Canticle officer reached the weapon, Sael would be too far
from the seam. If Veyr entered, he would bring calm, but calm could not make a
fired weapon unfire or an interrupted release clean again.

Sael thought of the outer threshold, open and closed. A door was an agreement.
This side and that side. Admitted and refused. Shelter and exposure.

The passage needed a door.

"After the first release," she said.

Tovan's jaw tightened. "That is concealment."

"It is containment."

"That is a better word for the same weight."

He was right. The Synod had trained them both too well for Sael to hide inside
language.

"Then I will carry it," she said.

Tovan looked past her to the Canticle survivors. "Why?"

Not accusation. Worse. He wanted the moral arithmetic.

Sael could have said because of the child. Because the captain had recognized
the thrum. Because Lio was no longer only a sound inside a casing but a point
where two records touched. All of that was true, and none of it was why she made
the next decision.

"Because there are nine penitents in active release," she said. "Because there
are seven open burdens in the ring. Because if fear enters now, it will not leave
by the door."

Tovan closed his eyes once.

The rite moved into the release phrase.

Sael turned to Ives. "Your people will go into the seal recess. You will sit
with your hands visible. You will not speak toward the chapel. You will not touch
the casing. If any of you reaches for a weapon, I will report with my full voice
and let the rite bear the damage."

The first officer said, "We are not prisoners."

Ives said, "We are breathing because she has not shouted."

The officer's mouth shut.

Sael opened the seal recess beside the maintenance seam. It was hardly a room,
only a kneeling hollow for panel work, deep enough for three bodies if none of
them objected to pain. The Narthex had kept it blank. No stored tools. No chalk
marks. No memory except the pressure of occasional hands.

"In," she said.

The first officer helped the smaller survivor crawl inside. Ives waited until
they were placed, then collected one weapon between two fingers and passed it to
Sael grip-first.

She did not take it.

"On the floor," she said.

He set it down.

"You do not trust me," he said.

"You entered through a wound."

"Your station made one."

"Yes."

The answer seemed to cost him more than an argument would have. He lowered
himself into the recess last, back against the blank wall, hands open on his
knees. Up close, without the passage between them, Sael could see how close he
was to losing coherence. His outline did not blur. Nothing so theatrical. But
attention slid from him at the edges. The eye wanted to look away and then
corrected itself, embarrassed.

Under-anchored.

Half-sick from the Horizon and still sorting exits, lines of fire, evidence,
orders. Competence worn past comfort.

"The memory," he said quietly. "Do not release it."

Sael held the recess door.

"That is not yours to command."

"No."

He looked toward the chapel wall again.

"It may not be yours either."

The sentence struck where doctrine had already loosened.

For a moment Sael hated him. Not because he was wrong. Because he had no right
to arrive broken through a maintenance seam and place his unclean certainty on a
burden she had been trying to weigh honestly. Because he was Canticle, and the
Canticle Fleet preserved grief until institutions could build walls with it.
Because his people had orders too, and orders could close as easily as ledgers.

Because he had heard the same thrum.

She shut the recess door.

It did not seal cleanly. The panel had been bent by the breach, and the hollow
had never been asked to hide living weight. Sael pressed both palms against it
and gave the Narthex a permitted sentence.

"This recess holds maintenance until service ends."

The door accepted the lie by function. Not by innocence.

Its edge darkened. The hollow behind it grew quiet enough that the chapel would
not hear breath unless the breath became panic.

Tovan looked at Sael's hands on the panel.

"You should not have said holds," he murmured.

She knew. Holds was a dangerous verb in a station built for release. It gathered
mass.

Through the wall, the first reliquary opened.

The effect was not light. Light would have been too easy. The chapel exhaled a
weight it had borrowed. The floor lifted by the smallest possible measure. A
penitent sobbed once, not in grief alone but in the astonishment of not being
asked to keep gripping.

For that breath, Sael remembered why she had given her life to this place.

Then Ives Mar coughed behind the recess door, and the sound stopped before it
became audible, bitten down by discipline or by fear.

Tovan said, "Veyr will know."

"Yes."

"Soon."

"Yes."

"And if the Canticle captain is right?"

Sael took her hands from the panel. The edges of her palms had gone pale where
the door borrowed pressure from them. "He has not proven right."

"No."

"He has proven connected."

Tovan glanced toward the chapel. "That may be worse."

Veyr began the second release. His voice came through the wall unchanged,
patient, humane, dangerous in the way a clean blade was dangerous because it
believed in cutting only what had to be cut.

Sael returned to the ledger stand before anyone in the chapel could mark her
absence as more than service work.

The folio waited beneath the black clasp. The preserved evacuation casing rested
beside it, dust gathered along one edge in a skirt fine enough to be mistaken for
shadow. In the chalk ring, the second small reliquary trembled toward ending. On
the benches, nine penitents leaned toward less.

Veyr did not look at Sael when she resumed her place.

That was his mercy, perhaps. Or his warning.

She set the stylus to the rite ledger and marked the first release complete.

Her hand did not shake.

Behind the west wall, three Canticle survivors breathed inside a recess the
station had not known how to hide that morning. In the ledger cell of her own
mind, Lio's name remained four letters and a palm against metal. Before her, the
rite continued doing real kindness under a roof that had been breached.

Sael wrote the second release mark.

The ink dried darker than it should have.
