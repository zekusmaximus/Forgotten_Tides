# THE ANCHORLESS TIDE

**I. The Ping**

The *Quiet Drift* registered the buoy at the frayed edge of the sensor web— a faint heartbeat in the static of the Forgotten Tides. Kael Voss leaned forward in the pilot cradle, the rig’s harness biting into his shoulders. Freelancers didn’t rate full anchor drives. Just patched harnesses and enough neural dampers to keep the void from eating your name.

“Signature unknown,” the ship said, voice flat as recycled air. “Memory envelope at thirty-seven percent integrity. Decaying fast.”

Kael knew the pattern. Relics from the early corridor runs. Colonies that launched before the Lattice stabilized, before memory-gravity became doctrine. Things the big crews wrote off as acceptable loss.

“Intercept course,” he said. “Slow approach. No grapples until I say.”

The engines murmured assent. Outside the viewports, the Tides swirled—great slow currents of half-remembered starlight and conceptual flotsam. Here, reality was negotiable.

**II. The Buoy**

It was beautiful in the way wrecks are: a sphere of blackened alloy studded with crystal growths that glowed the color of old bruises. As the *Quiet Drift* matched velocity, the first wave hit.

Not water. Not vacuum. A tide of *presence*.

A woman singing a lullaby in a language Kael’s implants couldn’t parse. The smell of wet soil after rain on a planet he’d never visited. Laughter that cut off mid-breath. Then the ache of erasure—entire family trees redacted in real time, names dissolving like salt in the current.

Kael’s remaining anchors—two personal, one civic—flared hot against his skull. He jacked the salvage interface anyway.

The buoy opened to him. Thousands of lives, frozen mid-sentence. A colony called Ebbhaven, swallowed by some catastrophe decades ago. Their last transmission had been a plea for anchor support that never came.

“Too much volume,” the ship warned. “Conceptual shear rising.”

Kael’s vision tunneled. He saw children pressing hands to viewport glass as their world’s orbit decayed. He felt the precise moment a father decided to forget his daughter’s face so the rest of the crew could survive the journey.

**III. The Ledger**

He unclipped and floated to the auxiliary console. The rig’s leads trailed behind him like kelp. One wrong move and the tide would pull him under—replace his memories with theirs until he became just another echo.

The buoy’s core pulsed faster. It wanted an anchor. Any anchor.

Kael had one left that was truly his: the memory of his mother’s hands teaching him to tie a sailor’s knot on a station dock, years before the recruiters took him. The last unspent piece of home.

He could feed it to the buoy. Stabilize the envelope, log the data, sell the salvage to the Archivists. Live another month.

Or he could let it go.

The tide pressed against the hull, whispering promises of belonging. Of not being alone in the drift.

Kael closed his eyes. “Ship. Open a wide-band release valve on my mark.”

“You’ll lose the salvage bounty.”

“I know.”

**IV. The Release**

He jacked in one last time and fed the buoy not an anchor, but permission.

*Remember yourselves,* he thought. *Then let go.*

The drive thrummed. The buoy’s crystal growths flared brilliant blue, then shattered outward in a silent explosion of light. Memories flooded the Tides—not contained, but liberated. Scattered like seeds into the greater current.

Some would dissolve. Some would catch on other buoys, other salvagers, other minds centuries from now.

The *Quiet Drift* rocked once, then steadied.

Kael unjacked. The space where his mother’s knot memory had lived was smooth and empty. No ache. Just... absence.

He smiled, small and tired.

“Some tides aren’t meant to be saved,” he told the ship. “They’re meant to teach the rest of us how to float.”

**V. Forward**

The buoy drifted away, dark and silent at last.

Kael reset the course for the next unmarked signal on the drift charts. No home to return to. No anchors left to weigh him down.

The Forgotten Tides rolled on around him—vast, indifferent, and full of stories waiting for someone brave enough to forget just enough to keep moving.

He checked his rig one last time.

“Next ping,” he said.

And the *Quiet Drift* carried him deeper.
