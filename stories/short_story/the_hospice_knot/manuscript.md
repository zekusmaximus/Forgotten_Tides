THE HOSPICE KNOT

The hospice chamber held no rails, only low stone ledges worn by decades of hands seeking purchase. Kael Torren sat with his back to the wall, sensorium ring dark on his brow. His last three anchors had thinned to threads.

Lira Voss knelt opposite, reliquary tray between them. “Name the knots,” she said.

“Third teacher. First corridor. Mother’s voice.” His reply came flat. The third had already begun to shear—syllables sliding out of reach.

She placed a memory stone the size of a thumb beside his knee. “This one holds the choice. Release or hold.”

Kael’s fingers hovered. The stone warmed under the hospice’s constant low chant. Outside, the Lattice Gap’s eddies tugged at the station’s outer skin; inside, the air remembered stillness.

“If I let the last go,” he said, “the corridor won’t answer me again.”

“If you keep it,” Lira answered, “you will forget the difference between self and ship.”

He closed his eyes. The ring flickered once, registering the micro-collapse of identity mass. A single knot unraveled with the sound of distant rain on a roof he could no longer name.

Lira caught the falling ring before it struck stone. “The lightness is real,” she said. “So is the mercy.”

Kael opened his eyes to a cleaner dark. The remaining anchors sat quiet, no longer pulling against the void. He had spent enough to stay human. He had spent enough to stop flying.

She rose. “The next pilot arrives at third pull. Rest now.”

He stayed seated, hands open on his knees, weightless for the first time in years.
