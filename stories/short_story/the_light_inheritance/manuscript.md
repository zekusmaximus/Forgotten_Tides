# The Light Inheritance

## I. The Recitation

Yenu Skather-Ait was sixteen standard years old and had never owned a memory she could not afford to lose. This was not a deficit. This was the discipline her clade had spent four generations learning, ever since the pod-spine of Skather-Ait had first uncoupled from the lattice and chosen the long drift.

She sat at her grandfather's bedside in the small green light of his sleeping-cell, and she recited him to himself.

"You were born on the spine," she said. "Before the uncoupling. You were the third child and the second son. Your mother was Ait-Vren. Your father was Skather-Hol. You learned drive-work at eleven, miniature work at fourteen, and corridor-work at nineteen, which was already late by lattice standards but which the clade considered prudent."

Her grandfather's eyes were closed. His hands lay on the blanket like two folded letters someone had decided not to send. He was eighty-four standard, which on a low-gravity pod was twice that in felt years, and he was at the edge of his last raveling. The pod's medic had said one day. Maybe two. The medic was Drift Clade and did not lie about such things; lying about endings was a lattice habit.

"You worked the corridor between Halix and the lattice for eleven years," Yenu said. "You were good. You were not famous. You miniaturized the third-stage flux throttle that the lattice still uses in their inner-ring couriers, although they do not call it by your name. You came home when your back gave out. You taught miniaturization to twenty-six apprentices over thirty-one years. Sixteen of them are still working. Four of them are dead. Six of them have stopped."

She paused. *Stopped* was the polite Drift Clade word. It meant *gone light* — meant a person had completed their thinning by clade choice and dispersed, by ritual or by drift, into the regions where coherence no longer cared to hold them. It was not the same as death. It was the thing some of her people did *instead of* death, when they felt their weight had become a burden the pod could not bear.

Her grandfather was thinking about stopping. He had not said so. He did not need to.

"You married Ait-Resh in your twenty-eighth year," Yenu went on, more slowly now. "She was a stone-tuner. She trained at Solace for nine years before coming back to the pod. She made the first-watch reliquary that the pod still uses for funerals. She died during the Bleaching, when so many stones soured at once that the markets crashed and three of her own works went with them. The pod could not afford to replace her bench. You did not remarry. You did not want to."

His mouth moved. A correction. She bent close.

"Not did not want to," he said. His voice was thin as paper held to a light. "Could not afford to."

She nodded, took the correction, and folded it into the recitation. Drift Clade recitation was not memorization. It was *negotiation*. The dying corrected the living, and the living carried the correction forward, and the dead remained accurate by the only means a fluid-identity people had ever trusted: by being spoken, often, by someone who had loved them recently.

"Could not afford to," she repeated. "You had three children. My mother was the youngest. She was born on the pod and has never left the pod and will not leave the pod, which the pod elder considers a virtue and you consider a small grief you do not speak about."

A flicker at the corner of his mouth. Not a smile. A pleasure at being read correctly.

"You have one grandchild," Yenu said, and stopped, because she was the grandchild and the recitation always faltered there, the way a person reading aloud falters at their own name.

He opened his eyes. They were very pale, the pale of long low-gravity life, and they were entirely present.

"Yenu Skather-Ait," he said, finishing it for her. "Sixteen standard. Apprenticed too early because her clade apprentices too early. A runner for Hol-Vren's miniaturization shop because there was no other place to put her. Not yet a tuner. Not yet a drive-hand. *Listening* well, which is the part that matters first."

She lowered her head so he would not see her eyes do the small wet thing they were doing.

"There is one more correction," he said. "It is not for the recitation. It is for the inheritance. Reach under the bedframe."

She reached. Her hand met a small wooden case the size of a child's fist, fitted with a felt-lined latch the way reliquary cases were fitted, the way her grandmother's bench had taught her grandfather to fit them on the long evenings before the Bleaching. She drew it out.

"It is yours now," he said. "It has been yours since you were born. I held it for you because a stone wills better than a will does."

She did not open it. She knew, by the weight of it in her palm, by the small honest pressure of it against the bones of her wrist, what was inside.

"Grandfather," she said.

"Listen first," he said. "Decide after. *Do not decide now.* Promise me."

"I promise."

"Good." He closed his eyes again. "Now finish the recitation. I am tired. You were almost at the part about the chair."

She finished the recitation. She talked about the chair, which had been Ait-Resh's tuning chair and which he had refused to throw out, even after the bench was sold to pay the funeral. She talked about the apprentices. She talked about the third-stage flux throttle and what he called its *honest miniaturization*, which had been a small-hearted, careful piece of engineering and the work of which he was most quietly proud.

When she finished, he was asleep.

She sat there a long time with the wooden case in her lap before she carried it out of the sleeping-cell and into the rest of her life.

## II. The Pod Frame

The pod's main corridor was running its low-cycle hum. Drift Clade habitats did not bother with day-night cycles the way lattice stations did; they ran instead on watch-cycles, four hours apiece, soft and rhythmic, so a body would feel always slightly drowsy and slightly attentive at the same time. It was, the pod elder said, the natural cadence of a people who lived inside something they could not entirely trust to remain solid.

The walls were murmuring tonight.

Yenu noticed it three steps into the corridor — a small, soft *forgetting* in a corner near the membrane-seam, the way a piece of cloth might forget for an instant that it was woven, become for half a second only loose strands, and then remember itself. She had grown up with the pod's small forgettings. She had never grown used to them. Tonight there was a new one near the sleeping-cells, and the old one near the galley felt deeper than yesterday.

The coherence-frame was failing.

Every Drift Clade pod was held together by two things: its physical hull, which was simple, and its coherence-frame, which was complicated. The frame was a low-resonant mesh of tuned filaments through which the pod's collective recitations were threaded — the names, the dailies, the lullabies, the genealogies — so that the pod *remembered itself as a thing that held*. Without the frame, a hull was just metal and polymer, and metal and polymer alone, in the outer sleeves of the Helios Drift, was not enough.

The frame needed re-tuning every eight or nine cycles. The pod's last re-tuning had been thirteen cycles ago. Hol-Vren had been making the math work for two seasons now, the way people make math work when there is nothing else to do with it.

Yenu walked past the new soft place near the sleeping-cells. She did not touch the wall. Touching a softening wall, Hol-Vren had taught her at eleven, only taught the wall to forget your hand was a hand.

The pod elder was in the galley, doing the kettle. Pod elders always did the kettle on third watch. It was, like everything else on a Drift Clade pod, a recitation: *I am still here, the kettle is still hot, water still knows how to be water at this temperature.*

"Sit," he said, without turning.

She sat. She put the wooden case on the table between them, latched.

He poured her a cup of the bitter green that the clade drank for company. He poured himself one. He sat down across from her and looked, very deliberately, at the case, and then, very deliberately, away from it.

"He gave it to you," he said.

"Yes."

"Have you listened?"

"No."

"Good." He drank. "Listen at Halix. Tess Vlin's bench. She is not honest the way Solace is honest, but she is honest the way Halix is honest, which is good enough for a valuation. Do not let her put it on the open shelf. Tell her you are inheriting it. She will know what that means."

"Elder," Yenu said, because she had been raised right and the question had to be asked outright before it could be answered sideways, "are you telling me to sell it?"

He set his cup down. He looked at her with the small, terrible kindness of a man who had been a pod elder for thirty-one years and had said this kind of sentence too many times.

"I am not telling you anything," he said. "I am telling you where the bench is. The frame needs eight cycles of re-tuning. A stone like the one in that case will buy us twelve, possibly fourteen, depending on the assay. Your grandfather knew the math. He held that stone for you for sixteen years. He did not tell me what was on it. He did not tell me what he wanted you to do. He told me only the bench."

She looked at the case.

"He told me to promise to decide later," she said.

The elder nodded once.

"Then decide later," he said. "But decide at Halix, not here. You cannot decide here. Here the walls are doing the deciding."

The kettle did its small final hiss. He poured her a second cup she did not want.

"Your mother is in the membrane-room," he added, "doing the recitation for your grandfather. She will be there until fourth watch. Go before she comes out. If you say goodbye to her she will say something she will be sorry for later, and you do not need that on the shuttle."

"Elder."

"Go."

She went.

## III. The Tether-Shuttle

The tether-shuttle to Halix was a six-hour ride on a thin filament the pod shared with two other Drift Clade pods and one Remembrancer outstation. The shuttle was a cup of pressurized air on a hook, basically; it had no Memory Drive, because tether transit did not need one, the filament's shared recitation being enough to hold the line.

She sat in the cup with the wooden case in her lap and her knees drawn up against the cold, and she did not open the case, because she had promised, and because she was afraid.

Drift Clade fear was a specific shape. It was not the lattice fear of losing a stable self; she had never had a stable self to lose. It was the fear of being *asked* to carry something a clade body was not built to carry. A long memory. A deep weight. A thing that would not disperse on schedule.

She knew, in the bones of her light arms, what was on the stone. It was her grandmother's voice, calling someone. It was a thing her grandfather had loved enough to ask his dying wife to tune for a granddaughter not yet conceived, and to carry it himself for sixteen years, and to hand it over now without naming what was on it because *naming would have been to spend it, a little, in advance.*

She had never met her grandmother. The Bleaching had taken her four years before Yenu was born.

The filament hummed. The cup swayed. Somewhere out the small porthole, the Helios Drift turned its slow blue weight, and an unremembered star did its work at the center of nothing.

Halix Exchange appeared as a smear of warm yellow light at the end of the third hour, swelled into a smear of warm yellow shapes at the end of the fourth, and resolved at the end of the sixth into what it was: a bazaar-ship four kilometers long, hung with banners in seven languages, smelling — even through the cup's filters — of incense, hot oil, sweat, ozone, and the faint cold metal smell of reliquary work being done at scale.

The cup docked. The clamps engaged. Yenu unfolded herself out of it and into the noise.

It hit her like a held breath being released into her face. Cries, bargains, recitations, prayers, the dense low hum of tuning rings working in a dozen booths at once. The air was *warm* — wrongly warm, the way a body is warm — and that was the first thing the clade in her noticed and disliked. A bazaar was a body. Bodies wanted things. She had been raised to be suspicious of any place that wanted things on its own behalf.

She walked. She had never been to Halix and she had been told the layout by three different cousins, none of whom had agreed, so she walked and let her ears find the bench.

She found it by sound.

Tess Vlin's counter was on the fourth deck, in the quieter quarter where the inheritance work was done, and Yenu knew it was the right counter because three booths before she reached it she heard, very faintly, the *good silence* a tuning ring made when it was lowered properly over a stone. A ring being lowered well had a sound like a held breath. A ring being lowered badly had a sound like a held breath someone meant to charge you for.

Tess Vlin's ring sang held.

Yenu stopped at the counter and waited. The brokerwoman was finishing with a customer — a heavy-set lattice man in a good coat, who was being told, politely and at length, that his stone was a fake. He took it badly. He left. Tess Vlin did not watch him go.

She looked up at Yenu instead.

She was a small woman, middle-aged, mid-gravity by build, with the long forearms of a tuner and the careful neutral face of a broker, and her eyes did the thing eyes did in this trade: they took Yenu in once, fully, and filed her under *Drift Clade, young, inheriting, alone, scared, will not be cheated by accident.*

"Sit," Tess said. "Show me."

Yenu sat. She placed the case on the bench. She unlatched it. She did not lift the stone.

Tess looked at the stone in the case for a long moment without touching it. Her face did not change. Then she said, quietly:

"That is a light-bequest."

"Yes."

"From a grandparent."

"Both."

Tess's eyebrows lifted, the smallest fraction. "Both? The donor and —"

"My grandmother was the donor. My grandfather had it tuned and held it for me. Sixteen years."

"Then it is a *long-held* light-bequest. The hold itself has thickened it." She set her hands flat on the bench. "Child. Before I lower the ring. You understand what you have brought me."

"I understand."

"Tell me, then."

It was a test, of course. Halix benches did not work with people who did not know what they were selling. Yenu sat up straighter. Her clade voice came out a little formal, the way it did when she was reciting.

"A light-bequest," she said, "is a memory stone willed across one missed generation. The donor and the intended hearer never met. The donor records the memory for someone who does not yet exist; the holder carries it; the hearer inherits it. When tuned, the stone sings in two registers. The first is the donor's, which is a personal memory and low-mass. The second is the *absence of the hearer* — the place where the recitation was supposed to land, which never did. The two together make the stone behave," — she searched for the word — "*inhabited*. Not civic. Not personal. Something between. Unstable in drives. Unsafe to burn. Honored, in inheritance, only by being heard or released, never spent."

Tess looked at her for a long second. Then she said, with the small involuntary respect of a professional acknowledging another professional, "Who taught you that."

"My grandfather. Two days ago. He was already dying. He thought I should know what was coming."

"He was a good teacher."

"He was."

"All right," Tess said. "I will lower the ring. Not to value it. To tell you what's on it. I will not put it on the shelf and I will not name a price until you ask me to. Agreed?"

"Agreed."

"And, child." Tess paused with her hand on the ring. "If you start crying, do it quietly. The booth two down is a Synod counter, and they listen for grief the way other people listen for tells."

"I won't cry."

"I have heard that before."

She lowered the ring.

## IV. The Two Registers

The booth thickened.

Yenu had heard tuning before — at the pod funerals, at the elder's bench, once at a Solace outstation when she was nine — but she had never heard a stone tuned that had been made for her. The difference was the difference between hearing a name spoken in a room and hearing your own name spoken in a room. The air changed shape around it.

The first register came up first, as Tess had said it would: a woman's voice, mid-range, warm, calling a name. The name was *Yen* — not Yenu, *Yen*, the diminutive Yenu's mother used and no one else, the diminutive Yenu had not known her grandmother had even known to plan for. The voice said *Yen* three times, the way one calls a child in from a courtyard at the end of a long afternoon, and under the voice was the smell of pod-air through a cooling vent and the small papery rustle of someone's sleeve and the feeling of a hand on the back of a neck, brief, sure.

Yenu had not cried at her grandfather's bedside and she did not cry now. She had been told not to and she had been raised to obey small useful instructions.

But the second register rose under the first, the way Tess had warned her it would, and the second register was the one that did the damage.

It was not a voice. It was the *shape of a listening that had never happened.* It was the cup-shape her grandmother had tuned into the stone for a granddaughter she would never meet — the carved hollow where Yenu's young attention was supposed to fit, and had not, because Yenu had not existed yet, and now did, and was sitting at a Halix counter sixteen years too late, hearing the place that had been waiting for her all that time.

The hollow was the size and shape of her.

Tess Vlin lifted the ring at the end of forty seconds, very gently, the way a person closes the lid of a piano that someone else has been playing.

The booth thinned again.

Yenu sat with her hands in her lap and her face entirely composed and she did not cry, because she had said she would not, and because the booth two down was a Synod counter and Tess had asked her not to.

"That," Tess said quietly, "is the cleanest light-bequest I have heard in nine years. The donor knew what she was doing. The holder kept it well. I am sorry for your losses, both of them, and I am sorry for the third one, which is whatever you are about to choose."

"Value it," Yenu said. Her voice was level. The clade was good at level voices.

Tess named a number.

It was, as the pod elder had said, more than enough. It was twelve cycles of frame re-tuning. It was, with care, fourteen. It was enough that Yenu's mother would not have to make the math work for two more seasons, and the corner near the sleeping-cells would remember it was a corner, and the pod would not drift into a void margin during the next bad sleeve-storm, which the medic had said was, frankly, the more likely of her grandfather's two endings.

Yenu sat with the number in her ears for a moment.

"All right," she said. "Thank you. I would like to —"

"Wait," said another voice.

She had not heard the second person come up to the counter. He had, she realized, been waiting one booth over, in line behind a fictional customer, doing the slow patient circling that lattice fixers did when they wanted to look like they had only happened by.

He was lattice. Mnemosyne, by the cut of the coat — that careful gray that meant Consortium money without ever quite saying so. He was not unkind-looking. People in his line of work seldom were; an unkind face cost the Consortium too much in negotiations.

"Forgive me," he said. "I could not help overhearing. May I make a counter-offer?"

Tess Vlin's face did the small closed thing professional faces did when they were about to be polite to someone they would have preferred to throw off the deck.

"This is an inheritance valuation," she said. "Not an open sale."

"Of course," he said. "I am not bidding to the bench. I am bidding to the child."

He turned to Yenu. His voice was good. It was the voice of a man who had been trained to address Drift Clade adolescents and had practiced.

"My name is Aurel Ket," he said. "I work for a cultural reinforcement program out of the Lattice Core. We are, frankly, in the business of stabilizing endangered cultural memory before it disperses. Light-bequests like yours are — I am sorry to be blunt — exactly the kind of stones we are commissioned to acquire. We pay above market. Significantly above. And we do not unmake them. We preserve them in a curated archive. They are heard. They are studied. They are *kept.*"

"Aurel," Tess said, with the tone of someone who had warned a man twice already.

"One moment, Tess." He did not look at her. He looked only at Yenu. "I will tell you the offer. You decide what you do with it. Your grandmother's voice would enter a permanent collection. She would be remembered, properly, in archives that do not crash. The Bleaching could not touch her there. She would also" — he hesitated, calibrating — "she would also become part of a cultural reinforcement field. Specifically, an inner-ring lattice node currently suffering some conceptual drift. Her recitation, woven into that node, would help stabilize approximately six thousand lives a day."

"Aurel."

"I am almost done, Tess." He named a number. It was three times Tess's number. "Plus a stipend, paid annually, to your pod, for the use rights. Indexed to inflation."

Yenu did not speak.

She understood the offer entirely. She understood it in the small, complete, terrible way clade children understood the world: by mapping every part of it onto the body of someone they loved.

The Consortium would take her grandmother's voice and weave it into a cultural reinforcement field. Her grandmother's *Yen, Yen, Yen* would become — slowly, over months — *part of the civic memory* of six thousand lattice citizens who had never met her, who would never know her name, who would feel her calling them in from their courtyards at the ends of their afternoons and would think it was their own grandmother, or the city's grandmother, or no one's at all, just a warm pressure in the air at evening, and her grandmother's voice would do that work for those six thousand citizens for as long as the field held, which would be — Yenu did the math in her clade-quick head — perhaps two hundred standard years.

And Yenu's grandmother would no longer be a personal memory.

She would be civic.

She would be *heard*, as Aurel Ket had said. She would not be unmade. She would not be lost.

She would also no longer be *for* Yenu.

The hollow in the stone — that exact cup-shape carved for a sixteen-year-old granddaughter's listening, which had been waiting sixteen years for Yenu to climb into it — would close. The Consortium did not buy hollows. They sanded them. The lattice was not made for inheritance memory; it was made for civic. Aurel Ket had not lied. He had merely declined to mention which part of the stone the inner-ring node could not use.

She thought, briefly and clearly, of the pod frame, and of the corner near the sleeping-cells that had softened tonight. Three times Tess's number. Indexed to inflation. The pod would not have to make the math work, possibly, for ten years. Possibly more.

She thought of her grandfather, who had told her *do not decide now*, and who had also taught her, six years ago at his bench, that *the hardest sale is the one that comes wearing a doctrine.*

She looked at Aurel Ket.

He had a good face. He was waiting patiently. He did this work because someone had to. He was not, by his own lights, the villain.

"Mr. Ket," she said.

"Aurel, please."

"Mr. Ket. The doctrine on the wall at Solace says, *Spend only what you can afford. Preserve what must endure.* My clade does not take that doctrine, exactly, but we have an old one of our own, which is closer than people think. It says, *We carry only what we can put down.*"

He nodded politely. He had heard variations of this from clade sellers before. It usually preceded a yes.

"I cannot put my grandmother down into a reinforcement field," Yenu said, "because the field is a thing I cannot lift back off her again. I am not refusing your offer because I am moral. I am refusing it because I am clade, and my clade does not put down what it cannot lift. Please go away now. I would like to finish with Tess."

He looked at her for a long moment. Then he did, to his credit, what a good Consortium man did when a deal had failed: he bowed his head one careful degree, set a card on the counter that Yenu would not pick up, and walked off into the bazaar without making a scene.

Tess Vlin let out a breath Yenu had not seen her take.

"Child," she said.

"Don't."

"All right." Tess reached across the counter and, briefly, touched the back of Yenu's hand with one finger — the small bench-tuner's gesture for *acknowledged.* "Do you want me to value it again, for the pod?"

"No," Yenu said. "I am taking it home."

"To do what."

Yenu looked down at the stone in its case. The hollow in it was, she could feel even with the ring up, still her shape. Still waiting.

"To listen to it," she said. "And then to let it go."

Tess Vlin closed the case for her, very gently, and latched it.

"Tether-shuttle leaves in forty minutes," she said. "Eat something first. The cup ride home is worse than the cup ride here."

"Why."

"You will know more on the way back."

## V. The Recitation in Return

She knew more on the way back.

She sat in the cup with the case in her lap and she opened the latch — just the latch, not the lid — and she listened to the stone through the wood, which was a thing tuners said you could not do and which her grandfather had taught her you could, if you had been raised on a pod where everything was always slightly forgetting to be itself.

The first register was faint through the wood. The second was clearer. The hollow was still her size. It had not minded the bazaar. It had not minded Aurel Ket. It was patient the way only an absence could be patient: it had nothing else to do.

She thought about her pod.

She thought about the corner near the sleeping-cells, which she had not touched, and which by the time she got home would either have remembered it was a corner or would have softened further into something the pod would have to route around. She thought about her mother in the membrane-room reciting her grandfather. She thought about the elder doing the kettle for someone else now. She thought about Hol-Vren, who was probably still in the miniaturization shop, eating cold soup, pretending he did not know she had gone to Halix.

She thought about the number Aurel Ket had named, and the number Tess Vlin had named, and the way both numbers were, in the end, smaller than the stone.

She closed the latch.

The cup got back to the pod a little after second watch on the following night. The pod was quiet. The galley light was on. The elder had left a cup of bitter green on the table, cold, untouched, in the polite Drift Clade way of saying *I waited, but I will not ask.*

She walked past it.

She went to her grandfather's sleeping-cell.

He was still alive. The medic was wrong; or the medic was right and he had simply held on the extra day on purpose, which was a thing Drift Clade dyers did when they had unfinished business and the discipline to manage their own oxygen. His eyes opened when she came in.

"Did you sell it," he said.

"No."

"Did you almost sell it."

"Once. To the lattice. The Consortium."

"For the field?"

"For the field."

"Mm." He closed his eyes again. "Good refusal. Did you tell them about the hollow?"

"No."

"Better refusal." His mouth did its small not-smile. "Yenu. Listen. I am going to ask you for something I do not have the right to ask. You can say no. Promise me that first."

"I promise."

"I want to hear her once more. Before."

She had known he would ask. She had known it on the cup ride home. She had been preparing herself for it the way clade children prepared for any inheritance: by deciding in advance to be generous.

"You don't have to ask," she said. "It's yours too. It was always yours too."

"It is *yours,*" he corrected, gently. "That is the whole point of the bequest. I am asking. Do not give me a clade answer. Give me a Yenu answer."

She thought about it.

"Yes," she said. "Once. With me."

She brought the case to the bedside. She unlatched it. She lifted the stone, for the first time, into her palm.

It was very small. It was very warm. It felt like a small living thing that had been holding still for sixteen years.

She had no tuning ring. She did not need one. The stone had been made for her listening, and her listening was a kind of ring that the stone recognized. She held it near her grandfather's temple — not pressed, just near — and she held it near her own, and she opened her attention to it the way her grandfather had taught her to open her attention to a flux throttle at the bench: *gently, completely, without expecting it to perform.*

It sang.

The first register filled the cell. *Yen, Yen, Yen.* The grass and the sleeve and the hand on the back of the neck. Her grandfather's face did the small soft thing faces did when a memory they had been carrying alone for forty years finally turned out to be the same shape someone else had carried.

The second register rose under it. The hollow. The cup-shape carved for Yenu, which was now, at last, occupied — by Yenu, listening, sixteen years late but listening — and which, because it was occupied, was *changing*.

She felt the stone shift, slightly, in her palm.

This was the part no one had taught her. This was the part the bench-tuners did not know, because the bench-tuners never got to hear a light-bequest *complete*; the stones never made it that far. They were always sold first, or vaulted, or — at Solace — refused and locked away. A light-bequest, when its intended hearer finally heard it, did not stay a light-bequest. It became something the lexicon did not yet have a word for. A *closed inheritance.* A stone that had done its work.

She felt her grandmother's voice settle, very quietly, into the place in her own mind that had been the cup-shape, and seal there — not as memory exactly, because Yenu's clade did not hold memory that way, but as a *recitation she would now be able to give.* She would be able to say, for the rest of her life: *My grandmother's voice was warm in the mid-range. She called me Yen. She smelled of cooling-vent air. Her sleeve was thin and her hand was sure.* She would be able to say it to her own grandchildren, if she had any. She would be able to say it at funerals. She would be able to say it to herself in the dark.

The stone in her palm went cool.

Her grandfather was crying very quietly. Drift Clade men cried in a particular small way, with their faces still, only the wet at the eyes. He had not, she thought, cried for her grandmother since the Bleaching. He had not been able to afford to.

"Thank you," he said. "That's enough."

"Yes."

"Yenu."

"Yes."

"I am going to stop now. In about an hour. I will not need the medic. Do not call her."

"All right."

"Recite me to your mother after. Get it right."

"I will."

"Get the part about the throttle right. It was honest miniaturization. People always remember the rest and forget that part."

"I will."

He closed his eyes.

She sat with him for the hour. She did not recite. She did not need to. The stone was cool in her hand, and her grandmother's voice was settled in her, and the cup-shape was gone, which meant the bequest was closed, which meant — she understood this slowly, sitting there in the green light — that what she now held in her palm was no longer an inheritance.

It was a small, used, honest object. A stone that had finished its work.

He stopped at the end of the hour. He did not make a sound. The Drift Clade had a saying about this, which her grandfather himself had taught her, and which she now, with him, found true: *the well-finished do not announce themselves.*

She sat with him another twenty minutes. Then she got up, and she did not call the medic, because he had asked her not to, and she carried the stone — not the case; the case stayed by the bed — out of the sleeping-cell, down the corridor, past the soft corner that had not gotten worse and had not gotten better, to the pod's outer membrane.

## VI. The Release

The outer membrane of a Drift Clade pod was a thin clear pane set into the hull, ringed with a small ritual seal that any pod-dweller could open in the case of a Releasing. It was for this. It had always been for this. Yenu had never opened it before. Her hands knew the motion anyway, the way clade hands knew motions they had only watched.

She opened the seal.

She did not break the membrane — that was a different ritual, used only for whole-body release, which was illegal in lattice space and frowned on by Solace and which the clade itself reserved for very particular endings. She opened only the small release-port, the size of her thumb, which was for stones.

She held the cool stone in her palm.

She thought, very briefly, of Aurel Ket's number, and of Tess Vlin's number, and of the corner near the sleeping-cells, and of her mother in the membrane-room reciting her grandfather, who would now also need reciting *for*, and of the pod elder doing the kettle.

She thought of her grandmother's voice, which was no longer in the stone, because the stone had finished giving it to her.

The stone in her palm now held only the small, closed, *neutral* residue of a completed bequest. A clean object. It would not power a drive — too small, too narrow, too quiet. It would not stabilize a frame — wrong tuning. It would not, in fact, do anything anymore, for anyone, except sit on a shelf and be a thing that had once been a memory.

She could keep it. The clade allowed that. Some grandchildren did. There were small ancestral shelves in some of the older sleeping-cells, lined with closed bequests, and they were considered, by people who liked that kind of thing, a kind of comfort.

She did not want a shelf.

She put the stone in the release-port and she closed the inner seal and she opened the outer one and the small puff of pod-air took the stone with it out into the Drift, where it would fall, very slowly, toward an unremembered star that none of her family would live to see it reach.

The Drift's dark matter took it. That was what dark matter was, after all: the universe's unconscious archive, the place forgotten-but-not-erased memory went to rest. The stone was not even a memory anymore. It was a small piece of carrier that had once been a memory. The dark matter would not mind. The dark matter did not mind anything. It was, her grandfather had once said, *the kindest part of the cosmos, because it asks nothing of what it holds.*

She closed the outer seal. She closed the inner seal. She closed the ritual seal.

She turned around.

The corridor was waiting for her.

She walked back along it, past the soft corner, which had — she felt this more than saw it — *firmed, very slightly,* in the last hour. Not because of the stone. The stone was gone. Because she had walked past it twice now, with the recitation of her grandfather already half-built in her mouth, and the pod's coherence-frame knew her voice, and her voice, just now, was a denser thing than it had been at second watch yesterday.

She walked to the galley.

Her mother was in the galley, holding the cold cup of bitter green that the elder had left out, and looking at it as if it were a small problem she would solve in a moment, once she had decided how. Her mother had heard Yenu come back from Halix. Her mother had heard Yenu go to the sleeping-cell. Her mother had been waiting for Yenu to come out, the way Drift Clade women waited for everything, which was: without saying so.

Yenu sat down at the table.

She did not pick up the cold cup.

"He stopped," she said.

Her mother nodded once, very slowly.

"You released the stone."

"Yes."

"Was she there. At the end."

"Yes."

Her mother put the cup down. She put both her hands flat on the table. She was a Drift Clade woman and she did not cry in front of her daughter, but she did the small breath-thing that meant *I am taking a moment, and you may have the table while I take it.*

Yenu took the table.

"He was the third child and the second son," she began, quietly, because there was no one else to begin it now and the recitation had to start somewhere and the pod needed her voice on the walls. "He was born on the spine. Before the uncoupling. His mother was Ait-Vren. His father was Skather-Hol. He married Ait-Resh in his twenty-eighth year, and she was a stone-tuner who trained at Solace for nine years, and she died during the Bleaching, and he did not remarry, because he could not afford to."

Her mother breathed out, a long breath, and the small soft place near the galley door — which Yenu had not noticed before — *firmed.*

"He had three children," Yenu went on. "I am the daughter of the youngest. He taught miniaturization for thirty-one years. He miniaturized the third-stage flux throttle that the lattice still uses. He called it *honest miniaturization,* and he was right to. He had one grandchild. Her name is Yenu Skather-Ait. She is sixteen standard. She has, just now, inherited her grandmother."

Her mother lifted her face. There was wet at her eyes. She did not wipe it.

"Keep going," she said.

Yenu kept going.

She did not know how long the speaking would hold the walls. The pod frame still needed eight cycles. The math had not changed. The corner near the sleeping-cells was firmer but not fixed. Hol-Vren would still be eating cold soup in the miniaturization shop tomorrow, pretending he did not know.

She spoke anyway. That was the work.

The pod, around her, listened.
