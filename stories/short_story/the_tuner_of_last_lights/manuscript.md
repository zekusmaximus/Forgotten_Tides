# The Tuner of Last Lights

## I. Second Watch

The stone came in at second watch, wrapped in unbleached linen and bedded in a courier-case that hummed two notes off true.

Indre Vael heard it before she touched it.

She kept a quiet bench at the back of the Solace Hospice — three rooms cut into the inner shell of the station, sealed with old felt and older silence. Tuning required quiet the way surgery required light. On the wall, the hospice's only ornament: a single line of script, half-lost under decades of incense smoke.

*Spend only what you can afford. Preserve what must endure.*

She had read it so often the letters had stopped meaning anything and started meaning everything.

The courier was new. Drift Clade by the look — thin frame, that loose-jointed walk people developed when their home gravity had been honest about its weakness. He set the case on her bench with the careful slowness of someone who had been told the contents were fragile, but had not been told why.

"For Calvent," he said. "Room twelve. Tonight."

"Tonight," Indre repeated.

"They said you'd understand."

She did. Mira Calvent was in her last raveling — perhaps a day, perhaps less. The mind comes apart at the end the way an old rope does: strand by strand, until you can no longer pretend the whole was ever a single thing. The Remembrancers had a word for this final hour, but Indre did not like to use it in front of the dying. *Unmaking.* It was honest, and the dying did not always need honesty.

What they needed was a stone. The right stone. A clean, well-tuned reliquary held near the temple while the breath slowed — not to preserve memory, which was already past saving, but to give the failing mind something kind to rest its weight against on the way down.

Indre took the case. The two off-notes prickled against her teeth.

"Who sent it?"

The courier's eyes did the thing eyes do when a person has been instructed not to know. "Private donor. Through Halix Exchange."

"Halix is on the outer ring."

"Yes."

"Halix doesn't move hospice stock."

He shrugged with both shoulders, a Drift Clade gesture that meant *I am too light to carry that question*. "Said you'd understand."

She let him go. There was no point in pressing a courier; couriers, like memory drives, only carried what they were fed.

When the felt door swung shut behind him, Indre sat for a long moment with her palms flat on either side of the case, the way she always sat before opening a new piece. The bench's tuning ring lay quiet above her head, a thin halo of brass and inductive copper. She had not yet lowered it. She wanted, for one more breath, to be a woman receiving a gift and not a reliquarist receiving evidence.

Then she opened the case.

The stone was small. Smaller than most. About the size and shape of a river pebble worn long by water, though no river had ever touched it. The surface had the faint nacre that good tuning produced — a milky shimmer where light caught the inner lattice. To an untrained eye, it would have read as a fine personal-memory reliquary, the kind families commissioned for a beloved relative's final hour. A wedding morning, perhaps. A son's first laugh. The smell of bread in a particular kitchen on a particular afternoon.

To Indre, who had spent twenty-two years with her hands on such stones, it read as wrong.

The shimmer was too even. Tuned stones held their light unevenly, the way memory itself was uneven — bright at the edges of significance, dim in the connective tissue. This one was polished smooth across its whole face, as though the irregularities had been *filed off*.

She lowered the tuning ring.

It settled over the stone, and the room thickened.

## II. The Cut Harmonic

A well-made reliquary, when probed, sang.

It was not a sound exactly. It was a sensation that the ear interpreted afterward as sound, the way a struck bell is first a pressure and only later a note. Personal memories sang in narrow registers: a single voice, a small chord. Civic memories roared — the reason they were dangerous to burn in a corridor, the reason no responsible reliquarist tuned them for hospice use. They did not comfort. They commanded.

This stone sang in a register Indre recognized, and did not want to recognize.

It began with the expected note: a woman's voice, mid-range, calling a name. *Mira.* So far, so consistent — this was meant for Mira Calvent; the donor had clearly been someone close to her, someone whose voice the dying mind would lean toward. Then, beneath the voice, the second harmonic: the smell of sun on dry grass, a hand around hers, a sense of being seen and unhurried.

A good memory. A small, generous, ordinary memory.

And under that, very faint, a third tone Indre had only heard four times in her career, and each time had refused to forget.

A scrape. A reluctance. The tonal signature of a memory that had been *removed* from a mind that had not consented to its removal.

Reliquarists called it the cut harmonic.

When an archivist rendered a memory from a willing donor, the memory came away clean — like fruit lifted from a stem ripened to release. The donor's other memories closed around the absence the way water closes around a thrown stone. The reliquary, when later played, carried no shadow.

When a memory was taken by force, the surrounding mind tore. The render did not lift; it *severed*. Even after polishing — even after the most careful cosmetic work — the stone retained the faintest harmonic of that severance: a high, off-true note like a string that had been cut rather than untied.

Indre lifted the ring. The room thinned again. Her hands were cold.

She looked at the stone for a long time.

It was, she judged, a memory of perhaps forty seconds. A woman, probably a mother or older sister, in some warm afternoon, calling Mira's name. Someone had been made to give that up. Someone, somewhere, no longer remembered that afternoon, that grass, that hand. Whoever they were, they had walked out of the extraction room lighter, and not by choice.

And the patient down the corridor — Mira Calvent, eighty-three, two daughters, six grandchildren, a small career as a station horticulturist that no one outside her family had ever found interesting — was going to die tonight, and a stranger had paid for her last hour to be eased by a stolen mouthful of someone else's love.

Indre Vael had been a Remembrancer for twenty-two years. She had buried more patients than she had birthdays. She knew her doctrines by the marrow.

*Spend only what you can afford. Preserve what must endure.*

The doctrine did not say whose afford. It did not say whose endure. That, she had been taught in her novitiate, was the entire weight of the work.

She closed the case.

She went to find the night sister.

## III. The Night Sister

Sister Olenne was in the dispensary, counting blister-vials of the soft sedative they gave to patients whose raveling had become frightening to themselves. She did not look up when Indre came in. Olenne had a face like a carefully folded letter, and a gift for not being surprised.

"Tell me," she said.

Indre told her. She kept her hands behind her back so the older woman would not see them shake.

Olenne finished her count. She wrote the number in her ledger. She capped her pen.

"You're certain."

"I'm certain."

"You're certain enough to refuse it."

"Yes."

Olenne folded her ledger closed. "Mira has perhaps eight hours, Indre. Possibly four. She has been afraid since yesterday morning. Her daughters are not here; the corridor from Tess is too thin this week for civilian traffic. She has a clade-grand-niece in room sixteen who comes and reads to her, but Mira no longer recognizes the voice, only the cadence. Without a stone, she will die in fear."

"I know."

"You know."

"Yes."

"And you would refuse the stone anyway."

Indre did not answer immediately. The dispensary smelled of antiseptic and the faint sweet rot of old paper labels. A pipe somewhere in the wall ticked through a thermal cycle.

"If I give her that stone," she said, "I will be using a person to ease her dying. Not a memory. A person. Someone who is still walking around without a piece of their afternoon, who probably knows something was taken and does not know what, who is lighter tonight than they should be."

"That is true."

"And if I do not give it to her, she dies frightened, and the theft stands anyway. The donor doesn't get their memory back because I refused. The harm is already done."

"Also true."

"So the question," Indre said, "is whether I become the person who *uses* what was stolen, or the person who *refuses to use* what was stolen. The stone exists either way. The donor's loss is the same either way."

Olenne nodded slowly, as though Indre had reached the bottom of a stair Olenne had climbed a long time ago and had been waiting at since.

"That is one question," she said. "It is not the only one."

"What's the other?"

"Whether you owe the dying woman her comfort more than you owe the living donor your refusal. They cannot both have you tonight."

Indre felt the floor of her stomach drop a small distance and stop.

"I don't know the donor."

"No."

"I know Mira."

"You have known Mira for six weeks."

"That's still knowing."

"Yes. It is." Olenne reached across the counter and took Indre's wrist, lightly, the way a sister will when she has nothing to offer but contact. "Indre. I am old, and the Order trained me in a stricter time. I would have refused the stone. I would have sat with Mira myself and held her hand through every hour of it, and I would have considered the holding a sufficient answer. I am telling you this not because I think I was right. I am telling you because I do not think I was right, and I still cannot promise you I was wrong."

"That's not useful."

"No." Olenne let go. "But it is true. And on this bench, the only thing you are ever permitted to use is what is true."

She picked up her pen again and went back to her count.

Indre stood in the doorway a moment longer.

"The courier," she said. "Drift Clade. Came through Halix Exchange. Halix doesn't move hospice stock."

"No."

"Halix moves Synod stock."

Olenne did not look up. "Sometimes."

"So this stone — if I refuse it, if I send it back —"

"It does not go back to the donor. The donor is not a destination on its route. If you refuse it, it goes onto the next shelf at the next exchange, and someone else, somewhere else, buys it for someone else's last hour. Or it doesn't sell, and the Synod takes it as a tithe, and they unmake it for sport, because that is what they do."

"So refusing it doesn't undo anything."

"No. Refusing it lets you sleep."

The word hit harder than Indre expected.

"That's not the same thing as being right."

"It is not." Olenne marked another number. "It is also not nothing."

## IV. Room Twelve

Indre walked the long way to room twelve. The hospice corridors at second watch were under low light — the soft yellow the Remembrancers used for the dying hours, on the theory that the human eye, given a choice, preferred to leave the world in something like late afternoon.

Mira Calvent's door was open. It was always open now. The hospice did not close the doors of patients in their last day; the closing was meant to come from inside the room, in its own time.

Indre stopped in the doorway.

Mira lay propped against three pillows, very small, the way the dying often became small in the last hours — as though the body, beginning to forget itself, was rehearsing how little space it would soon need. Her eyes were open and tracking something Indre could not see. Her left hand moved slightly against the blanket, fingers shaping a gesture from a life Indre had never been part of.

Beside the bed, in the visitor's chair, the clade-grand-niece had fallen asleep with a book in her lap. She was perhaps twenty, fine-boned, the Drift Clade lightness in her sleeping face. Her thumb was still inside the book, keeping the page.

Indre stepped inside.

Mira's eyes moved to her. There was the brief flicker of recognition — not of Indre specifically, but of the *kind* of person Indre was. The robe. The tuning ring at her belt. The presence that meant a stone was nearby.

"Oh," Mira said. Her voice was thinner than yesterday. "Are you the one with the —"

She stopped. The sentence had ravelled before she could finish it. Her face did the small searching thing patients did when they had lost a word that, an hour ago, they had owned.

"I'm here," Indre said, which was what one said when one did not know what else.

Mira's hand found hers. Mira's hand was warm. Mira's hand was alive.

"I'm frightened," Mira said. The word arrived clean, and she seemed surprised by it, as though she had not meant to be that honest.

"I know."

"Will you stay?"

"Yes."

"Do you have —"

She could not finish that sentence either, but she did not need to. Indre felt the weight of the courier-case at her hip the way one feels the weight of an unanswered letter.

She thought of the donor.

She thought of the cut harmonic — that thin, high, severed note, riding underneath the kindness like a wire under skin. If she pressed the stone to Mira's temple, Mira would not hear the cut harmonic. Mira would hear only the voice, the grass, the warm afternoon. Mira would die in someone else's love and would not know it was stolen.

She thought, very briefly, that this was a kind of mercy.

She thought, immediately after, that *that* was the sentence every coercive extractor in the galaxy told themselves, and the Obsidian Synod had built a doctrine on it, and the Remembrancers had built their entire Order in answer to it.

*The lightness that follows forgetting is neither triumph nor tragedy. It is transformation.*

But not the lightness that follows *theft*. The doctrine was specific. The Order had bled to make it specific.

Indre sat down on the edge of the bed. She did not let go of Mira's hand.

"Mira," she said. "I want to tell you something. I don't know if you'll be able to follow it. That's all right. You don't have to follow it. You just have to hear my voice."

Mira's eyes were on her.

"I have a stone," Indre said. "Someone sent it for you. It's a kind stone. It would make this easier. But it isn't — it isn't *honestly* kind. Someone was hurt to make it. I didn't know that until I tuned it. I'm not going to use it. I'm sorry. I know that means tonight will be harder."

Mira's fingers tightened, very slightly, on hers.

"I'm going to stay," Indre said. "I'm going to stay the whole time. And I'm going to tell you about my own afternoon. Not a stone. Just — a thing I remember. From when I was small. It isn't yours, and it isn't anyone you knew, but it's mine and I am giving it to you freely and you can hold onto it on the way."

Mira's mouth moved. It might have been a smile. It might have been the muscles forgetting what a smile was. Either way, Indre took it.

She began to talk.

She told Mira about a courtyard on the station of her childhood, where the air recyclers had vented warm in the late hours and the children had played a game with chalk and a piece of cord. She told her about the smell of someone's cooking through a window. She told her about a cat that had not been anyone's cat, who had decided, for a season, that she belonged to Indre, and then one day had decided otherwise. She told her about the feeling of the chalk dust on her fingers afterward, and the way her mother had not been angry about the dust on her sleeves, because her mother had been a woman who understood the order of importances.

Mira's breathing slowed.

She talked for a long time.

She did not know, after, how long.

## V. Dawn Audit

At some point the clade-grand-niece woke, looked at the bed, looked at Indre, and understood. She did not speak. She came and sat on the other side of the bed and took Mira's other hand, and the three of them stayed that way until Mira's breath, which had been getting smaller for an hour, did not come back from one of its small absences.

The room was very quiet.

The clade-grand-niece bent her head over Mira's hand and made a small Drift Clade sound — a thing between a word and a held breath — that Indre had heard at Drift Clade funerals before. It meant something like *go light*.

Indre let go of Mira's hand and stood up. Her legs were stiff. Her face was wet. She had not noticed when that had started.

She walked back to her bench.

The stone was still in the case where she had left it. It looked, in the dawn light, exactly like what it was: a small, milky, polished pebble that had once been part of a stranger's afternoon.

She did not put it on the shelf with the morning's other reliquaries.

She did not destroy it, either. Destroying a memory stone was not within her authority, and besides — she was not certain anymore that destruction was the right answer for a memory that had not chosen to be taken. The memory itself had done nothing wrong. It was, in a way, still innocent.

She put it in a locked drawer at the back of the bench. She wrote a single line in her audit ledger:

*One stone received off-channel, second watch. Cut harmonic confirmed. Withheld from patient. Provenance investigation requested. — I.V.*

She closed the ledger.

Then she sat down and wrote a second document, in her own hand, on her own paper, not for the audit and not for the Order. A letter.

*To whoever you are,* it began. *Somewhere tonight you are lighter than you should be. I do not know your name. I do not know what was taken from you. I have heard the cut in the stone they made of it, and I am writing this because you should know that someone heard.*

She wrote for a long time.

She knew, even as she wrote, that the letter would never find its donor. The Synod's chains of extraction were too long and too dark; provenance investigations almost never recovered names. The letter would sit in her drawer beside the stone, and one day a Continuity Cleric would find them both and not know what to do with either, and the stone would go to a vault and the letter would go to a fire.

She wrote it anyway.

There were two reasons, and she understood both of them, and she did not despise herself for either.

The first reason was that the donor deserved a witness, even an inadequate one, even one they would never read.

The second reason was that Indre Vael, who had spent the night holding a stranger's hand and refusing a stolen gift, needed the act of writing it to keep her own anchors true. Refusing a thing was a kind of memory you had to make on purpose, or it would not stay. She had learned that her first year on the bench, with her first hard stone, and she had not forgotten it.

She finished the letter. She folded it. She put it in the drawer with the pebble.

She locked the drawer.

She got up to begin the dawn audit, because the dawn audit happened whether the night had been easy or not, and she was, after everything, a Remembrancer.

In the corridor outside, the lights had begun their slow climb toward what the station called morning. Somewhere down the hall, in room sixteen, the clade-grand-niece was making a call to a corridor that was probably too thin to carry her voice clearly, telling daughters in Tess that their mother had gone light.

Indre passed room twelve.

The door, for the first time in six weeks, was closed.

She paused. She put her palm flat against it, very briefly, the way one might rest a hand against an instrument one had recently played and would not play again.

Then she went on to her bench.

The line on the wall, in the rising light, was easier to read than it had been at second watch. The smoke had not lifted; her eyes had simply adjusted.

*Spend only what you can afford. Preserve what must endure.*

She had spent a night, and a small piece of her own childhood, telling a dying woman about a courtyard and a chalk game and a cat that had not been anyone's cat. She had preserved a refusal, and a letter, and the warmth of a hand she had held until it was no longer warm.

It was not a triumph. It was not a tragedy.

It was, she decided, sitting down at her bench and lowering the tuning ring over the morning's first honest stone, transformation.

She had work to do.

She began.
