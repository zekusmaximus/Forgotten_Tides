# The Liturgy of Rust

## I. The Morning Recitation

The station of Ostra-Vael did not wake up; it was reminded to exist.

Every morning at 0600 station time, Continuity Cleric Kaelen knelt in the central sanctum—a small, circular room positioned at the exact gravitational center of the station—and began the Liturgy of the Hull. 

"This is Ostra-Vael," he intoned, his voice amplified by the acoustic baffling of the sanctum, carrying through the vents and corridors. "Built in the eighty-fourth year of the Lattice Expansion. Seven decks. Three primary struts. The hull is tritanium weave. The airlocks seal tight. The floors hold weight."

He had spoken these words every morning for twelve years. For the first eight, they had felt like a prayer. For the last four, they had felt like a lie.

Ostra-Vael was a mining station perched on the edge of the Remnant Seas. Decades ago, it had processed silicate from the surrounding asteroid fields, a bustling hub of three thousand souls. Now, the silicates were gone. The companies had moved on. Only three hundred people remained—those too poor to leave, too old to care, or too stubborn to admit the station was dying.

"Deck four is residential," Kaelen continued, feeling the familiar dull ache behind his eyes. "Deck five is hydroponics. The water runs clean. The bulkheads are strong."

As he spoke, he pictured Deck Four. He pictured the empty hab-blocks, the flickering lumen strips, the smell of recycled air that had been breathed too many times. He pictured Deck Five, where the hydroponics vats were half-empty, choked with brown algae.

He stumbled on the next line. "Deck six is... Deck six is processing."

It wasn't. Deck six was a graveyard of rusted machinery. No one had processed anything there in five years.

The moment the doubt entered his mind, the sanctum shuddered. It was a subtle vibration, not mechanical, but *conceptual*. A softening. 

Kaelen squeezed his eyes shut and gripped the edge of his lectern. He pushed the doubt away, reaching for his primary anchor-knot—a memory of his ordination at the High Archives, the weight of the ceremonial robes, the smell of old paper and ozone. He used the mass of that memory to bear down on the station.

"Deck six is processing," he repeated, forcing the words to carry weight. "The station holds. The station remembers itself."

The shuddering stopped. The walls felt solid again.

Kaelen exhaled a shaky breath and finished the liturgy. When he stood up, his knees popped in the quiet room. He wiped a thin line of sweat from his forehead. 

The station was holding, but barely. And Kaelen knew, with the cold certainty of a man who understands the physics of his own failure, that the drift wasn't coming from the void outside. 

It was coming from him.

## II. The Softening

By midday, the consequences of his faltering faith were visible.

Kaelen was walking through Sector 4 when he saw it. A maintenance worker named Corin was standing in front of an access hatch, staring at it with a look of profound confusion.

"Cleric," Corin said as Kaelen approached. "It's doing it again."

Kaelen looked at the hatch. The metal was there, visually, but it looked... smeared. The edges where the hatch met the bulkhead were indistinct, like a watercolor painting left out in the rain. 

"Don't touch it," Kaelen said sharply.

"I wasn't going to," Corin muttered, taking a step back. "I needed to check the filtration valves, but the handle... I can't remember how the handle works."

That was how it started. Not with the metal melting, but with the *idea* of the metal unraveling. The hatch was forgetting it was a hatch. Corin was forgetting how to interact with it. Soon, the corridor would forget it was a corridor.

"Stand back," Kaelen said.

He stepped up to the hatch. He didn't touch it either. Touching a softening object was dangerous; if the object forgot its solidity while your hand was on it, your hand might forget its solidity too.

Kaelen closed his eyes. He didn't use a reliquary stone—Continuity Clerics were expected to use their own trained focus, their own internal reserves of institutional memory. 

*This is an access hatch,* he thought, projecting the concept outward with deliberate force. *It is made of steel. It has a rotary latch. It opens outward. It serves a purpose.*

He held the concept in his mind, pushing it against the smeared reality of the bulkhead. He felt resistance—the sluggish, apathetic pushback of entropy. The station was tired. It wanted to forget. It wanted to rest.

*Hold,* Kaelen commanded it. 

He sacrificed a small, trivial memory to fuel the push—the taste of a synthetic apple he'd eaten yesterday. It vanished from his mind, a tiny blank spot appearing in his recent history. The energy released by the forgetting flared outward.

The hatch snapped back into focus. The edges sharpened. The handle looked like a handle again.

Corin let out a breath. "Thanks, Cleric."

"It's holding," Kaelen said, his voice tight. "Get your work done quickly."

He walked away before Corin could see his hands shaking.

The Archivist doctrine was clear: *Spend only what you can afford. Preserve what must endure.* 

But what if the thing you were preserving didn't *deserve* to endure? What if Ostra-Vael was just a ghost haunting its own hull? Three hundred people living in a rusted shell, waiting for an end that Kaelen was artificially delaying every morning. If he stopped the liturgies, the station would drift. It would soften, dissolve, and eventually fall into oblivion. 

It would be a tragedy. But wouldn't it also be a release?

## III. The Iron Vow

The alarm sounded at 1800 hours. It was the harsh, mechanical blare of the proximity sensors, a sound Ostra-Vael hadn't heard in months.

Kaelen hurried to the command deck. The station master, an exhausted woman named Vane, was already there, staring at the main auspex display.

"What is it?" Kaelen asked.

"A ship," Vane said. "Coming in hot and heavy. Canticle Fleet."

Kaelen frowned. "Canticle? Out here?"

"They're not here for us," Vane said, pointing to the display. Behind the bright, hard blip of the Canticle ship was a massive, swirling distortion in the sensor readouts. It looked like a storm made of static.

"Eddy," Kaelen breathed.

"A big one," Vane confirmed. "Predatory. It's tracking the ship's memory wake. They're requesting emergency dock and tether. They need our mass to anchor against the pull."

Kaelen felt a cold spike of panic. "Our mass? Vane, the station's conceptual integrity is at thirty percent. If a Canticle ship tethers to us with an eddy on its tail, the sheer mnemonic shear will tear us apart. The station won't hold."

"We can't turn them away, Kaelen," Vane said, her voice hard. "They're Canticle. And if we leave them out there, the eddy will consume them, grow stronger, and then consume us anyway. We have to let them dock."

She hit the comms stud. "Canticle vessel, this is Ostra-Vael command. You are clear for emergency dock at Bay Two. Be advised, our coherence is low. Tread lightly."

"Copy, Ostra-Vael," a crisp, disciplined voice replied over the static. "This is the *Iron Vow*. We are coming in. Brace for impact."

The docking was brutal. Even with the dampeners, the physical impact of the heavy Canticle courier hitting the bay made the deck plates groan. But the physical impact was nothing compared to the conceptual one.

The *Iron Vow* was dense with memory. Canticle ships always were—packed with reliquaries, disciplined pilots, and the heavy, militaristic weight of their own institutional history. When it locked onto Ostra-Vael, it was like dropping a boulder onto a frozen pond. 

The station screamed.

Not audibly, but Kaelen felt it in his teeth. The lights flickered. The gravity plating hummed a discordant note. Down in Sector 4, Kaelen knew, corridors were likely turning to mist.

"They're locked," Vane said, gripping her console. "But the eddy is right behind them. It's hitting the perimeter."

Kaelen looked at the auspex. The static storm washed over the station's outer markers. 

An eddy wasn't a physical storm. It was a localized region of aggressive forgetting. It didn't tear metal; it tore meaning. And Ostra-Vael was already so close to losing its meaning.

"Cleric," Vane said, turning to him. Her eyes were wide. "You need to recite. Now. With everything you have."

## IV. The Weight of the Anchor

Kaelen ran back to the central sanctum. The corridors were already shifting. The floor felt spongy beneath his boots. The walls were losing their color, fading into a uniform, terrifying beige. 

He burst into the sanctum and threw himself behind the lectern. He gripped the edges, closed his eyes, and reached for the Liturgy of the Hull.

"This is Ostra-Vael," he shouted into the acoustic baffles. "Built in the eighty-fourth year... Seven decks. Three primary struts."

The eddy hit the station's core.

It felt like a physical blow to the chest. The air in the sanctum grew freezing cold. The words of the liturgy were ripped from Kaelen's mouth and swallowed by the void. 

*Why bother?* a voice whispered in his mind. It wasn't the eddy speaking; eddies didn't speak. It was his own doubt, magnified and reflected back at him by the forgetting field. *This place is dead. Let it go. Let it end.*

The sanctum walls began to waver. The lectern under his hands felt less like wood and more like the *memory* of wood, fading fast.

"The hull is tritanium weave!" Kaelen screamed, fighting the pull. "The airlocks seal tight!"

He reached for his anchors. He had six major anchor-knots, carefully constructed over his lifetime to keep his identity stable. He grabbed the first one—the memory of his mother's face, the exact shade of her eyes, the sound of her laugh. 

He shoved it into the station's failing coherence.

The memory burned. It flared bright and hot, a sudden influx of mass. The sanctum walls solidified. The lectern hardened. 

But the memory was gone. Kaelen gasped, a sudden, terrifying blankness opening up in his mind. He knew he had a mother. He knew he had loved her. But he could not picture her face. He could not hear her laugh. 

The eddy surged again, hungry, tasting the fresh memory and wanting more. The station groaned. Deck Six was collapsing. He could feel it. The rusted machinery was dissolving into pure concept, and the concept was unraveling.

"Deck four is residential!" Kaelen cried, tears streaming down his face. "Deck five is hydroponics!"

He grabbed his second anchor. His first love. The boy with the scarred hands in the lower archives. The smell of rain on a lattice station. 

He burned it. 

The station held. The blankness in his mind grew wider. 

*Stop,* his doubt pleaded. *You are killing yourself for a graveyard.*

He grabbed his third anchor. His ordination day. The pride. The purpose. 

He hesitated. If he burned this, he wouldn't just forget an event; he would forget *why* he was a Cleric. He would become a man performing a job without knowing the reason. 

The station shuddered violently. Vane's voice crackled over the internal comms, panicked and thin. "Kaelen! The bay doors are softening! The *Iron Vow* is losing its lock!"

If the ship lost its lock, the Canticle crew would be cast adrift in the eddy. And the three hundred souls on Ostra-Vael would dissolve with them.

*Spend only what you can afford,* the doctrine said.

*I can't afford this,* Kaelen thought. 

But he looked at the wavering walls of the sanctum. He thought of Corin, the maintenance worker, trying to fix a hatch that was forgetting itself. He thought of Vane, exhausted, holding the line on the command deck. 

They were not ghosts. They were alive. And they needed the station to be a station.

Kaelen took a deep breath. 

He did not burn his third anchor. 

Instead, he reached deeper. He reached past his personal memories, past his constructed knots, down to the raw, unrefined core of his own presence. The dangerous technique. The zero-anchor.

He gathered his entire sense of *now*—his fear, his exhaustion, his stubborn, furious refusal to let the void win—and he slammed it into the Liturgy.

"THIS IS OSTRA-VAEL!" he roared, his voice echoing with the force of a physical detonation. "THE STATION HOLDS! THE STATION REMEMBERS ITSELF!"

The zero-anchor ignited. 

Pain arced through Kaelen's nervous system, white-hot and absolute. It felt as though his bones were turning to glass. But the coherence wave that blasted outward from the sanctum was massive. It wasn't a memory of the past; it was a violent assertion of the present. 

The eddy shrieked—a silent, conceptual tearing—and broke against the wave. It shattered, dispersing into harmless mnemonic static that washed over the hull and faded into the Drift.

The sanctum snapped into razor-sharp focus. The lectern was solid wood. The walls were hard metal. 

Kaelen collapsed to the floor, gasping for air, his vision swimming with dark spots. 

## V. The Aftermath

He lay there for a long time. 

Eventually, the door to the sanctum opened. Footsteps echoed on the solid metal floor. 

Kaelen forced his eyes open. A Canticle officer stood over him, wearing the dark, heavy coat of a corridor pilot. Her face was grim, but her eyes held a profound, recognizing respect.

"Cleric," she said softly. She offered a hand.

Kaelen took it. Her grip was strong, pulling him to his feet. His legs trembled, but they held.

"The *Iron Vow* thanks you," the officer said. "The eddy is gone. The station is stable."

"It's stable," Kaelen echoed. His voice sounded hollow to his own ears. 

He checked his mind. The blank spots were there. The missing mother. The missing boy. The aching, empty spaces where love used to be. And the zero-anchor burn had left a lingering numbness, a sense that he was slightly less attached to his own body than he had been an hour ago.

"You spent heavily," the Canticle officer noted, watching his face. "Too heavily for a station this far gone."

"They're still here," Kaelen said, looking past her, out the open door of the sanctum, toward the corridors of Ostra-Vael. "The people. They're still here."

"Yes," the officer said. "They are."

She didn't offer pity. Canticle pilots knew better than anyone the cost of holding back the void. She simply nodded, a sharp, precise dip of the chin, and turned to leave.

"We depart in two hours," she said over her shoulder. "We'll leave a few reliquaries with your station master. Small ones. It won't fix the rust, but it will help with the drift."

"Thank you," Kaelen said.

When she was gone, Kaelen stood alone in the sanctum. 

He walked over to the lectern. He ran his hand over the wood. It was solid. Real. 

He had saved the station. He had saved the ship. He had lost pieces of himself he would never get back. 

He looked at the chronometer on the wall. It was nearly time for the evening recitation. 

Kaelen took a breath. He gripped the edges of the lectern. He did not feel faith. He did not feel conviction. But he felt the solid weight of the wood under his hands, and for now, that was enough.

"This is Ostra-Vael," he began, his voice steady in the quiet room. "The station holds."
