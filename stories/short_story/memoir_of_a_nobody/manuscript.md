# Memoir of a Nobody

## SITUATION

I check my lanyard. The name printed there is **VESS CORMICK**. Maintenance Tech III. Employee ID: varies.

That's me. Probably. I wore it yesterday. Pretty sure that was also me.

My apartment door has no number, which makes coming home an act of faith and muscle memory. Fourth floor, third door from the fire escape, smells like someone's eternally reheating synthetic curry. The neighbor to my left thinks they live next to a storage closet. The neighbor to my right submitted a maintenance request about "persistent hallway drafts." I am the draft.

Morning routine: shower, dress, check lanyard again (still Vess), pocket my wallet and the protein bar I didn't finish last week, and walk into The Beige.

Waystation Commercial Hub 11-C. The Beige. Named, I assume, by a committee that was trying to name something else entirely and gave up halfway through. Every surface is regulation grey-beige. Every light fixture emits the exact minimum lumens required by commercial station ordinance. The air smells like recycled air that's been recycled again for good measure. If you asked me to describe The Beige in one word, I would say "compliance." If you asked me to describe it in two words, I would forget you asked.

I pass through Security Station 3. The guard is reading something on a datapad. He glances up as I approach. Our eyes meet.

He glances down.

I walk through. No badge scan. No "good morning." No moment of recognition that might accumulate into memory-weight and tether me to his consciousness like a normal person. Instead: the faint warmth I've come to associate with dissolution. Somewhere in his brain, a cluster of neurons started to encode my face, my gait, my presence. Then — contact. The memory flowed toward me like water finding a drain. Gone.

He shifts slightly, frowns at his datapad. I've seen this a thousand times. He feels lighter. Doesn't know why.

I continue to Sub-Level 4, Executive Cafeteria. The door requires clearance I don't have. I press my palm to the reader anyway. It flashes red. I wait. The system tries to log the failed attempt, searches for my employee record, finds nothing, experiences what I imagine is the computer equivalent of mild vertigo, and gives up. The door clicks open.

Inside: real coffee. Not the molecular print approximation served in the staff lounge, but imported, shipped cross-void, probably worth more than my monthly housing credit. I pour myself a cup. The attendant is arranging pastries. She looks at me.

Looks past me.

I take a croissant. It's excellent.

I attend the 0900 Infrastructure Planning Meeting. Senior Management only. I sit in the back. Someone starts to ask, "Who—" then stops mid-word, looks confused, looks at their notes. The meeting proceeds. I contribute nothing. My coffee is still excellent. I learn that Sub-Sector 7's coolant filtration is due for replacement in Q3. Useful. I make a note.

By 1100, I've saved three minor crises:

1. Recalibrated a pressure sensor in Bay 12 that was reading 0.4% high — would've triggered false containment alerts by end-week
2. Rerouted foot traffic around a deck panel that's developing microfractures (submitted anonymous maintenance request; will be ignored; I'll fix it myself Tuesday)
3. Stopped a kitchen fire in the commissary by activating the suppression system before anyone noticed it started

No one thanks me. No one saw. This is fine.

This is fine.

I eat lunch in the observation deck. It's empty. It's always empty when I'm here — people wander in, feel vaguely unsettled, wander out. I sit in the same spot and look at the void. The stars here are stable. No tidal churn, no meaning-collapse. Just regular old photons traveling through regular old nothing. Boring.

Perfect.

I think about the first time I came here. Right after the incident. Twenty-three years ago? Twenty-four? I was young. Scared. I'd just been exposed to— what did the medical report call it? "Micro-particulate eddy material, trace neurological integration, prognosis uncertain." The doctor explained it to me three times. Each time, she seemed to be starting fresh. By the third explanation, I understood: she would never remember telling me. No one would.

I could've left. Gone somewhere else. Tried to find a treatment that didn't exist. Instead, I stayed. Found this job. Learned to be invisible.

Why?

I don't remember deciding. But it must've made sense at the time.

The void is pretty today. Unremarkable, but pretty.

I finish my sandwich.

At 1630, the station-wide announcement chimes. I'm in Sub-Corridor 9, replacing a burnt-out lumen strip that wasn't technically burnt out yet but would've been by Thursday. I stop, listen.

"Attention all personnel. Station Administration is pleased to announce a new position opening: **Continuity Maintenance Supervisor**. This role will oversee long-term infrastructure integrity, preventative maintenance scheduling, and system continuity protocols. Applications accepted through standard channels. Deadline: end of cycle."

I stand there, holding my mag-driver, staring at the half-installed lumen strip.

Continuity Maintenance Supervisor.

That's... that's exactly what I do. What I've been doing. Unseen, unlogged, unremembered. I know this station's infrastructure better than anyone. I *am* the continuity. I'm the reason coolant systems don't fail, deck plates don't fracture, kitchen fires don't spread. I'm the ghost in the machine, and the machine runs because of me.

I should apply.

I should absolutely not apply.

I'm going to apply.

It's a joke, obviously. A lark. Just to see what happens. I haven't wanted something — really *wanted* it — in so long that I've forgotten what wanting feels like.

Oh.

There it is.

Warm. Sharp. Uncomfortably present.

I want this job.

I climb down from the ladder, pack my tools, and head to the nearest terminal. The station hums around me. People pass. No one sees.

But I see the job posting, clear as anything.

And I start typing.

## ESCALATION

The application form has 47 fields.

I fill them out carefully:

- **Name:** Vess Cormick
- **Employee ID:** 48291-C (I think? It's what's on my lanyard today)
- **Current Position:** Maintenance Tech III
- **Years of Service:** 23 (I've been here since the beginning; no records will confirm this)
- **Reason for Application:** *I am already doing this job. I would like to do it with a title.*

I submit.

The screen flashes: **ERROR: EMPLOYEE ID NOT FOUND. PLEASE VERIFY AND RESUBMIT.**

Right. Of course.

I try my backup ID: 48291-D. Then 48219-C. Then I go into the system archives and find six other variations, each attached to a single maintenance log, each filed and forgotten.

None work.

I try manual override — there's a checkbox for "ID Lookup Issues." I tick it. Write a note in the comments field: "Records are inconsistent; I am a current employee; please process manually."

Submit.

**ERROR: DUPLICATE SUBMISSION DETECTED. USER NOT REGISTERED.**

I am being rejected by a form for being too persistent and not existing at the same time.

I sit back. The terminal's screen dims to idle. My reflection stares back: tired eyes, regulation jumpsuit, lanyard with a name I have to keep checking.

Fine. In-person, then.

---

Human Resources is on Level 2, behind a frosted glass door labeled **HR - PERSONNEL SERVICES**. I walk in. A woman at the front desk looks up.

"Hi," I say. "I'd like to apply for the Continuity Maintenance Supervisor position, but the system won't accept my application."

She blinks. "Of course. Let me just—" She looks at her screen. Frowns. Looks at me. "Sorry, what was your name?"

"Vess Cormick."

She types. Waits. "I'm not seeing you in the directory. Do you have your employee ID?"

I show her my lanyard.

She types the number. Her frown deepens. "This isn't... hold on." She picks up her handset, dials an extension. "Hi, yeah, I have someone here who— wait, who did you say you were?"

"Vess Cormick."

"Right. Vess Cormick. They're applying for—" She stops. Stares at her screen. "Sorry, I just need to—" She looks at me. Fully looks at me, as if seeing me for the first time. Again.

I feel it happen: the memory forming, flowing toward me, dissolving. Warmth. Gone.

"Can I help you?" she asks.

"I'm trying to apply for a job."

"Of course. Which position?"

"Continuity Maintenance Supervisor."

"Great. Let me just pull up your file. Name?"

"Vess Cormick."

"And your employee ID?"

I show her the lanyard. She types. Frowns. Looks up. "Sorry, this number isn't— wait, have we done this already?"

"Yes."

"Oh." She looks embarrassed. Looks at her screen. Looks at me. The memory flows. Dissolves. Her expression clears. "Can I help you?"

I leave.

The hallway is very beige. The lights are very bright. I stand there for a moment, hands in pockets, looking at the frosted glass door.

It stung more than I expected.

Over the next week, I try everything.

I write a cover letter. Print it on physical paper. Leave it on the HR director's desk with a note: *"Please consider. - Vess Cormick, Maintenance Tech III."*

Three days later, I check the HR office. The letter has been filed under **Anonymous Suggestions**. There's a handwritten note stapled to it: *"Good infrastructure insights. Author unknown. Follow up if they identify themselves."*

I record a video application. Sit in my apartment, talk to the camera for five minutes. Explain my qualifications, my experience, my vision for the role. I'm articulate. Calm. I almost convince myself.

I submit the file.

The next day, I check the application queue. My video is there, flagged: **REVIEW REQUIRED - VISUAL CORRUPTION.** I play it back.

The frame shows my empty apartment. My chair is visible. My desk. The window behind me. But where I should be: nothing. Just a faint shimmer, like heat distortion. My voice is there, clear and steady, narrating to an empty room.

I delete the file.

I try something desperate: I approach Kell, a fellow maintenance tech. We've worked near each other for years. I've "introduced" myself to him 300, maybe 400 times. He's always polite. Always surprised.

"Kell," I say. "I need a favor."

He looks up from his terminal. "Sure, what's up?"

"I'm applying for the Continuity Maintenance Supervisor job. I need someone to vouch for me. Just tell HR I'm a good worker. That I exist."

He laughs. "Yeah, of course. You've been here forever, right?"

"Right."

"What's your name again?"

"Vess."

"Vess. Got it. I'll tell them." He makes a note on his datapad. "When's the application deadline?"

"End of cycle. Two days."

"I'll go today." He grins. "Good luck, man."

"Thanks."

I watch him walk toward the lift. He's still holding the datapad. Still has my name on it.

He looks at me. "Sorry, do I know you?"

"I'm Vess. You just vouched for me."

"I— what? No, I was just in there asking about... uh..." He checks his datapad. The note is gone. He frowns. "Weird. I thought I had something to do. You need something?"

"No," I say. "Thanks anyway."

I walk away before he can ask again.

That night, I sit in my apartment. The lights are low. The station hums its endless mechanical lullaby.

I take out my journal — a small, battered notebook I've kept for years. I open it to a blank page.

Write: *I don't need recognition. I just—*

I stop.

Just what?

I stare at the sentence. The pen hovers.

I can't finish it.

Because the truth is: I do need it. I've needed it for twenty-three years. I've told myself I made peace with invisibility, that I found freedom in it, that I'm fine.

But I'm not fine.

I'm so tired of being fine.

When was the last time someone *chose* to remember me? Not forgot-and-was-reminded. Not saw-and-immediately-forgot. Actually held me in their mind. Thought about me when I wasn't there. Wondered how I was doing.

Never.

The answer is never.

The contentment was a lie. The peace was dissociation. I've been sitting in the void, telling myself the stars are pretty, because the alternative was admitting I'm disappearing.

And I'm so, so lonely.

I close the journal.

The apartment is quiet.

I check my lanyard.

**VESS CORMICK.**

Still me.

For now.

## CHOICE

I can't sleep.

At 0300, I go to the station archives. Sub-Level 8, Records Storage. No one's here. No one's ever here. The door unlocks for me because the system can't decide if I'm authorized or just a data error, and errors get rounded down to "probably fine."

I search for my own name.

The first hit: a personnel file. Opened four years ago. Contains one entry: a shift log from a day I don't remember. Signed "V. Cormick."

I search again.

Another file. This one six years old. One entry: a safety training completion certificate.

Again.

Nineteen files total. Scattered across two decades. Each with one or two entries, like breadcrumbs I've been leaving without realizing. Evidence that I was here. Proof I existed, at least in fragments.

I open the oldest one.

It's a medical report. Twenty-three years old. The file is partially corrupted — sections are missing, redacted, or degraded. But I can read enough:

> **Incident Date:** [CORRUPTED]
> **Exposure Type:** Micro-particulate eddy material, trace neurological integration

I keep reading.

Tucked into the file, handwritten on a scrap of paper:

> *I can't do this anymore. I'm disappearing. Not from the world—from myself. I don't remember who I was before this. I don't remember deciding to stay. I keep finding notes I don't remember writing. I'm leaving. I have to leave. By the time you read this, I won't remember writing it.*

The handwriting is mine.

The date: three years ago.

I don't remember writing this. I don't remember deciding to leave.

I stayed.

Why?

I go back to my apartment. The sun is starting to rise — or whatever The Beige's day-cycle simulation calls sunrise. I sit at my desk. Open my journal.

Flip backward.

Page after page. Entries I wrote. I recognize my handwriting. But it *changes*. Not dramatically — just small shifts. The slant of letters. The pressure of the pen. Some entries are detailed, others vague. Some contradict each other.

One page, dated eight months ago:

Both in my handwriting.

I can't.

The eddy isn't just erasing me from others. It's erasing me from *myself*.

## COST

**Final Entry**

My name is Vess Cormick, and I don't know if that's the name I was born with.

I've been living on Waystation Commercial Hub 11-C for twenty-three years. I work maintenance. I'm good at it. I keep this station running, and no one knows I'm doing it, and that's fine.

It has to be fine.

Twenty-three years ago, I was exposed to micro-eddy material during a reliquary transport accident. It didn't erase me. It nested. I became a living eddy — localized, low-intensity, targeted. I only erase memories of myself.

At first, it was terrifying. Then inconvenient. Then liberating. Then fine.

Now: I don't know what it is. The eddy is turning inward. I'm forgetting myself. I've written this journal for years, but the handwriting changes. The stories shift. I can't verify my own past. I have no anchors. No one remembers me long enough to confirm what's true.

I don't know who I was before the incident.

I don't know if the memories I have are real or just... narrative drift.

But I know this:

I applied for a job I'll never get. I tried to be seen. I failed. And in failing, I realized I've been lying to myself. I wanted to matter. I wanted to be held in someone's mind. I wanted to be chosen, remembered, *kept*.

I didn't get that.

But I'm here. Right now. Sitting at my desk, writing this, and I'm present. Fully, painfully present.

That's something.

Maybe it's enough.

I don't know what happens next. The eddy is growing. I can feel it — small gaps in my thoughts, moments where I lose the thread and have to check my lanyard to remember my name. Eventually, I'll forget this too. Forget writing this. Forget wanting the job. Forget being lonely.

Maybe I'll forget I was ever anyone at all.

But right now, I'm Vess Cormick. I fix things. I walk through The Beige and no one sees me, but I see them. I see the vendor who smiles even when she's tired. The kid who runs through the corridors laughing. The couple who argue about nothing important and everything important.

I see the station. I keep it running.

I was here.

If you're reading this — whoever you are, whenever you find it — know that I was here. I existed. I mattered, at least to myself, at least for now.

That's my testimony.

That's all I have.

It's enough.

Or it has to be.

Or it's not, but it's what there is.

I'm going to close this journal and leave it on my desk. I'm going to walk into the station. I'm going to do my job. I'm going to be present.

And when the eddy finally takes me — when I forget who I am entirely, when there's nothing left but warmth and dissolution — I hope I still see the stars.

I hope I still think they're pretty.

— Vess
