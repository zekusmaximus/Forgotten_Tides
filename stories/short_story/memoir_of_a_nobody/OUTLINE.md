# Memoir of a Nobody — Story Outline

**Format:** Short Story (~5,000 words)
**POV:** Vess (first person recommended)
**Tone:** Very dark comedy
**Canon Status:** Approved expansion (eddy internalization unique to Vess)

---

## Premise

A station maintenance worker who was accidentally exposed to micro-eddy material twenty-three years ago has become a living mnemonic siphon — everyone forgets them immediately. They've made peace with invisibility, even found freedom in it. When they decide to pursue a promotion on a whim, the attempt to become memorable forces them to confront how much of themselves they've already lost — and how little time remains before they forget who they were entirely.

---

## Protagonist: Vess

### The Condition: Mnemonic Siphon

Vess has a neurological irregularity — their brain *pulls in* nearby memory-weight but cannot hold it. Memories of Vess flow toward them and then dissipate. Annihilated on contact.

**Physics:**
- Normal people: memories of them accumulate in observers, creating gravitational/mnemonic imprint
- Vess: memories of them are *attracted* to Vess, then erased upon reaching them
- Net effect: observers feel the memory forming, then... nothing. Brief confusion. Sense something was there.

**Observable Symptoms in Others:**
- Mid-sentence forgetting: "I was just talking to— wait, who?"
- Micro-disorientation: a slight wobble, like gravity flickered
- The "tip of tongue" sensation, permanent
- Some people feel *lighter* after talking to Vess — they've lost a tiny bit of memory-weight

**What Vess Experiences:**
- A faint warmth when someone's memory of them dissolves
- Almost feels those memories — fragments, impressions — before they're gone
- Sometimes catches glimpses: *oh, she was about to remember my birthday*
- It's intimate and horrible

### Origin: The Incident

Twenty-three years ago, Vess was a young cargo handler on a reliquary transport. Routine job. Boring. Forgettable — even then.

A secured reliquary canister cracked during transit. Not a dramatic rupture — a hairline fracture. Micro-eddy material seeped out. Everyone else evacuated. Vess didn't hear the alarm. (Nobody thought to check if anyone was still in the hold.)

They breathed it in.

The eddy material didn't erase Vess — it *nested*. Bonded with their neural architecture. A fragment of primordial forgetting now lives inside them, continuously running at low intensity.

They became a **living, localized eddy** — but one that only affects memories *of themselves*.

**Why This Is Unique (Canon-Safe):**
- Eddy exposure is rare; direct micro-exposure almost unheard of
- Internalization is unprecedented — normally eddies erase you
- Vess survived because the fragment was too small to annihilate, large enough to integrate
- Not replicable; freak accident, not template
- Doesn't change eddy rules — still erases meaning-first, just locally

**The Dark Note:** The fragment may be growing. Slowly. Vess's own memories are starting to erode — the eddy is turning inward.

### Character Details

- Keeps meticulous personal records because official ones don't stick
- Has a lanyard with their own name on it — checks it sometimes
- Apartment door has no number; mail never arrives
- Has "introduced" themselves to the same coworker 300+ times
- Not bitter — adapted with dry, patient humor
- Finds their condition occasionally useful
- Genuine existential unease buried under pragmatic coping
- Talks to themselves because nobody else will remember the conversation

## Setting: Waystation Commercial Hub 11-C ("The Beige")

A thoroughly unremarkable commercial station in a stable memory region. Notable only for being utterly unnoteworthy. Aggressively beige. Bureaucratically forgettable.

## SITUATION (~1,000 words)

### Beat 1: The Invisible Routine
- Vess's morning. Checks lanyard for own name. Apartment door has no number.
- Walks through station. Demonstrates invisibility: passes security, takes food from executive cafeteria, sits in on a meeting they weren't invited to.
- Tone: wry, adapted, almost cheerful. They've mastered this.
- *Negative space: Vess's internal monologue is confident, but small details are wrong — they describe a "usual" routine but some elements contradict each other. They don't notice.*

### Beat 2: The Contentment Lie
- Vess reflects on the benefits: freedom, access, no obligations.
- Internally narrates: "I stopped needing recognition years ago."
- Performs an act of invisible kindness — fixes something critical, prevents a minor disaster. No one notices or thanks them. They tell themselves this is fine.
- *Negative space: They've done this exact good deed before. They don't remember. The reader might notice repetition in phrasing — Vess is looping.*

### Beat 3: The Inciting Moment
- Station announces new position: **Continuity Maintenance Supervisor**. Perfect job for invisible infrastructure work.
- Vess decides to apply. Tells themselves it's a joke, a lark. Just to see what happens.
- *Negative space: Why do they want this? They don't examine the want. It's the first authentic desire they've felt in... they can't remember how long.*

---

## ESCALATION (~1,500 words)

### Beat 4: The Application
- Vess submits application. System rejects: "Employee ID not found."
- Resubmits with manual override. Rejected: "Duplicate submission from unregistered user."
- Goes to HR in person. HR representative forgets them mid-sentence, three times. Vess finishes the conversation with an empty chair.
- Comedy peaks here — absurdist bureaucratic nightmare.
- *Cost: First crack in contentment. Vess expected this, but it stings more than anticipated.*

### Beat 5: Escalating Tactics
- Vess tries increasingly creative approaches:
  - Leaves written notes. Notes get filed under "Anonymous Suggestions."
  - Records video application. Playback shows empty room, static voice.
  - Tries to get a coworker to vouch for them. Coworker agrees enthusiastically, walks into meeting, forgets why they're there.
- Each failure lands harder. Vess's narration shifts: less wry, more brittle.
- *Negative space: Vess keeps checking their lanyard. More frequently now. Do they trust it?*

### Beat 6: The Confrontation with Want
- Vess sits alone in an empty conference room after another failure.
- Tries to write in journal: "I don't need recognition, I just—"
- Cannot finish the sentence. Stares at the page.
- Realizes: they cannot remember the last time someone *chose* to remember them. Not forgot and was reminded — actually held them in mind.
- The contentment was a shell. Beneath it: hunger. Old and starving.
- *Cost: The lie breaks. Vess wanted this all along. The "peace" was dissociation.*

---

## CHOICE (~1,000 words)

### Beat 7: The Discovery
- Vess, searching for any record of themselves, finds fragments:
  - 19 separate personnel files, each with one entry
  - A medical report they don't remember (the eddy diagnosis, partial)
  - A resignation letter in their own handwriting, dated three years ago: *"I can't do this anymore. I'm disappearing. By the time you read this, I won't remember writing it."*
- They don't remember writing it. They stayed. Why?
- *Negative space revealed: Vess has been losing memories of themselves. The eddy is turning inward. They've already forgotten previous crises, previous attempts to leave, previous versions of this exact realization.*

### Beat 8: The Journal
- Vess flips through their journal. The handwriting changes — not dramatically, but enough. Some entries contradict others. Facts shift.
- They realize: they've been telling themselves a story about who they are, and the story keeps drifting.
- The childhood memories they cherish? They can't verify them. No one remembers them as a child. Do they?
- *Cost: Identity itself is in question. Vess may not be who they think they are. May never have been.*

### Beat 9: The Choice Point
- Vess could:
  - Fight harder. Do something catastrophic, force the universe to notice.
  - Give up. Disappear quietly.
  - Or: accept. Write down what's true *now*. Be present while they still can.
- They choose acceptance. Not surrender — presence.

---

## COST (~800 words)

### Beat 10: The Final Entry
- Vess sits in their apartment. It's late. The station hums.
- They write a final journal entry — not for themselves (they won't remember it), but for whoever finds it after.
- The entry is clear, precise, unexpectedly warm:
  - Who they are (as best they know)
  - What happened to them
  - What it felt like to be forgotten
  - What it felt like to forget
- They don't ask to be remembered. They simply... testify. "I was here."
- *Cost: This is goodbye. Vess knows the next version of themselves may not know they wrote this. They're writing a message to their own ghost.*

### Beat 11: The Walk
- Vess leaves the journal on their desk. Walks into the station.
- Morning shift. The Beige wakes up. People pass. No one sees them.
- But Vess sees *them*. Notices a vendor's tired smile. A child's laughter. A couple arguing about nothing.
- Vess is present. Fully. Perhaps for the first time in years.
- They feel, briefly, the warmth of the eddy — memories of them dissolving nearby — and instead of grief, something like peace. Real, this time.

---

## CHANGE (~700 words)

### Beat 12: Coda — Years Later
- A station archivist finds a journal in a sealed room during renovation.
- No name on the door. No records of occupancy.
- The journal is beautifully written. Devastating. Funny. True.
- The archivist reads it in one sitting.
- Published as *Memoir of a Nobody*. Attributed to "Anonymous." Bestseller.
- Critics call it "a profound meditation on presence." "The author's identity remains unknown."
- Final line: *"Whoever they were, they were here. And that was enough."*
- Or was it?

## Thematic Core

**What Vess wants:** To matter. Just once. Just to someone.

**What Vess fears:** That they never did. That every memory anyone ever had of them is already gone. That they're a ghost who doesn't know they died.

**The joke:** Everything.
**The tragedy:** Also everything.
